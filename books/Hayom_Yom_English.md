# Hayom Yom

**15 Tishrei**

The Baal Shem Tov taught that the love of Hashem, the love of Torah, and the love of a fellow Jew are one. This is because the three are bound together: Hashem is one, His Torah is one, and the Jewish people are one. 

Through these three loves, one achieves the highest levels of dveikus. Even a person who is not yet on a level to attain dveikus through profound intellectual meditation can achieve it through these three loves, which are accessible to everyone.

## Kislev

The month of Kislev is the month of light. The name Kislev is related to the word *kesel* (confidence/trust), as it is written, "And my heart shall trust (*eksal*) in Him." This is the time of the revelation of the light of the Torah, specifically the light of Chassidus, which is the "lamp of mitzvah and light of Torah."

The month of Kislev is the time of the *geulah* of the Alter Rebbe. This *geulah* is not merely a personal liberation, but a liberation for all of *anash*, for the entire path of Chassidus, and for the *avodah* of spreading the wellsprings of the Baal Shem Tov outward. It is a time for *hisorerus* in *hiskashrus* to the Rebbes, and for a renewed commitment to the *avodah* of *hafatzas hamaayanos*.

## 19 Kislev

The Shaliach Tzibbur does not wrap himself in a tallis for Mincha or Maariv, neither on Shabbos and Yom Tov nor on Rosh Hashanah.

The Hillula of the Maggid of Mezeritch occurred on Tuesday, Parshas Vayeshev, 5533; his resting place is in Anapoli.

Admur HaZaken was liberated from his first imprisonment on 19 Kislev, Tuesday, Parshas Vayeshev, 5599, toward evening.

From a letter of Rabbeinu HaZaken: "However, this must be made known: that the day Hashem has made for us, the day of 19 Kislev—a Tuesday, upon which 'it was good' was doubled—is the day of the great Hillula of our holy Rebbe, may his soul rest in Eden. As I was reading in the Sefer Tehillim the verse, 'He has redeemed my soul in peace' (Tehillim 55:19), before I began the following verse, I emerged in peace from the 'peace of Hashem'."

It is a day for farbrengens and for making good resolutions regarding the setting of fixed times for the study of Nigleh and Chassidus in public, and for strengthening the ways of Chassidim in brotherly love.

It is customary to organize a *chalukas haShas* (a division of the entire Talmud for communal study), according to the order explained in Iggeres HaKodesh, in the maamar beginning *Hocheiach Tochiach*. In Lubavitch, from the year 5663 onwards, the *chalukas haShas* was organized on 24 Teves—the Hillula of Rabbeinu HaZaken—due to the lack of time on 19 Kislev.

## 20 Kislev

The printing of the Sefer Shel Beinonim — Tanya — was completed for the first time on Tuesday, 20 Kislev 5557, in Slavita. It included:
Part One — Sefer Shel Beinonim; Part Two — Chinuch Katan and Shaar HaYichud VeHaEmunah.

Iggeres HaTeshuvah — the first edition — was printed for the first time in Zolkiev in 5559; the second edition was printed for the first time in Shklov in 5566.

Iggeres HaKodesh was printed for the first time in Shklov in 5574. A corrected edition of all four parts was published by Romm in Vilna in 5660, and it has since been reprinted many times.

## 21 Kislev

One should learn Mishnayos by heart, each person according to their own capacity, and walk in the street reciting them. Through this, one will merit to receive the face of Moshiach.

## 22 Kislev

Among the takanos of the Rebbe: To recite Tehillim every morning after davening—including on Shabbos, Yom Tov, Rosh Hashanah, and Yom Kippur—according to the monthly division. After reciting it with a minyan, recite Kaddish Yasom.

In a month of twenty-nine days, on the twenty-ninth day, recite the portion for both the twenty-ninth and the thirtieth.

## 23 Kislev

From the sichos of my grandfather, the Rebbe [Rashab] — Elul 5635: The three days preceding Shabbos are a preparation for Shabbos. Regarding Shabbos, the Zohar states: "From it, all the days are blessed." "All the days" refers to the entire six days of the week, for which Hashem gave a general blessing: "Hashem your G-d will bless you in all that you do." This blessing of Shabbos extends both before it and after it. The preparation for Shabbos begins on Wednesday, and the signal for this is the "small" *Lechu Neranena*, which consists of three pesukim.

## 24 Kislev, Erev Chanukah

(Continued). An explanation of the three verses of *Lechu Neranena* according to the average level of every person: On Wednesday, when one begins to think about where one will obtain what is needed for Shabbos—both in a simple sense, and with what one will "make" Shabbos—every Jew is indeed [connected to] Shabbos, but one person becomes depressed. The advice is *Lechu Neranena*—trust. When Thursday comes and it gets closer to Shabbos, and it is still not there, at that point one does not "sing"; one understands that one must do something. So, when one sits on Thursday night and learns Chassidus, then on Friday one hears *Ki Kel Gadol... U'Melech Gadol...* [For Hashem is a great G-d... and a great King...], and with this, a Jew "makes" Shabbos.

At Mincha, we do not say Tachanun.

## 25 Kislev, First Day of Chanukah

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, would light the Chanukah candles between Mincha and Maariv. He was not particular about whether the doorway was positioned from south to north or from east to west. He would recite "HaNeiros Hallalu" after lighting all the candles. He would then sit near the candles for half an hour—except on Erev Shabbos, when he would not remain for half an hour—and he was particular that the candles should burn for at least fifty minutes.

Mincha, Chanukah candle, Shabbos candle.

## 26 Kislev, 2nd Day of Chanukah

We bless the new month of Teves. The entire Tehillim is recited at daybreak.
It is a day for a farbrengen. We begin saying "Tal U'Matar." We light the Chanukah candles after Havdalah and before saying "V'yiten Lecha." In the beis medrash, the candles are lit before Havdalah.

On the Shabbos Mevarchim of the new month, Anash should gather in the beis medrash early in the morning to recite the entire Tehillim. After the recitation, they should study a maamar of Chassidus for about an hour that is accessible to everyone, and then proceed to davening. The time for the farbrengen should be set according to what is appropriate for the local conditions and the place of residence, for success in both material and spiritual matters.

After reciting the entire Tehillim on Shabbos Mevarchim, Kaddish Yatom is recited. If there is a chiyuv—such as a Yahrtzeit or an avel—Kaddish Yatom is recited after each individual book of Tehillim.

## 27 Kislev, Third Day of Chanukah

During the second imprisonment of the Alter Rebbe—in the year 5561 (1800)—he was not held in the same harsh confinement as the first time. However, the denunciation was more severe, as the primary accusation was against the teachings of Chassidus, and the opposition was immense. He was held in the "Tayne Sovet." The day he was released to freedom was the third candle of Chanukah.

## 28 Kislev, 4th Day of Chanukah

It was the custom of the Tzemach Tzedek that on one of the nights of Chanukah, they would hold a type of farbrengen with the members of the household, which the daughters-in-law would also attend; this was called a "latkes evening." This was also the custom of the Alter Rebbe and the Mitteler Rebbe. Among the stories that the Rebbes would recount at this meal were stories that they would discuss every Chanukah, even though they had already spoken about them the previous year.

My father, the Rebbe, would distribute Chanukah gelt on the night of the fourth or fifth candle.

## 29 Kislev, 5th Day of Chanukah

The sins of the Jews (during the time of the Greeks) were: associating with the Greeks, studying their culture, desecrating Shabbos and Yom Tov, eating non-kosher food, and failing to observe the laws of Jewish purity. The resulting punishment was the spiritual destruction of the Beis Hamikdash, slaughter, and enslavement in exile. Through teshuvah and mesiras nefesh, the great Divine miraculous salvation—the miracle of Chanukah—was brought about.

## Teves

The month of Teves is the month of the "eye." The sense of sight corresponds to the month of Teves.

The *avodah* of the month of Teves is to refine the sense of sight—to see only the good in others and to avoid looking at anything that is not *tznius*.

The *mazal* of the month of Teves is *Gedi* (Capricorn). This alludes to the *avodah* of *iskafya*—subduing the *yetzer hara*—which is the path to *is'hapcha*—transforming the *yetzer hara* into good.

## 1 Teves, Rosh Chodesh, 6th Day of Chanukah

It is the custom that an individual recites the blessing before and after Hallel, even on days when the full Hallel is not recited. The text recited is: "Yehalelucha Hashem Elokeinu kol maasecha," omitting the word "al." All tefillin are put on before Mussaf, but the shiurim are studied after the conclusion of the entire davening.

## 2 Teves, 7th Day of Chanukah

The war of the Greeks was "to make them forget Your Torah and force them to stray from the statutes of Your will." As the Midrash (Bereishis Rabbah 2:4) states, they wrote [on the horn of an ox] that you have no portion in the G-d of Israel. The entire war was against Elokus. One could study Torah, one could fulfill the mitzvos of *mishpatim* and *eidus*, provided one did not mention that it is the Torah of Hashem and that the mitzvos are the statutes of His will, may He be blessed; not to mention in Torah and mitzvos the concept of G-dliness.

## 3 Teves, 8th Day of Chanukah

In *Torah Or*, in the *maamar* beginning "Tanu Rabbanan: The Mitzvah of the Chanukah Lamp," the phrase "ki ein kedai kol hadinim" should read "ki kedai kol hadinim."

In the same *maamar*, in the explanation of the discourse beginning "Rani V'Simchi," the section beginning "ve'atah navo" which states "shehu penimius Atik Yomin" should read "shehu penimius Ein Sof."

## 4 Teves

The Nesi'im of Chabad would customarily perform *shnayim mikra v'echad targum* for one or two *parshiyos* on Thursday night, *ohr l'yom shishi*. On Friday afternoon, after *chatzos*, they would begin again from the start and complete the entire *sedra* with the *haftorah*. On Shabbos morning, before *davening*, they would perform *shnayim mikra* again, starting from *shvi'i*. When two *haftaros* were involved, they would recite the *haftorah* of the *sedra* on Friday, and the other *haftorah* (such as for *Rosh Chodesh*, *Machar Chodesh*, etc.) on Shabbos.

Good dreams regarding matters of Torah, which are revealed in a dream, generally come as a result of great diligence in Torah study during the day. When one occupies himself with Torah with great diligence, or engages in *avodah shebalev* with immense exertion, then at night, when his *neshamah* ascends on high to draw life from the supernal sources—as explained in the *Zohar*—he is shown *chiddushei Torah*, whether in the *nigleh* of Torah or in the *pnimiyus* of Torah, each person according to the diligence of his *avodah* during the day.

## 5 Teves

When Moshiach comes, the superiority of *hoda'ah* (acknowledgment) and *temimus* (simple sincerity) will be revealed—the fact that everyone believes with *emunah tehorah* (pure faith) in Hashem, blessed be He, and in His Torah and *mitzvos*. *Talmud*—intellectual understanding—even the greatest understanding, has a limit. But *hoda'ah*—faith—is a feeling without limit. Melech HaMoshiach will make it understood that *temimus*—heartfelt, earnest *avodah*—is a great achievement.

## 6 Teves

It is written, "Know the G-d of your father and serve Him with a whole heart." For every matter of knowledge and comprehension, even regarding the most profound subjects, must be brought about through avodah—that is, the active process of clarifying and refining the middos and achieving an internal hiskashrus, which in the language of Chassidus is called avodah.

## 7 Teves

The measure for being spared *chibut hakever* (the "pounding of the grave") is to recite the letters of the Torah—specifically *Tehillim*—for one-sixth of every twenty-four-hour period. To merit the refinement of the *neshamah* required to be spared *kaf hakela* (the "sling of the soul"), one must review—as much as one is able throughout the entire day—*Mishnayos*, *Tanya*, and *Tehillim* by heart.

## 8 Teves

The Tzemach Tzedek instructed all the melamdim who taught his young grandsons that, in addition to their regular studies, they must teach them the translation (the simple meaning) of the order of davening. Once a month, they would come before the Tzemach Tzedek to be tested on this.

## 9 Teves

It is our custom in the *Aleinu* prayer to say: "For they bow to vanity and emptiness" — and we say this as well in the *Musaf* prayer of *Rosh Hashanah* — and we are careful not to say "and they pray," etc. The spitting occurs after saying these words. The reason for the spitting is that speech generates saliva, and we do not wish to derive any benefit from this saliva.

## 10 Teves

A fast day, Selichos, Avinu Malkeinu.

The Tzemach Tzedek’s response during yechidus: It is written, "Let the wicked forsake his way, and the man of iniquity (aven) his thoughts..." *Aven* is related to the word *on* (with a cholam), which means strength and might. Just as "the wicked forsake his way" is a necessity—for without teshuvah it is impossible to approach the holy—so too must the "man of iniquity," meaning the person who is strong in his own opinion, forsake his thoughts. He must not say, "I say so," or "I hold this way," for every "I" and every sense of self is the source of evil and the cause of division between hearts.

## 11 Teves

A person’s life depends on the atmosphere in which they find themselves. One cannot live without air, and the quality of one’s life is determined by the atmosphere in which one lives. When one lives in an atmosphere of Torah and mitzvos, life is healthy. When one lives in an atmosphere of *kefirah*, life is sickly, and one is constantly in danger of contracting infectious diseases. The primary, universal remedy is to purify the air. The task of *taharas ha-avir* (purifying the atmosphere) rests upon those who are *yodei sefer* and *yodei Torah*. *Taharas ha-avir* is achieved through the *osiyos* of Torah. When one is standing in a store, walking in the street, or riding the subway, and one recites the *osiyos* of Torah, one purifies the atmosphere. Every one of the *yodei sefer* and *yodei Torah* must have something memorized by heart—Chumash, Tehillim, Mishnayos, Tanya—so that at any time and in any place, they can think and recite the holy *osiyos* of Torah.

## 12 Teves

In the year 5648 (1887), my father, the Rebbe, was chosen as a gabbai of the Chevra Kadisha. As was the custom, they escorted him to the shul on Simchas Torah, accompanied by a large crowd. He then delivered the maamar, "Hashem does not deal harshly with His creatures." He concluded his words by saying: Even the people of great intellect who are present now must set aside their own reasoning and not follow their own logic and understanding, for they can be led astray by their own intellect, etc., until, chas v'shalom, the end will be bitter, etc. This is the essential point in this time, in the footsteps of Moshiach: not to follow one's own intellect and logic, but rather to fulfill the Torah and mitzvos with sincerity and simple faith in the G-d of Yisrael.

## 13 Teves

When you set up a lamp, objects gather in the light, because light attracts — *lichtigkeit zieht tzu*.

## 14 Teves

The Shpoler Zeide was a man of intense *hispaalus*, to a degree far exceeding that of his colleagues, the students of the Maggid. During his visit to Liadi to see the Alter Rebbe—in the year 5569 or 5570—he related that when he was three years old, he saw the Baal Shem Tov, "and he placed his holy hand upon my heart, and from that moment on, I have felt warmth."

A movement of a *tzaddik*, and certainly seeing him or hearing his voice, should be internalized so that it is never forgotten.

## 15 Teves

In Torah Or, the maamar beginning *Osri*, in the section starting *VeHinei Zehu*, it should read: "And the elevation of the sparks that fell, even the animals will be elevated, just as it was in the beginning."

Listen and hear, O Yisrael: This time is the appointed time for the Geulah through Moshiach Tzidkeinu, and the tribulations coming upon us are the birth pangs of Moshiach. Yisrael will only be redeemed through teshuvah. Do not believe the false prophets who promise you salvation and comfort after the war ends. Remember the word of Hashem (Yirmiyahu 17:5): "Cursed is the man who trusts in man and makes flesh his arm, and whose heart turns away from Hashem." Return, O Yisrael, unto Hashem your G-d, and prepare yourself and your household to greet Moshiach Tzidkeinu, who is coming very soon.

## 16 Teves

The Tzemach Tzedek said to R' Hendel during yechidus: The Zohar elevates the nefesh, the Midrash awakens the heart, and Tehillim with tears washes out the vessel.

## 17 Teves

Regarding the custom not to study on Nittel, I heard from my father, the Rebbe, that the reason is so as not to add vitality.

Once, my father, the Rebbe, said: "As for those *masmidim* for whom the eight hours [of sleep] are vital to their very being, I do not hold by [their custom of not studying]."

And this applies only until midnight.

## 18 Teves

The person called to the Torah also recites *Chazak Chazak*.
When reciting *Vayehi Noam* on Motzaei Shabbos, the verse *Orech Yamim* etc., is repeated, but this is not done during Shacharis.
When the Tzemach Tzedek was a child and was learning the verse, "And Yaakov lived in the land of Egypt seventeen years," his teacher translated it for him—based on the commentary of the Baal HaTurim—as, "Our father Yaakov lived his seventeen best years in Egypt." When he came home from *cheder*, he asked his grandfather, the Alter Rebbe: "How is it possible that Yaakov Avinu, the choicest of the Patriarchs, should have the choicest years of his life be the seventeen years he lived in the land of Egypt, the 'nakedness of the land'?"
The Alter Rebbe answered him: "It is written, 'And he sent Yehuda before him to Yosef, to instruct before him to Goshen,' and it is stated in the Midrash—cited by Rashi—that Rabbi Nechemiah said: 'To establish a house of study for him, so that Torah would be there and the tribes would be occupied with Torah.' *Lehoros lefanav Goshnah*—when one learns Torah, one becomes closer to the Ribono shel Olam, and thus, even in Egypt, there was *vayehi*—he truly lived."

## 19 Teves

Among Chabad Chassidim, it is a tradition passed down from generation to generation—an enactment of the Alter Rebbe—that every day one should study the portion of Chumash with Rashi for the weekly parsha. The Rebbes, the Nesi'im of Chabad, also conducted themselves in this manner.

## 20 Teves

The Mitteler Rebbe once answered someone during yechidus: When one person speaks with another about matters of avodah and they learn together, it is a case of two nefesh Elokis against one nefesh hativ'is.

## 21 Teves

(The Rebbe encountered someone carrying buckets full of water and said:) When you encounter water, the Baal Shem Tov says: when you encounter water, you should say that this is a sign of blessing.

## 22 Teves

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, declared at one of the farbrengens: Just as putting on tefillin every day is a mitzvah d’oraisa for every Jew—without distinction between a great Torah scholar and a simple person—so too is it an absolute obligation for every Jew to spend half an hour each day thinking about the chinuch of their children, and to do everything within their power, and even beyond their power, to ensure that their children follow the path in which they are being guided.

## 23 Teves

In *Torah Or*, the *maamar* beginning "Mi Sam Peh," in the section starting "And the analogy can be understood: 'For there is a level of *ma'alah*,'" it should read "And so there is a *ma'alah*."

One cannot be saved by sighs alone. A sigh is merely the key to the lock—to open the heart and open the eyes so that one does not sit idly by, but rather organizes *avodah* and action, each person doing whatever they are able to accomplish and perform in the *ta'amulah* to strengthen Torah, disseminate Torah, and observe *mitzvos*—one through his writing, another through his speech, and another through his financial support.

## 24 Teves

The passing of the Alter Rebbe took place in the village of Piena on Motzaei Shabbos Kodesh, Parshas Shemos, 5573. He is interred in the city of Haditch.

My grandfather [the Tzemach Tzedek] asked the Mitteler Rebbe: "What did the Zayde [the Alter Rebbe] intend with the *darkei hachassidus* (ways of Chassidus), and what did he intend with Chassidus itself?"

The Mitteler Rebbe replied: "*Darkei hachassidus* means that all Chassidim should be like one family according to the Torah, in love. Chassidus is *chaius* (vitality). It means to bring in *chaius* and illuminate everything—even that which is 'not good'—so that one should know where one's own evil lies, in order to rectify it."

## 25 Teves

We bless the new month of Shevat, recite the entire Tehillim at daybreak, and hold a farbrengen.

From the sichos of my father, the Rebbe: The Exodus from Egypt (Yetzias Mitzrayim) is the departure from "Mitzrayim" (Egypt) and its limitations, while Chassidus is about departing from the "Mitzrayim" and limitations of the world.

There is a distinction: Yetzias Mitzrayim was a matter of breaking and leaving; therefore, they actually departed from Egypt. The "Exodus" of Chassidus is one of *birur* (refinement) and *tikkun* (rectification)—a departure from the "Mitzrayim" and limitations of the world, yet while remaining within the world. That is to say: while being in the world, one must be in a state of departure from the "Mitzrayim" and limitations of the world. To remove the "Mitzrayim" and limitation, and to perceive the truth—that the world itself is truly good, for it is the will of the Ribono shel Olam—is achieved through the avodah of Chassidus.

## 26 Teves

In the blessing of *V’lamalshinim*, one should pause slightly between the word *v’temager* and the word *v’tachni’a*. This corresponds to the intent that *v’ta’aker, v’tishbor, v’temager* are directed against the three *kelipos* that must be completely removed, whereas *v’tachni’a* is directed against *kelipas nogah*, which requires *hachna’ah* (subjugation) and contains an element of *birur* (refinement).

## 27 Teves

The Alter Rebbe said: Jewish physicality is spirituality. Hashem gives us physicality so that we may transform it into spirituality. Sometimes, when for a moment it is not so, we must offer Hashem even a poor man’s offering, and He will grant a full measure.

## 28 Teves

In the printed edition of *Sefer HaChakirah* (*Derech HaEmunah*) by the Tzemach Tzedek, many glosses are missing. The Tzemach Tzedek authored it in connection with his travels to Petersburg, where he had to provide answers to several questions regarding these matters.

## 29 Teves

We are the workers of the day. The day is light, and our avodah is the avodah of light—to make the world bright with the light of Torah. Beyond the fact that one must personally stand in a proper state and position—one must be as one ought to be—the entire avodah is to merit producing students who are *gruntike mentshen* (thorough, grounded individuals), who are devoted in their hearts and minds to the inner intent. It is not enough to study the revealed Torah and cherish the holiness of observing the mitzvos; there must also be the avodah of the heart.

## Shevat

The month of Shevat is the month of the "New Year for Trees." This is the time when the sap begins to rise in the trees, which is the beginning of their growth and fruit-bearing. This is a lesson for every chossid: even when one is in a state of "winter"—a period of concealment or coldness in one's avodah—one must know that the potential for growth is already stirring within. Just as the tree begins its renewal from within, so too must a chossid awaken his inner vitality to ensure that his avodah brings forth fruit, which are the mitzvos and good deeds that Hashem desires.

## 1 Shevat, Rosh Chodesh

The recitation of Tehillim as prescribed (daily after davening) makes no distinction between the shuls of anash, may they live, or those who daven according to Nusach Ashkenaz or Nusach Polin, may Hashem grant them life. Out of ahavas Yisrael—and especially due to the immense importance of reciting Tehillim in a tzibbur and its inner intent, which affects Klal Yisrael literally in both gashmius, in children, life, and sustenance, and in ruchnius—we must exert every possible effort to ensure it is established in every beis medrash, regardless of the nusach.

## 2 Shevat

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, related: The maamar beginning *Va'era*... *v'shomi Hashem*... which discusses the concept that "no one shall be cast away from Him" (this is not the version printed in *Torah Or*), earned the name "the pious *Va'era*." The Alter Rebbe was accustomed to repeating it once every three years, and each time he would say it almost word-for-word. The Tzemach Tzedek said: "Each time, it was a new light." My father [the Rebbe Rashab] replied: "A light is always like a new thing."

## 3 Shevat

*Torah Or* was first printed in 5597 in Kapust (without the additions). In his letter dated 3 Shevat 5597, the Tzemach Tzedek writes: "The book *Torah Or*, which is now being printed... contains the *maamarim* and most of the *drushim* from the year 5556 until the end of 5612, many of which our Rebbe, of blessed memory, personally reviewed and edited, and he gave his holy consent to bring them to the printing press. The book consists of two parts: the first part is on the first two books of the Torah, Chanukah, and Purim, as well as several *drushim* for the holiday of Shavuos—the time of the Giving of the Torah—in *Parshas Yisro*, and a few for Pesach in *Parshas Vayakhel*. The second part, G-d willing, will be on the remaining three books of the Torah, *Shir Hashirim*, the *Moadim*, Rosh Hashanah, and Yom Kippur... and the name of the author is fitting for him: *Schneur*, which is *ahavah* in gematria, twice *or*..." (End quote).

They did not manage to print more than the first part, because due to an informer, the government closed several Hebrew printing houses in Russia, among them the printing house in Kapust where *Torah Or* was being printed. In the year 5608, the second part was printed in Zhitomir under the changed name, *Likutei Torah*.

## 4 Shevat

Mitzrayim (Egypt) is a term denoting *meitzar* (limitation) and boundary. The spiritual exile of Mitzrayim is the way in which the *nefesh habehamis* limits and conceals the *nefesh ha'elokis*, until the *nefesh ha'elokis* becomes so constricted that it enters a state of *katanus* (smallness) and concealment. The *Yetziyas Mitzrayim* (Exodus from Egypt) is the removal of this limitation and boundary. This means that the intellect in the brain illuminates the heart, manifesting in actual, good *midos*.

## 5 Shevat

A person should increase the recitation of *osios* (reciting *Tehillim*, reviewing *Mishnayos*) whenever and wherever he can, in order to strengthen the *kiyum ha'olam*, to be saved from *chibut hakever* and *kaf hakela*, and to merit all the highest *giluyim*.

## 6 Shevat

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, writes that he heard in the name of our Rebbe, the Alter Rebbe, that all the authors of halachic works up until the time of the Taz and the Shach—inclusive of them—composed their works with *ruach hakodesh*. Regarding the matter of *ruach hakodesh*, it is stated in *Korban HaEdah* at the end of the third chapter of *Shekalim* that this refers to when the secrets of Torah are revealed to a person, which is from the level of the hidden aspect of *Chochma*.

## 7 Shevat

When the Alter Rebbe was nine years old, he studied the sciences of geometry and astronomy. At age ten, he calculated a calendar for fifteen years. When he was twelve, he happened to teach the laws of Kiddush HaChodesh from the Rambam in public, and the geonim who were present could not find their hands or feet in the beis medrash.

## 8 Shevat

Especially in this time, when by the grace of Hashem we stand on the threshold of Geulah, we must exert every possible effort to strengthen all matters of religion with *hiddur mitzvah*, and to observe all customs without compromising even the slightest detail. It is a *mitzvah* and an obligation upon every Rav in Yisrael to inform his congregation that these troubles and sufferings are the *chevlei Mashiach*, and that Hashem demands from us *teshuvah* and [the observance of] *Torah u'mitzvos*, so that we do not delay the coming of our righteous Mashiach, immediately.

## 9 Shevat

When my revered father, the Rebbe, completed the Shas for the third time, he recited a maamar of Chassidus, the content of which served as a hadran.

In the year of mourning following his mother’s passing, my revered father completed the six orders of the Mishnah by the end of the eleven months, and by the day of the yahrtzeit—the entire Shas.

## 10 Shevat

In *Torah Or*, in the *maamar* beginning *Lema'an Tihyeh*, at the end of the section starting *Omnam*, the phrase "He shall not cease by day from seeing" should read "He shall not cease by day and by night from desiring."

My grandmother (the Rebbetzin Rivkah, of blessed memory) became ill when she was about eighteen years old—in the year 5611 (1851). The doctor ordered that she must eat immediately upon waking, but she did not wish to taste anything before *davening*. She would *daven* early in the morning and eat her breakfast only after *davening*. When this became known to her father-in-law, the Tzemach Tzedek, he said to her: "A Yid must be healthy and strong. Regarding *mitzvos*, it is written, 'And you shall live by them' (*V'chai bahem*). The meaning of 'And you shall live by them' is that one must bring *chayus* (vitality) into the *mitzvos*. In order to be able to bring *chayus* into *mitzvos*, one must be strong and be in a state of *simcha*." He concluded: "You do not need to be fasting. It is better to eat for the sake of *davening* than to *daven* for the sake of eating." He then blessed her with long life—(she was born in 5593 (1833) and passed away on 10 Shevat 5674 (1914)).

My father, the Rebbe, said this *maamar* to someone during *yechidus*, and added: "And one must do this with *simcha*."

## 11 Shevat

The order of the day begins with Modeh Ani. This is recited before washing one’s hands, even with ritually impure hands, for all the impurities in the world cannot render the Modeh Ani of a Yid impure. A person may be lacking in this or that, but the Modeh Ani remains whole.

## 12 Shevat

Intellect and *hispaalus* are two separate worlds: one is cool and composed, the other is fervent and tumultuous. It is the *avodah* of man to unite them into one. When this is achieved, the turbulence is transformed into aspiration, and the intellect becomes a guide for a life of *avodah* and action.

## 13 Shevat

Yahrtzeit — [It is observed] on the day of passing, even during the first year, and even when the day of burial is far removed from the day of passing.

The Tzemach Tzedek’s response to my grandfather [the Rebbe Maharash] when he was about seven years old: The kindness and superior quality that Hashem bestowed upon man, creating him upright so that he walks with an erect posture, is that even though he walks upon the earth, he nevertheless gazes toward the heavens. This is not so for one who walks on all fours, who sees only the earth.

## 14 Shevat

At Mincha, Tachanun is not recited.

The coming of our holy forefathers is, aside from the matter of awakening compassion for those connected to them, an avodah of bringing these connected individuals to mind, and contemplating their love and hiskashrus—"as water reflects a face to a face." This awakens the inner powers of the one being thought of, as we see tangibly: if one stares intensely at another, the other must look back, because the inner gaze awakens the essence of the soul, and the power of thought works in the same way.

## 15 Shevat

Tachanun is not recited.

When the preparations were being made to print the second part of *Torah Or*, the Chassidim were already aware that the Tzemach Tzedek had written glosses and explanations on the *maamarim*. They urged the Tzemach Tzedek to print them together with the *maamarim*, but he refused. He then had a dream in which his grandfather, the Alter Rebbe, came to visit him and requested that he print them. Even then, he kept the matter quiet, until three of his sons also had this same dream and told their father about it. Only then did he agree to print his glosses and explanations for the second part as well, and they named it *Likutei Torah*.

## 16 Shevat

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, said: Between coldness and *kefirah* there is only a thin partition. It is written, "For Hashem your G-d is a consuming fire" (*Devarim* 4:24); *Elokus* is a blazing fire. One must study and *davening* with *hispaalus* of the heart, so that "all my bones shall say" (*Tehillim* 35:10) the words of Hashem in *Torah* and *tefillah*.

## 17 Shevat

It is a custom to eat buckwheat.

Afterward, he related to his son, my grandfather the Rebbe [the Mitteler Rebbe]: "On Shabbos Beshalach of the year 5765, my grandfather [the Alter Rebbe] delivered this maamar. Afterward, he called me and told me that in the year 5729, while he was in Mezeritch, the Maggid called him into his room and said to him: 'On Shabbos Beshalach of the year 5716, the Baal Shem Tov delivered a maamar on the verse "And the sea returned at the approach of morning to its strength" (*eitano*), which the Sages interpret as "to its condition" (*litna'o*). In the year 5721 (a year after the passing of the Baal Shem Tov), my Rebbe (the Baal Shem Tov) came to me,' the Maggid continued, 'and delivered the maamar, adding an explanation regarding the concept of "those who do His will" (*osai retzono*)—and not "those who speak" (*dovrei*)—of the Omnipresent. And today, my teacher came to me to repeat the maamar.' The Maggid then delivered the maamar before the Alter Rebbe, adding an explanation regarding the concept of *genai nahara* (the shame of the river), which is akin to the concept of the splitting of the Sea."

The Tzemach Tzedek concluded: "Today, the Baal Shem Tov, the Maggid, and my grandfather were with me, and each one delivered the maamar to me in his own style." A few hours later, the Tzemach Tzedek called for my grandfather the Rebbe once again and delivered an explanation of the maamar before him.

## 18 Shevat

The Tzemach Tzedek told my grandfather, the Rebbe [the Rebbe Rayatz], that the maamar "u'mareihem u'maaseihem" — found in Torah Or, Parshas Yisro — is the very first maamar delivered by the Maggid of Mezritch upon assuming the leadership, on Shavuos 5521. He heard this from our Alter Rebbe, who heard it from Rav Menachem Mendel of Horodok — author of Pri HaAretz — who was present at that occasion; the Alter Rebbe simply explained the maamar according to his own style.

## 19 Shevat

The first positive commandment—which the Rambam phrases as, "To know that there is a First Being who brings into existence all that exists... and the knowledge of this matter is a positive commandment, as it is written, 'I am Hashem your G-d'"—is a commandment of the mind and the intellect. This means that even though every single Yid believes in Hashem with simple faith and his heart is whole with Hashem, it is the obligation of the mind and intellect to bring this faith into the realm of knowledge and comprehension. This is what is meant by "to know that there is a First Being there"—he must know this specifically through *hasagah* (intellectual grasp) and *havanah* (understanding). As it is written, "Know the G-d of your father and serve Him with a whole heart," and it is also written, "And you shall know this day, etc."

## 20 Shevat

My revered father, the Rebbe, writes in one of his letters: According to the Hagahos Ashri, one should perform netilas yadayim for a meal three consecutive times. My revered father—the Rebbe Maharash—would do exactly this, and would pour a small amount of water from the third pouring into his left palm, with which he would then rub both of his hands together.

## 21 Shevat

It is the duty of the wives and daughters of Chassidim to stand in the front ranks of every endeavor to strengthen Yiddishkeit and Torah observance in general, and specifically regarding the laws of Taharas Hamishpacha. They must organize groups for the daughters of Chassidim to provide guidance and education, just as has been the custom in Chassidic homes since time immemorial.

## 22 Shevat

There are two types of laws: a) a law that creates life, and b) a law that is created by life. Human laws are created by life; therefore, they differ from country to country, according to the conditions of the land. Toras Hashem is the Divine law that creates life. Toras Hashem is the Torah of truth; the Torah is the same in all places and at all times. Torah is eternal.

## 23 Shevat

Once, upon leaving his room, the Alter Rebbe overheard the Rebbetzin saying to several women, "Mine says."

The Rebbe responded: "With one mitzvah I am yours; with how many mitzvos is one Hashem’s?" He then fell upon the floor and became lost in *dveikus*. Upon emerging from this *dveikus*, he said: "*Tze'enah u're'enah* (Go out and see) — to go out of oneself and see *Elokus* — this is achieved by *benos Tzion* (daughters of Zion), for *Malchus* awakens *Ze'er Anpin*. In the future to come, it will be, 'A woman of valor is the crown of her husband.'"

## 24 Shevat

"And the people saw and trembled" (*Vayiru ha'am vayanuu*). When they perceived Elokus, there was immediately a *vayanu*—it became a movement of life.

If you knew, the Tzemach Tzedek said, the power of the pesukim of Tehillim and their effect in the highest heavens, you would recite them at all times. Know that the chapters of Tehillim break through all barriers, ascending from level to level without any hindrance, to prostrate themselves before the Ribono shel Olam and accomplish their purpose with chesed and rachamim.

## 25 Shevat

Study a portion of Chumash with Rashi every day (on Sunday up to the second aliyah, on Monday up to the third, and so on), and recite Tehillim every day. Complete the entire book of Tehillim on Shabbos Mevorchim. These are practices one must observe; they are vital for oneself, one’s children, and one’s children’s children.

## 26 Shevat

Ahavah is the spirit of life in the avodah of Chassidus; it is the thread that connects Chassidim to one another, and the thread that connects the Rebbe to the Chassidim and the Chassidim to the Rebbe. It operates both by way of Ohr Yashar and by way of Ohr Chozer, has no barriers, and is beyond the limitations of space and time.

## 27 Shevat

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, writes in one of his maamarim: The Chassidim of old made a pact within themselves that for anything which is permitted, but for which they felt some desire or craving of the heart, they would abstain from doing it; and through this, they achieved the breaking of their desires.

## 28 Shevat

One of the teachings of the Baal Shem Tov:
"When you see the donkey" — when you look with careful scrutiny at his *chomer* (materiality), which is the body, you will see "your enemy" — that it is hostile to the *neshamah* which yearns for *Elokus* and spirituality. Furthermore, you will see that it is "crouching under its burden" — the burden that the *Ribono shel Olam* placed upon the body to be refined through *Torah* and *mitzvos*, yet the body is sluggish in fulfilling them. And perhaps it will occur to you "that you shall refrain from helping him" — that you might be able to fulfill your mission by starting with *sigufim* (ascetic practices) to break the materiality. However, the light of *Torah* does not dwell in this way. Rather, "you shall surely help him" — to clarify and refine the body, not to break it through *sigufim*.

## 29 Shevat

The Alter Rebbe once said: Rashi’s commentary on Chumash is the "wine of Torah," which opens the heart and reveals essential ahavah and yirah. Rashi’s commentary on Gemara opens the mind and reveals essential intellect.

## 30 Shevat, Rosh Chodesh

Many Chassidim considered the day they arrived in Lubavitch to be their true birthday. My teacher, the Rashbatz, arrived in Lubavitch for the first time on the eve of Friday, Parshas Mishpatim, in the year 5608 (1848). Every year thereafter, he would stay awake the entire night, timing his putting on of tefillin to the exact moment he had first entered the presence of the Tzemach Tzedek.

## Adar I

The month of Adar I is a month of preparation for the service of Adar II. The *avodah* of Adar I is to refine the *nefesh habehamis* through *tzedakah* and *teshuvah*, and to increase in joy. This joy is not yet the complete joy of Purim, but rather a preparation for it, as the *maamarim* explain that the *avodah* of the entire year is dependent on the *avodah* of the days of Adar.

## 1 Adar I, Rosh Chodesh

Torah Or, in the maamar beginning *Lo Tihyeh Meshakelah*, in the section starting *VeHinei Achar*: Regarding the phrase "b'mesiras nefesh b'f"a," the acronym stands for "b'pnimius atzmuso" (in the inwardness of His essence).

It is written: "Man goes forth to his work and to his labor until evening" (Tehillim 104:23). Every neshamah, upon its descent below, has general and specific tasks. "Man goes forth to his work" refers to the departure of the neshamah from its station in the heights of heaven, in the treasury of souls, and its descent from level to level until it comes to be enclothed in a body and the nefesh habehamis. This is for the sake of "his work"—the general tasks of overcoming the material with the spiritual, and illuminating the world with the light of Torah and the candle of mitzvah. "And to his labor" refers to the specific task, for every neshamah has a specific avodah in the mochin and middos according to its nature and character. "Until evening"—while there is still time to act, as it is written: "Today to perform them." 

In the deeper sense, the explanation of the verse is that it refers to the general concept of the ascent achieved through the descent of the neshamah below—that "man goes forth." When the neshamah ascends from its state below, where it was enclothed in a body, "to his work"—it engages in the World to Come in the same manner it engaged in this world. If one set fixed times for Torah, they bring him into the tents of Torah there as well. "To his labor"—if one engaged in his avodah as is fitting, then his ascent is "until evening," rising from level to level until the pleasantness and sweetness of the essence of the Ein Sof, blessed be He.

## 2 Adar I

Our great Rebbe—the Alter Rebbe—established for Anash the avodah of the mind: to seek the truth and to examine every movement, ensuring it is only in accordance with the truth and achieved through avodah.

This avodah is not, as some mistakenly believe, a matter of needing to move mountains and shatter rocks—to turn the world upside down. The absolute truth is that any avodah or action, whatever it may be, is sufficient and complete if done with true kavanah: a bracha with kavanah; a word in davening recited properly, with preparation of the heart and the awareness of before Whom you stand; a pasuk of Chumash with the knowledge that it is the word of Hashem; a pasuk of Tehillim; or a good deed to draw a fellow’s heart closer with love and affection.

It is true that to reach this, one needs great and immense labor—simply to learn a lot and to understand—each according to his own level—and then Hashem will assist him so that it will be in accordance with the truth.

## 3 Adar I

The Alter Rebbe said: The mitzvah of ahavas Yisrael applies to any fellow Jew whom one has never even met, and all the more so to a member of the Jewish community in one's own place of residence, who is a brother or sister of one's own community.

## 4 Adar I

The primary preparation for engaging in intellectual study—and specifically in the study of Elokus—is: a) physical exertion (*yigias basar*) to remove the sense of pleasure from worldly matters, and b) spiritual exertion (*yigias nefesh*) to awaken a sense of pleasure in intellectual concepts in general, and in matters of Elokus in particular.

## 5 Adar I

Without a shadow of a doubt, or even a shadow of a doubt upon a doubt, every place where our feet tread is for the purpose of bringing merit and purifying the land through the letters of Torah and tefillah. We, all of Klal Yisrael, are shluchim of the Merciful One, each and every one of us according to the decree of Divine Providence. No one is exempt from the holy avodah that has been placed upon our shoulders.

## 6 Adar I

From the sichos of my father-in-law, the Rebbe: It is a wonderful merit when Hashem grants a person the sense and the taste for doing a favor for a fellow Jew, to the point that the other person becomes dearer to him than he is to himself. For regarding himself, he can find many reasons why, Heaven forbid, he might deserve to suffer, but regarding another person, this is not applicable at all.

## 7 Adar I

Every person in Yisroel must know that he is a shaliach of the Master of all, in order to bring into actuality—wherever he may be—His will, may He be blessed, and His intent in the creation of the world, which is to illuminate the world with the light of Torah and avodah. This is accomplished through the performance of practical mitzvos and the rooting of good middos.

## 8 Adar I

The Alter Rebbe once called in a young man, one of the Maggid’s disciples, and said to him—with a nigun, as was his custom—: "I have a mitzvah of 'And you shall teach them to your children,' and you have a mitzvah of providing for your household. Let us trade: I will provide for you so that you can fulfill your mitzvah, and you will learn with my son"—this was the Mitteler Rebbe. He then explained to him the order of study: "First, one learns the letters, Alef, Beis... What is an Alef? A dot above, a dot below, and a line in the middle—that is an Alef. A child must know that the Alef of Torah is the yud above and the yud below, with the line of emunah that connects them." Another version exists: "A yud above is the neshamah, a yud below is the guf, and a line of yiras shamayim in the middle."

## 9 Adar I

When one walks in the street and thinks of Mishnayos or Tanya, or sits in a store with a Chumash or a Tehillim, it is more precious now than in the times when the street was illuminated with the light of Torah. One must not walk in the street empty-handed; one must have Torah with which to walk in the street.

## 10 Adar I

In Birchas Hamazon, before Mayim Acharonim, one recites "Al Naharos Bavel," "Lamnatzeiach B'neginos," and "Avarchah" — this is one section.
On a day when Tachanun is not recited: "Shir Hamaalos B'shuv," "Livnei Korach," and "Avarchah" — this is one section.
After Mayim Acharonim, one recites "Vayedaber Eilai."

## 11 Adar I

It is a well-known and accepted principle regarding the *kavanah* of *tefillah* that for those who are not sufficiently learned to focus on the specific *kavanos*, whether due to a lack of knowledge or an inability to recall the individual *kavanos* during *davening*, it is sufficient to have a general *kavanah*: that their *tefillah* be heard before Him, may He be blessed, together with all the *kavanos* explained in the books of Kabbalah.

## 12 Adar I

It is written: "A man’s gift makes room for him, and brings him before great men" (Mishlei 18:16). There are people who are considered "great" by name, whether due to their wisdom or their wealth. However, when it comes to *matan adam*—when a person gives of himself, of his own being, to some endeavor in support of the faith—this not only "makes room for him," but furthermore, "brings him before great men."

## 13 Adar I

At Mincha, Tachanun is not recited.

My father, the Rebbe, said: It is clear to me that when a Chassidish Yid sits in the beis medrash and learns or reviews Chassidus in public, there is joy among the grandfathers [the Rebbes], and their joy is sufficient to provide him, his children, and his children's children with an abundance of good, both in gashmius and in ruchnius.

## 14 Adar I, Purim Katan

We do not say Tachanun.

My grandfather [the Rebbe Rashab] related to my father [the Frierdiker Rebbe]: The maamarim that are printed in Likutei Torah were selected by Father [the Tzemach Tzedek] from two thousand maamarim.

## 15 Adar I, Shushan Purim Katan

We do not say Tachanun.
One makes a mark on the bread before reciting the blessing of Hamotzi, but one must be careful not to actually cut it.
Also, when making Kiddush over bread, we say "Savri Maranan."

Regarding the future, it is written: "A stone shall cry out from the wall, and a beam from the wood shall answer it." Currently, the inanimate world is silent; one walks upon it, and it remains silent. But a time will come, in the revelation of the future, when the inanimate will begin to speak, to recount, and to demand: if one did not think or speak words of Torah while walking, why did one walk upon it?

The earth upon which we walk has been waiting for thousands of years, since the six days of Bereishis. In the meantime, various creatures and animals walk upon it, until a Yid—or two Yidden—walk there and speak a *dvar Torah*.

## 16 Adar I

The Alter Rebbe said: The contributions for the Mishkan included gold, silver, and copper. There was nothing that shone, however, except for the mirrors of the women who assembled, from which the laver and its base were made. The laver and its base are the final items among all the vessels of the Mishkan, yet their usage precedes all the work of the Mishkan, for their beginning is anchored in their end.

## 17 Adar I

Many years after Petersburg (the imprisonment there in the year 5559), the Alter Rebbe once came out to the chassidim and said: In Gan Eden, the preciousness of this world is understood. It is not just the ministering angels; even the highest *ne'etzalim* would give everything for one *Amein Yehei Shmei Rabba* of a Yid, provided he says it with all his strength, according to its interpretation, and with all his intent—meaning, he is entirely within it.

This was the entire *maamar*, and he brought about such *hislahavus* and *hislahatus* among the listeners that for an entire year, they said *Amein Yehei Shmei Rabba* with a fire.

## 18 Adar I

In the *Beracha Me'ein Shalosh*, if one drank wine and ate fruits from the *Shivah Minim*, the conclusion is: *v'al pri hagafen v'al hapeirot, baruch atah Hashem al pri hagafen v'al hapeirot* (and not *v'al* hapeirot).

## 19 Adar I

The Chassidim, even the beinonim, were well-versed in Tanach. It was their established custom that after studying the Mishnayos shiur following Shacharis, while rolling up their tefillin, they would recite a shiur of Tanach, such that they would complete the entire Tanach over the course of three months.

## 20 Adar I

Avodah is not merely that one’s avodah should be "true"; rather, truth itself is an avodah—that one’s very fingernails are "true." Why are you so moved? The attribute of truth—*ra'ah* (Sanhedrin 111a) and he fell upon his face.

## 21 Adar I

The name "Chassid" is an ancient term, found in the words of our Sages even regarding Adam HaRishon (Eruvin 18b). It is a title denoting perfection and excellence, whether in intellect, in character traits, or in both together. However, in the teachings of Chassidus Chabad, the title "Chassid" refers to one who recognizes his own essence and standing in the knowledge of Torah and study, as well as his situation in the fulfillment of mitzvos. He knows what he lacks, he sets his heart and worries about filling that lack, and he increases his discipline through the path of kabalas ol.

## 22 Adar I

My revered father and teacher writes in one of his maamarim: The fact that there is no Seudah Shlishis today, etc., means that bread is not required, but one must taste something. And Rabbi Yossi said, "May my portion be among those who eat three meals."

The teaching of Rabbeinu HaZaken at the beginning of his arrival in Liozna: A person should always be careful with the Mincha prayer. The advantage of the Mincha prayer over the prayers of Shacharis and Maariv is that it occurs in the middle of the day, at a time when people are occupied and busy with their affairs, and they pause for the Mincha prayer. And this is the meaning of: "Always (le'olam) — the avodah of a person in the world (olam) is that he should be a 'person' (adam), meaning that the intellect (seichel) which illuminates and acts upon the middos should be 'careful' (zahir) — that is, it should 'illuminate' (ya'ir), which is the triumph of form over matter. And this is evident in the matter of the Mincha prayer.

## 23 Adar I

The Rabbis and those learned in Torah are called the "eyes of the community" and the "heads of the thousands of Yisrael." When the head is healthy, the body is healthy as well.

## 24 Adar I

The Mitteler Rebbe answered R’ Hillel Paritcher regarding his question of whether he should repeat Chassidus in towns where the people have no concept of Chassidus: "The *neshamah* hears the words of Chassidus. And it is written, 'And flowing streams from Levanon' (*Shir Hashirim* 4:15); *Levanon* is *Lamed-Beis Nun-Nun* (32 and 50), representing the *Chochma* and *Binah* within the *neshamah*. And when the *neshamah* hears, it flows and drips into the illumination of the *neshamah* that gives life to the body, and this creates strength in the *aseh tov* of the 248 *mitzvos aseh* and in the *sur meira* of the 365 *mitzvos lo taaseh*."

## 25 Adar I

*Hoshianu* is recited after the *Shir Shel Yom* (Psalm of the Day), both on weekdays and on Shabbos, Yom Tov, Rosh Hashanah, and Yom Kippur.

## 26 Adar I

With three vessels of service—love of Hashem, love of Torah, and love of a fellow Jew—young Torah scholars must approach the work in the vineyard of Hashem Tzeva-os, to bring the hearts of their brothers closer to the fulfillment of practical mitzvos and the setting of fixed times for Torah study, without regard for the malaise of partisanship. The absolute truth is that the heart of a Jew is like a fountain of living waters, and a covenant has been made that labor and outreach effort will never return void.

## 27 Adar I

My revered father and teacher said: Truth is the path of the middle. A leaning to the right—to be stringent with oneself and find faults or sinful matters in oneself that are not in accordance with the truth—or a leaning to the left—to be lenient with oneself, to cover up one’s own faults, or to be lenient in matters of avodah out of self-love—are both false paths.

## 28 Adar I

Regarding the Krias Shema al Hamitah: The prayer "Ribono shel Olam, I hereby forgive..." and the passage "Lamnatzeach..." (beginning with "b'vo...") are not recited on Shabbos or Yom Tov. However, they should be recited on other days when Tachanun is not said. At the conclusion of the three paragraphs of Shema, one says the word "Emes." "Ya'alzu Chassidim" is said once, and "Hinei Mitaso" is said three times. During Tikkun Chatzos, one does not recite "Lamnatzeach..." (beginning with "b'vo...") on days when Tachanun is not said.

## 29 Adar I

There are two formulas for saying "L'chaim":

1) "L'chaim tovim u'l'shalom" (For a good life and for peace). The reason for this blessing is that the first time wine is mentioned in the Torah, the results were not good—"And Noach began..."—and the Tree of Knowledge was a grapevine. Therefore, we bless that this wine should be for a good life.

2) The Maggid of Mezritch would respond, "L'chaim u'l'vracha" (For life and for blessing). Once, at a farbrengen where the Alter Rebbe was present, the Alter Rebbe responded, "L'chaim u'l'vracha." After the farbrengen, the Chassidim discussed the reason for this formula, as they had heard it for the first time. One Chassid said that because "when wine enters, secrets emerge," and in avodah this refers to the revelation of the middos, one is in need of a blessing. The formula is "L'chaim u'l'vracha"—the letters of which spell *lev-racha* (a soft heart). The Tzemach Tzedek remarked on this: "That is something a Chassid can say who has davened and engaged in avodah for thirty years."

## 30 Adar I, Rosh Chodesh

My father, the Rebbe, said: A Chossid creates an environment. If he does not, he must take a good look into his own pack to see what is going on with himself. And the very fact that he does not create an environment should break him like a splinter. He must ask himself: What am I accomplishing in this world?

## Adar II

The month of Adar II is the month of "reversal." In the first Adar, the *mazal* is *dagim* (fish), which are hidden from the eye. In Adar II, the *mazal* is also *dagim*, but the *avodah* is to bring this hiddenness into the open. This is the inner meaning of "they were turned to the contrary" (*v'nahafochu*): that which was previously hidden and concealed is revealed and made manifest. This is the preparation for the month of Nissan, the month of redemption, which is entirely revealed.

## 1 Adar II, Rosh Chodesh

In Ya'aleh V'yavo: *Zochreinu Hashem Elokeinu bo* — the *beis* is *dagesh* — and *ufokdeinu bo* — the *beis* is *rafeh*.

## 2 Adar II

A blessing requires something to take hold of, just as rain requires a field that has been plowed and sown, or the early and late rains require the produce of the field and the vineyard. But in a fallow field that has not been plowed or sown, the early and late rains bring no benefit.

## 3 Adar II

The response of the Tzemach Tzedek to a Chassid who was well-versed in Shas and a great maskil in Chassidus: Kabbalas ol changes one’s essence. Through the kabbalas ol of a simple servant—whose yoke of servitude is evident even while he sleeps—even a lamdan and a gaon can attain the level and importance of a simple, sincere Yid of mesiras nefesh.

## 4 Adar II

When the Mitteler Rebbe would say Chassidus, it was very quiet, yet during the Chassidus he would often say: "Sha, sha" (hush, hush).

My father explained that this was to quiet the gushing forth of the *mochin* (intellectual faculties). With this, he explained the statement of the Zohar regarding the *Saba* (the Elder): "His mind is hidden, quiet, and tranquil."

## 5 Adar II

The study of Nigleh, the study of Chassidus, and avodah in practice—all three are equally essential. Not only is it impossible to say that one should displace or be displaced by the other, but on the contrary, they support one another. As the saying goes: "An ignoramus cannot be a Chassid"—which necessitates the study of Nigleh. "Who is a Chassid? One who acts kindly toward his Creator"—which necessitates the study of Chassidus. And the ultimate purpose of all study is avodah in practice.

## 6 Adar II

It is a tradition among the elders of Anash: The Sefer HaTanya is a collection of the advice that our great Rebbe gave to Anash during yechidus in the years 5540–5550. In the summer of 5552, he began to arrange the Sefer HaTanya in the form in which it is before us now. By 5553, there were already many copies of it, but over time they became corrupted, and people even began to forge them. For this reason, our Rebbe hastened to have it printed. There is another version: Our Rebbe wrote his Sefer HaTanya over the course of twenty years, and he was precise with every single word. By the year 5555, it was already refined and purified, and at that point he gave permission for it to be copied; when the copies multiplied and became corrupted, he had it printed.

The Tzemach Tzedek related that on the first Rosh Hashanah of his life—the year 5550—our great Rebbe delivered a maamar on the concept of "They adjure him: 'Be a tzaddik...'", and that is the first three chapters of Sefer HaTanya.

## 7 Adar II

When walking in the street, one should be occupied with words of Torah, whether in thought or in speech—depending on whether it is halachically permitted to speak words of Torah in that location. However, if one walks along and is not occupied with words of Torah, the stone upon which one treads says to him: "You fool (golem), why are you stepping on me? In what way are you superior to me?"

## 8 Adar II

My revered father and teacher writes in one of his letters: One action is better than a thousand sighs. Our G-d is living, and Torah and mitzvos are eternal. Abandon the sighing and apply yourself to actual avodah, and G-d will grant you success.

## 9 Adar II

In the blessing *Borei Nefashos* (Creator of souls) for all that You have created — the *chof* is vocalized with a *cholam*, not a *kamatz*.

The intensity of the longing for *hiskashrus* can only be satisfied when one studies the *maamarim* of *Chassidus* that the Rebbe says and writes, for merely seeing his face is not enough.

## 10 Adar II

Before departing from his place of residence, one should arrange a *chassidishen farbrengen* and receive a parting blessing from his good friends. As the well-known saying goes: *Chassidim* do not say goodbye, because they never truly part ways; wherever one is, they remain one family.

## 11 Adar II

Chassidus comprises two primary elements: 1) Understanding every halacha at its root and essential source in spirituality—that is, in the Sefiros and levels of each respective world, as explained in Chassidic discourse. 2) Understanding the relevance of every halacha to avodah—meaning that even though it is Divine wisdom, intellect, and the law of the Torah, one must nevertheless find within it a matter of avodah for a person’s conduct in the life of this world.

## 12 Adar II

The beginning of the *parsha* of the *korbanos* is: "When a man among you brings an offering to Hashem" (*Vayikra 1:2*). It should have logically stated, "A man from among you, when he brings an offering..." The Alter Rebbe explains: "When a man brings an offering" (*Adam ki yakriv*)—in order for a person to become closer (*nuhunter*) to Hashem, "from among you shall be the offering to Hashem" (*mikem korban laHashem*). The offering must come from you yourselves; one must offer up his own animal—the *yetzer hara*, which is called the *nefesh habehamis*.

## 13 Adar II

Haftorah: *Ko Amar Hashem*. We recite *Av Harachamim*. We do not recite *Tzidkascha*.

"Then Amalek came and fought with Yisrael in Refidim" (*Shemos 17:8*)—they weakened (*rafu*) their hands from the words of Torah. When one becomes weak in Torah—for the study of Torah is intended for the purpose of doing and fulfilling—Amalek comes and cools the Jew’s enthusiasm. "And fought with Yisrael" (*Amalek im Yisrael*): the acronym of *Yisrael* (*Yud-Shin-Reish-Alef-Lamed*) refers to the six hundred thousand letters of the Torah—for every single Jew has one letter in the Torah, which is why it is the custom of all Yisrael to have a letter written in a Torah scroll—and Amalek cools the holiness of the Torah. The counsel for this is "Choose for us men" (*Shemos 17:9*)—the men of Moshe, the extension of Moshe in every generation, for in every generation there are the heads of the thousands of Yisrael. And "Go out, fight with Amalek"—the command is in the singular, because the Torah is eternal, in every generation, at every time, and in every place, equally.

(9:2) *v'ish lo amad bifneihem* and *v'ish lo amad lifneihem*. When saying "this letter" and "this second letter of Purim," we shake the Megillah.

There is another version: "And behold, the aspect of 'on that day' is the aspect of Binah, *Imma Ila'ah*, which dwells in Beriah, in the *Kisei*. However, the Malchus of Atzilus becomes an Atik to Beriah, for it is removed and elevated above Beriah, and it does not come into revelation in the world of Beriah, for even the aspect of 'on that day' is in a state of concealment and hiddenness. And that which is said, 'I will surely hide My face'—for this is what Malchus is called, and from the Malchus that is in Chochma, it becomes the Chochma that is in Malchus, etc."

R' Moshe Meislish related: The Rebbe (the Alter Rebbe) taught us that the aleph of Chassidus is to utilize one's natural tendencies in avodah. The beginning of this avodah should be to utilize the nature of one's faculties—for example, the natural dominance of the mind over the heart.

## 20 Adar II

The Alter Rebbe related: Among the teachings that my teacher (the Maggid) said to me in private was a discourse on the verse, "A constant fire shall burn upon the altar; it shall not be extinguished" (*Vayikra 6:6*). Even though the fire descends from above through *is'arusa d'le'eila* (arousal from above), it is a mitzvah to bring [fire] from a common person, for through *is'arusa d'l'tata* (arousal from below) comes *is'arusa d'le'eila*. As it is said, "Spirit brings spirit and draws down spirit"—spirit from below brings spirit from above, and draws down spirit from higher and higher still. It is a positive mitzvah to kindle fire upon the altar; the altar is the person, as in the verse "When a person among you brings an offering" (*Vayikra 1:2*). The offering itself is not enough; one must kindle a fire upon the offering that is "among you." And this fire "shall not be extinguished" (*lo tichbeh*), for it extinguishes the "no" (*lo*).

My teacher said this discourse to me ten times—in order to engrave it upon my ten soul-powers—and he said to me: "You, my student, are in need of this constant fire, because you are tasked with extinguishing a great 'no' (of the opponents). You will extinguish the 'no,' and Hashem will transform the 'no' into 'yes' (*hen*)."

## 21 Adar II

The Tzemach Tzedek said at the conclusion of the aforementioned (20 Adar II): My grandfather, the Alter Rebbe, is the Moshe Rabbeinu of the Torah of Chassidus Chabad. Regarding Moshe, our Sages of blessed memory said that the Torah was given to him, yet he acted with a "good eye" [generosity]. The *eish tamid* (eternal fire) of Elokus that pertains to the Torah of Chassidus Chabad was given by the Maggid to my grandfather, and my grandfather acted with a "good eye" and gave it to all who engage in the study of the Torah of Chassidus. It is clear to me that whoever learns with his fellow and awakens within him this *eish tamid* of Elokus, his reward is with him, for this merit of his shall never be extinguished.

And all matters must touch upon the innermost point of the soul, literally, for it is his very life."

## 27 Adar II

From a sicha of my father, the Rebbe: The sin of the sons of Aharon was that they drew near before Hashem and died—it was *ratzo* without *shov*. The truth is that when one looks "before Hashem," there must be *tiharu*—purity and clarity. It must be felt in *maaseh b'poel*. The higher the level, the lower it must descend; *ratzo* without *shov* is death. Regarding this came the command of Hashem to Moshe (*Vayikra 16:2*) to speak to Aharon—the letters of *Aharon* are the same as *nir'eh* (to be seen), and in the powers of the soul, this refers to *haskalah* (intellectual comprehension). This means that in order to enter the *Kodesh*, even within the *paroches*—which is before the *Tzimtzum*—one must know that upon *Aharon* (the intellect) there is a *kapores* (a cover), a *kavanah* of concealment, which is the *pnei ha'kapores*. Therefore, "and he shall not die"—one must not remain with *ratzo* alone. "For in a cloud I will appear upon the *kapores*"; the purpose of the first *Tzimtzum* is revelation, and the entry point to this is the *va'el* (the "and he shall come"), which is *bitul atzmi*—doing what Chassidus instructs, not what the intellect dictates. Then, *yavo el ha'kodesh*—he will come into the *Kodesh*.

## 29 Adar II

At one of the farbrengens, my revered father, the Rebbe, said: Hashem created the world and all physical things from *ayin* (nothingness) to *yesh* (being). *Un Yidden darfen machen yesh me-ayin* — and Jews must make *yesh* into *ayin*; to transform physicality into spirituality, and to make the physical a *keli* (vessel) for spirituality. This is a *chovas gavra* (a personal obligation), and every single individual is obligated in this specifically.

## Nissan

The month of Nissan is the month of redemption. The *avodah* of Nissan is to illuminate the darkness of the world with the light of *geulah*. Just as the *geulah* from Mitzrayim was an act of Hashem that transcended the natural order, so too must our *avodah* in this month be one of *mesiras nefesh* that transcends our own limitations. Through this, we draw down the *ohr* of *geulah* into our daily *avodah*, transforming the world into a dwelling place for Hashem.

## 1 Nissan, Rosh Chodesh

We do not recite Tachanun throughout the entire month of Nissan.

After reciting the Nasi, we recite the *Yehi Ratzon* printed in the Siddur Torah Or; Kohanim and Leviim also recite it.

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, instructed his brother-in-law, R' M.H. HaKohen, to recite the *Yehi Ratzon* that follows the Nasi, and told him that even a Kohen and a Levi must recite it, because it pertains to the *ibbur* (the leap month/intercalation).

## 2 Nissan

The Hilula of my revered father-in-law, the Rebbe. He passed away on the night of Sunday, 2 Nissan, in the year 5680 (1920), in Rostov, where he is interred.

The first Maamar he delivered in public after the passing of his father was on the second day of Chol Hamoed Sukkos, 5643 (1882), beginning with the words "Kesser Yitnu Lecha" (A crown they shall give You), etc. The last Maamar he delivered in public while in this world was at the Purim Seudah, 5680 (1920), beginning with the words "Reishis Goyim Amalek" (The first of nations is Amalek), etc., [continuing to] "Ketz Sam Lachoshech" (He set an end to darkness).

## 3 Nissan

From the shiurim of my revered father: Every day, study the parsha of the Chumash with Rashi. Recite Tanach by heart: one chapter from Torah and one from Kesuvim. Study one chapter of Mishnayos. The shiur for Gemara b'iyun is two blatt per week; the shiur for girsa is three blatt per day. There is also a shiur—not by heart—in Poskim. Throughout the year, he would complete the entire Midrash Rabbah: "borrowing" from the long sedros and "repaying" during the short ones.

## 4 Nissan

The avodah of businessmen is divided into two primary categories:

1) The avodah with oneself. When actually engaged in business, during spare moments—while sitting in the shop or similar situations—one should study Mishnayos or a chapter of Tanya. One should have certain Torah texts, such as Chumash, Mishnayos, or Tanya, that one is fluent in by heart, so that one can review them while walking in the street, in the marketplace, and the like.

2) The avodah with others. When speaking about business matters, one should steer the conversation toward sharing a meaningful story, and find a pretext and reason to inspire [the other person] regarding Torah study and the like.

## 5 Nissan

In the *Retzei* paragraph of *Birkas Hamazon*, one says "Ba'al HaYeshuos" with a *dagesh* in the *beis*, and "u'va'al HaNechamos" with a *rafe* (without a *dagesh*) in the *beis*.

My grandfather, the Rebbe, said regarding the Sages' teaching, "A person’s place does not honor him; rather, a person honors his place": The word *kavod* (honor) has two meanings. One is related to *koved* (heaviness), as the Sages say regarding "Pharaoh’s heart was hardened (*kaved*)"—his heart became heavy. The second is *kavod* in the sense of glory—the revelation of a sublime *makif* light. 

And this is the meaning: "A person’s place does not honor him"—meaning, it does not make him "heavy" or cold. Rather, "a person honors his place"—he is given the strength and power to illuminate his place with the light of Torah and *avodah*. Just as when the *neshamah* descends into the body, they adjure it, "Be a *tzaddik*," and this oath is a matter of being "satiated" (*sova*) with sublime powers so that it can fulfill the intent of its descent, so too is it for every single person in the place where he is.

## 6 Nissan

Experience has shown that when one minimizes intellectual speculation and instead works with order and firmness, in accordance with the pleasantness of Torah—using the right hand to draw near and the left hand to push away—then one will certainly achieve results, especially regarding the fundamental principles of the faith.

## 7 Nissan

The text of *Vayehi Binsoa*: "And according to the will of all Your people, the House of Israel."
The family name of the Alter Rebbe: Boruchovitch.
The family name of his son, the Mitteler Rebbe: Shneuri.
The family name of the Tzemach Tzedek: Schneersohn.

## 8 Nissan

Every soul has a specific avodah in its mochin and middos, according to its own nature and character. It is written, "From my enemies You make me wise" (Tehillim 119:98); from the negative inclinations that one feels in his natural middos, he becomes wise and learns how to manage the rectification of his middos and the subjugation of his faculties in the avodah of the Ribono shel Olam.

## 9 Nissan

Houses and money are not the true wealth of a Jew. The eternal wealth of a Jew is that we are Jews who observe Torah and mitzvos, and that we bring children and grandchildren into the world who observe Torah and mitzvos.

## 10 Nissan

Regarding the effort to promote *taharas hamishpacha* in one’s settlement, one should contemplate the following: If it were imaginable that Hashem had orchestrated for him to save a Jewish settlement from destruction, *r"l*, he would surely have been willing to sacrifice his life for it, and he would have praised and thanked the *Ribono shel Olam* for the immense kindness of granting him the merit of such a great deed. Yet, this effort is even greater than that, for it is a literal saving of souls.

## 11 Nissan

On one’s birthday, a person should seclude himself, review his memories, and contemplate them. Whatever requires rectification and teshuvah, he should repent for and rectify.

## 12 Nissan

From the time of the Exodus from Egypt, the Children of Israel were called the "Legions of Hashem" (*Tzivaos Hashem*). The difference between "servants" (*avadim*)—a term also used for the Children of Israel—and "legions" (*tzivaos*) is as follows: A servant performs the work of his master—which includes various levels, from the skilled artisan to the one performing other tasks, to the one performing menial labor—and while this involves great toil and exertion, it is not a matter of *mesiras nefesh*. A legion, however, is engaged in an offensive war; they stand at their post with the utmost strength, without being affected by the adversary or the enemy. Their service is not a matter of intellectual comprehension, for they conduct themselves according to the command of their commander.

Now, the souls of Israel in Egypt were in the depths of degradation, subjected to harsh and bitter afflictions, yet they did not change their names, their language, or their mode of dress. They stood at their post with the utmost fortitude, for they knew that the Holy One, Blessed be He, had promised to redeem them. One who conducts himself in this manner, in such a state, is of the "Legions of Hashem," for Hashem assists him in a way that is above nature.

## 13 Nissan

The Hillula of the Tzemach Tzedek. He passed away in the year 5626 in Lubavitch, and there is his final resting place.

From the stories of my grandfather: The Tzemach Tzedek began writing both Nigleh and Chassidus when he was twelve years old.

## 14 Nissan

In the Rebbe’s house, the custom is not to appoint the Rav as an agent (*morseh*) for the sale of *chametz*, but rather to sell the *chametz* to him outright, and to appoint him as a contractor (*kablan*).

When baking *shmurah* on the afternoon of Erev Pesach after midday, my father, the Rebbe, would be present. He would also recite Hallel, yet he would interrupt himself—even in the middle of a *perek*—to give instructions regarding the kneading, and even regarding matters of lesser importance.

## 15 Nissan, First Day of Pesach

For maror and chazeret: both horseradish and lettuce. Kiddush is recited while standing. In "Ha Lachma," the *ha* is vocalized with a *tzeirei*. When reciting the blessing for eating maror, one should also have in mind the maror in the korech sandwich.

At the first Seder, my father, the Rebbe [Rashab], would be brief in order to eat the afikoman before chatzos, but at the second Seder, he would be lengthy. He would begin before the ninth hour and conclude at three or four hours after chatzos, and he would elaborate extensively on the explanation of the Haggadah.

The Alter Rebbe said: The first night is *michla d’meheimanusa* (food of faith). The second night is *michla d’asusa* (food of healing). When healing brings about faith—meaning one says, "Thank You, G-d, for my healing," implying that one was previously sick—that is one thing. But when faith brings about the healing, one is, *lechatchilah*, not sick at all.

## 16 Nissan, 2nd Day of Chol Hamoed Pesach

On the second night of the Festival of Matzos, one recites the Shema of the Bedtime Shema just as on any other Yom Tov.

Even one who does not have a father, *r"l*, should say before *Mah Nishtanah*: "Tatty, I will ask you four questions." Similarly, even one who does not have a father or mother, *r"l*, should say during *Birkas Hamazon*: "May the Merciful One bless my father, my teacher... my mother, my teacher..."

## 17 Nissan, Second Day of the Omer

"This is praiseworthy" (*harei zeh meshubach*) — the word "this" (*zeh*) indicates a level of holiness, as in "Behold, this is our G-d" (*hinei Elokeinu zeh*). And regarding the statement "Whoever adds more... this is praiseworthy," the word "this" refers to that which is his own.

## 18 Nissan, Third Day of the Omer

In the year 5608 (1848), the Tzemach Tzedek delivered a maamar on the verse, "If you lend money to My people..." (Shemos 22:24). "Money" (kesef) refers to the neshamah, so named because it is always in a state of longing (kusef) and desire to ascend Above, as it is written, "The spirit of man is that which ascends Above" (Koheles 3:21). "Man" (adam) refers to the Jewish people, as it is said, "You are called 'adam'." The neshamah is given to man as a loan. It is written, "Days were fashioned..." (Tehillim 139:16), implying that there is a fixed number of days, etc., and if one day is missing, a garment is missing.

## 19 Nissan, Fourth Day of the Omer

Also with joy and gladness.
*Shalom Aleichem*, *Eshes Chayil*, *Mizmor L’Dovid*, *Da Hi Se'udata*, and *V’Yiten Lecha* are recited in a whisper.

Once, my revered father, the Rebbe, explained: "Mah Nishtana" — how is this night (the *galus* which is compared to night) different — "HaLayla HaZeh" (this final *galus*) — "MiKol HaLeilos" (from all the *galusim* that preceded it).

"SheB’Chol HaLeilos Ein Anu Matbilin" — *matbilin* refers to scouring, cleansing, and purification — "Afilu Pa'am Echas" — the purification was not completed in them, for there was another *galus* after them. "HaLayla HaZeh Shtei Pe'amim" — the purification of the body and the revelation of the *neshamah*.

"SheB’Chol HaLeilos Anu Ochlin Chametz O Matzah" — meaning that even after the *galus*, the *avodah* is from the *nefesh elokis* (matzah, which is *bitul*) and from the *nefesh habahamish* (chametz, which is *yeshus*). "HaLayla HaZeh" — after the final *galus* — "Kulo Matzah" — the spirit of impurity will be removed.

"SheB’Chol HaLeilos Anu Ochlin She'ar Yirakos" — whoever is envious, his face turns green (*yirakos*); in all the *galusim* there is much of the aspect of envy: the envy of scholars and the like. "HaLayla HaZeh" — after the final *galus* — "Kulo Maror" — the greatest envy, in the manner of our Sages of blessed memory: "Every *tzaddik* is scorched by the canopy of his fellow."

"SheB’Chol HaLeilos Anu Ochlin Bein Yoshvin U’Vein Mesubin" — all the revelations that come through the *galusim* — "ochlin" (eating) refers to the aspect of *taanug*. There is the diffusion of *taanug* and the essential *taanug*; there are those who reach the diffusion of *taanug* through their *avodah*, and those who reach the essential *taanug*. "HaLayla HaZeh" — after the final *galus* — "Kulanu Mesubin" — all of Israel will reach the level of essential *taanug*.

## 20 Nissan, Fifth Day of the Omer

Rabbi Chaim Avraham then related that the Alter Rebbe had said: "On Pesach, one should not pressure others to eat and drink, but one is permitted to take for oneself."

## 21 Nissan, Shevi'i Shel Pesach, 6th of Omer

In Lubavitch, it was customary to be present for Shevi'i Shel Pesach — as well as for Shavuos and Hoshana Rabbah. By the time I was nine years old, I no longer went to sleep on Shevi'i Shel Pesach — one must learn throughout the entire night.

## 22 Nissan, Acharon Shel Pesach, Seventh Day of the Omer

On this day, the custom is to make Kiddush, then daven Mincha, and afterwards partake in the Yom Tov meal.

The Baal Shem Tov would eat three meals on Acharon Shel Pesach.

The meal of Acharon Shel Pesach was called—by the Baal Shem Tov—Moshiach’s Seudah. Acharon Shel Pesach is Moshiach’s Seudah, because Acharon Shel Pesach is the revelation of the light of Moshiach.

In the year 5666, the custom began that the talmidim of Tomchei Temimim would eat together on Acharon Shel Pesach in the study hall. There were 310 talmidim and 18 tables. My father, the Rebbe, would eat the Acharon Shel Pesach meal together with the talmidim, and he instructed that each talmid be given four cups. He said then: "This is Moshiach’s Seudah."

## 23 Nissan, Isru Chag, Eighth Day of the Omer

There is the concept that Acharon Shel Pesach is the revelation of Moshiach, which is relevant to all of Yisrael. After all, Pesach is the time of "leaping" and the "night of guarding," and in general, it resonates with freedom; yet afterwards, one falls back into the world. This [revelation of Acharon Shel Pesach] is the revelation of Moshiach—the strength that it grants so that one is able to go out into the world.

## 24 Nissan, Ninth Day of the Omer

It is the custom not to recite the *Shehecheyanu* blessing during the days of the *Sefiras HaOmer*.

"Not good" (*lo tov*) we all have. The *sa'ir l'azazel* (scapegoat) was an *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash*. Since a created being has "not good," one must send it away to a "land of isolation" (*eretz gezeirah*).

## 25 Nissan, 10th day of the Omer

Avodah is incumbent upon every person according to their essence and stature. If someone has the ability to drill pearls or polish precious stones, yet occupies themselves with the work of baking bread—and this is understood as a metaphor for avodah—even though it is work and labor that is highly necessary, it is nevertheless considered a sin for that individual.

## 26 Nissan, 11 Omer

We bless the new month of Iyar. Recite the entire Tehillim at daybreak. A day for farbrengen.
Haftorah: "Are you not as the children of the Cushites..." (Amos 9:7).

From the sicha of my revered father, the Rebbe: Chassidus demands, "He shall wash his flesh in water and put them on" (Vayikra 16:24). That is, the *haskala* (intellectual comprehension) of Chassidus must wash away the *basar* (flesh), and rinse off the *es besaro*—the *tafel* (secondary/superfluous) aspects of the flesh—all the habits that the flesh creates. Only then can one put on the *bigdei kodesh* (holy garments). The thoughts and speech of Chassidus, and the conduct of Chassidim in *hisbonenus* (contemplation) before davening, are *bigdei kodesh*. These are garments granted from *HaKodesh* (the Holy), but the "washing of the flesh in water"—that, one must do oneself. The *levushei hanefesh* (garments of the soul) are given from Above, but washing away the *tafel* that stems from carnality, and making the flesh itself into *basar kodesh* (holy flesh)—that is only possible through one’s own strength. This is what Chassidus demands, and for this, our great Rebbe had *mesiras nefesh*. He opened the channel of *mesiras nefesh* for *avodas Hashem* in *tefillah*, binding oneself to *Atzmus Ein Sof*. Chassidus places a Chossid face-to-face with *Atzmus Ein Sof*.

## 27 Nissan, 12th of Omer

Things that are permitted, if one does them for one's own pleasure, are completely evil, as our Rebbe (the Alter Rebbe) wrote (Tanya, Chapter 7). Therefore, "sanctify yourself in that which is permitted to you." One must infuse holiness into permitted things so that they serve the purpose of Torah, mitzvos, yiras shamayim, and good character traits.

## 28 Nissan, 13th of Omer

The Chassidim asked the Alter Rebbe: Which avodah is higher, ahavas Hashem or ahavas Yisrael? He replied: Ahavas Hashem and ahavas Yisrael are both engraved as one in the neshamah, ru'ach, and nefesh of every single Yid. And the verse explicitly states, "I have loved you, says Hashem" (Malachi 1:2); thus, ahavas Yisrael is greater, for one loves what the Beloved loves.

## 29 Nissan, 14th of Omer

The introduction to Likutei Torah on the three instances of "to understand the matter of the animal soul, it is written 'you shall not eat'" is a maamar that the Alter Rebbe delivered before the Tzemach Tzedek, and the Tzemach Tzedek repeated it before him. The Alter Rebbe said: "Nu, the emphasis?" The Tzemach Tzedek then wrote down a transcript with emendations in brackets. The Rebbe emended the transcript and instructed that the emendations be incorporated into the body of the maamar. Based on the suggestion of my father, the Rebbe, this maamar was chosen as the introduction to Likutei Torah.

## 30 Nissan, Rosh Chodesh, 15th of Omer

The farbrengen of Seudah Shlishis, Shabbos Mevarchim, and special days—on Rosh Chodesh and the Yomim Tovim of Anash—must take place in the beis medrash. The Melaveh Malkah farbrengen should be held by Anash in their own private homes.

## Iyar

The month of Iyar is an acronym for the verse, "I am Hashem your Healer" (*Ani Hashem Rofecha*).

The *avodah* of the month of Iyar is the refinement of the *middos* (character traits). This follows the month of Nissan, which is the month of *geulah* (redemption) and the revelation of *Elokus* from above, which serves as the preparation for the *avodah* of refining the *middos* through one's own efforts.

The *avodah* of *Sefiras HaOmer*—which takes place during this month—is the process of refining the seven *middos*, each containing its own seven sub-aspects, totaling forty-nine. This is the preparation for receiving the Torah on Shavuos, which is the ultimate goal of the exodus from Mitzrayim.

## 1 Iyar, 2 Rosh Chodesh, 16th day of the Omer

Once, during a farbrengen in the years 5651–5653, it was said to my father: "The Alter Rebbe’s Chassidim used to always count [the days of the Omer]." My father was very pleased with this remark. He said: "This is the essence of avodah. The hours must be counted, and then the days are counted. When a day passes, one must know what one has accomplished and what one must do next... In general, one must ensure that tomorrow will be much more beautiful than today."

## 2 Iyar, 17th Day of the Omer

The birthday of my grandfather, the Rebbe [the Tzemach Tzedek] — in the year 5593.

When he was seven years old, the Tzemach Tzedek once examined him. The grandfather [the Mitteler Rebbe] performed so well that his melamed was deeply impressed; he could not contain himself and said to the Tzemach Tzedek, "What do you say? He is doing well." The Tzemach Tzedek replied to him, "Why the hispaalus? It is Tiferes sheb'Tiferes doing well."

## 3 Iyar, 18th of Omer

One does not drink water before Havdalah.

All *midos*—even those that are not good, or are outright evil by their description and name—can be utilized for the *avodah* of Hashem according to the Torah. Just as the *tzaddik* Rabbi Meshulam Zusya of Anipoli, *ztz"l*, learned several methods for *avodah* from a thief: 1) He acts in secrecy; 2) He puts himself in danger; 3) He considers even the smallest thing as something significant; 4) He exerts great effort; 5) He is swift; 6) He is confident and hopeful; 7) If he does not succeed the first time, he tries many more times.

## 4 Iyar, 19th of Omer

It is a Jewish custom not to shave or cut a young boy’s hair until he reaches the age of three.
The cutting of the hair—the *upsherinish*—is a significant practice among Jewish customs. Its primary purpose is the chinuch of leaving the *peios* of the head. From the day of the haircut and the establishment of the *peios*, it is customary to be diligent in accustoming the child to wearing *tzitzis katan*, reciting *birchos hashachar*, *birchas hamazon*, and *krias shema* before retiring to bed.

## 5 Iyar, 20 Iyar

The Alter Rebbe received a tradition from R' Mordechai the Tzaddik, who heard it from the Baal Shem Tov: A neshamah descends to this world and lives for seventy or eighty years for the sole purpose of doing a favor for another Yid—in material matters, and especially in spiritual matters.

## 6 Iyar, 21st Day of the Omer

Our Sages have said: A person should only take leave of his fellow with a matter of Halacha. Our holy Rebbes, the leaders of our people, explained this to mean: a *dvar Torah* that makes him—the listener—into a *mehalech* (one who walks). The concept of walking is to ascend from level to level, in a process of continuous elevation. This is the superiority of the *neshamos* over the *malachim*; this ascent is achieved primarily through practical *avodah*—doing a favor for a fellow Jew, in material matters generally, and in spiritual matters specifically.

## 7 Iyar, 22nd of Omer

When wrapping the tallis gadol, one does not need to cover the head down to the mouth, as is written in the Laws of Tzitzis in the Siddur. However, the custom is to also cover the eyes with the upper part of the tallis gadol.

The custom during the days of Sefirah is to study Masechta Sotah—in addition to one's regular shiurim—at a rate of one daf per day.

## 8 Iyar, 23rd of Omer

A shaliach is one and the same with the one who sent him—similar to the concept explained regarding an angel being called by Hashem’s own Name when it acts as a messenger from Above, and all the more so regarding neshamas, where it is explained that the level is even higher.

And Chassidim are shluchim of the Rebbe, of the Alter Rebbe. So when one acts [as a shaliach], one is mekushar, and at that moment he is connected in everything: a Chossid walks, a Chossid eats, a Chossid sleeps.

## 9 Iyar, 24th of Omer

The Baal Shem Tov said: Everything a person sees or hears is a directive for how to conduct oneself in the avodah of Hashem. This is the essence of avodah: to understand and internalize how every experience serves as a path in the avodah of Hashem.

## 10 Iyar, 25 Omer

It is customary to dip the *Hamotzi* slice three times into salt, and not to throw the salt onto the slice.

On the second day of *Chol HaMoed* 5621, during the meal, the Tzemach Tzedek related: On the second day of *Chol HaMoed* 5595, during the meal, my grandfather (the Alter Rebbe) said: On the second day of *Chol HaMoed* 5565, during the meal, my master and teacher (the Maggid of Mezeritch) said: "And you shall count for yourselves" (*U'sefartem lachem*); *u'sefartem* is related to the word *sefiros* (luminous emanations) and *behirut* (clarity). "And you shall count for yourselves"—you must ensure that your *lachem* (yourselves) becomes clear and luminous.

My grandfather then leaned on his holy arms and sang the *nigun* of *Arba Bavos* with great *dveikus*. Afterward, he raised his holy head and asked—in a questioning *nigun*: "And with what does one count the *lachem*?" And immediately, in the same breath, he answered—in a responding *nigun*: "With 'seven complete weeks'—by clarifying the seven *midos*, such that each and every *midah* is comprised of seven, and the seven *midos* themselves become seven *shabbosos*, for *Shabbos* requires no clarification."

## 11 Iyar, 26 Omer

In our davening, we say:
*V’chen* — with a *tzeirei* and not a *patach* — *shnei kesuvim*.
*Chiyisani* — with a *kamatz* and not a *patach* — *mi’yordei bor*.
*Zecher* — with a *zayin* under a *segol* and not a *tzeirei* — *rav tuvecha*.
*Mishaliach korcho* — with a *patach* and not a *kamatz* — *kaptim*.
*U’malchuso v’emunaso la’ad kayemes.*

## 12 Iyar, 27th Day of the Omer

In davening, we say: *M’nu* (from the phrase *m’nu ma’amar*) — the *mem* is vocalized with a *kamatz*. (As in, "Today, etc.")

*Ne’emar* — the *mem* is vocalized with a *patach*.

*Zechor* — with a *cholem* — "For we are dust."

*V’nitaltani* — the *tes* is vocalized with a *patach*, the *lamed* with a *shva*, and the *tav* is doubled with a *dagesh* and vocalized with a *patach*.

*V’ahavatcha lo tasur* — and not *v’al tasir*.

## 13 Iyar, 28th Day of the Omer

At Mincha, Tachanun is recited.

The Alter Rebbe — during those years when he would recite short maamarim — said: "Know what is above you" (*Da mah l'ma'alah mimcha*); know that everything that exists above, in the *partzufim* and the supernal *sefiros*, is all from you—meaning, it is dependent upon the *avodah* of man.

## 14 Iyar, 29th of Omer

The concept of Pesach Sheni is that there is no such thing as "lost"—one can always rectify. Even one who was *tamei* (ritually impure), or one who was on a distant journey. And even in the case of "for yourselves"—where the situation was brought about by one's own will—one can still rectify it.

## 15 Iyar, 30th Day of the Omer

In the days of the Alter Rebbe, the following saying was common among the Chassidim: "The piece of bread that I have is yours as much as it is mine." And they would place the word "yours" first—"yours as much as mine."

## 16 Iyar

When my father, the Rebbe, would cut his fingernails, he would mix them with a wooden splinter before burning them.

My grandfather, the Rebbe Maharash, once said to the Chassid R' Eliyahu Abeler—a man of simple standing in terms of his talents and knowledge—when he entered for *yechidus*: "Eliyahu, I envy you. You travel to the marketplace and see many people. When, in the midst of business, you speak with someone about a *Yiddishe* word, a word of *Ein Yaakov*, and you inspire them to study *nigleh* and *Chassidus*, this creates *simcha* Above. And the Ribono shel Olam pays for that brokerage with children, life, and sustenance. And the larger the marketplace and the more work there is, the greater the *parnassah*."

## 17 Iyar, 32nd Day of the Omer

We do not say *Tzidkascha*.

In the *Krias Shema* that some recite in the morning—before *davening*—in order to fulfill the obligation of *Krias Shema* at its proper time, one also repeats the three words [Hashem Elokeichem Emes], and concludes with the word *Emes*. In the *Krias Shema* of *Tefillin d’Rabbenu Tam* and *Shema al Hamitah*, one does not repeat the words, but one does say *Emes*.

On *Lag B’Omer* of the year 5604—approximately—the *Tzemach Tzedek* recounted a teaching of the *Baal Shem Tov*: It is written, "For you shall be a land of desire, says Hashem Tzeva-os." Just as the greatest sages will never fully fathom the vast, natural treasures that the *Ribono shel Olam* has embedded within the earth—for everything came from the dust—so too, no one can fathom the great treasures that lie within *Yidden*, who are the *Ribono shel Olam*’s "land of desire." The *Baal Shem Tov* concluded: "I want to make of *Yidden* that they should yield the produce that the *Ribono shel Olam*’s 'land of desire' is capable of yielding."

## 18 Iyar, Lag B'Omer

For the Mitteler Rebbe, Lag B'Omer was one of the most significant Yomim Tovim. They would go out into the field; he would not wash [for a meal], but he would partake of mashkeh, even though, for health reasons, he was not supposed to. Many mofsim were seen at that time. The majority of these mofsim concerned children. And all year long, people would wait for Lag B'Omer.

## 19 Iyar, 34th Day of the Omer

Chassidus is a Divine intellect—the intellect that shows a person how small he is, and how great he can become.

## 20 Iyar, 35th Day of the Omer

There is no one from whom to take over [the responsibility of your own avodah]. One must toil; with patience and a conciliatory approach, one can, with the help of Hashem, accomplish everything. Through belittling others and aggrandizing oneself, one, Heaven forbid, loses everything.

## 21 Iyar, 36th day of the Omer

Every single person must know that Hashem, in His Divine Providence, grants every individual the opportunity to bring the *Ratzon HaElyon* (the Supreme Will) from potential into actuality, through the performance of *mitzvos* and the strengthening of Yiddishkeit and our holy Torah at all times and in every place. This depends solely upon one’s own *avodah*.

## 22 Iyar, 37th Day of the Omer

Once, a group of early Chassidim gathered for a farbrengen—in the year 5544-5547—and the substance of their conversation was this: The Rebbe—our Alter Rebbe—accomplished the fact that one is not alone. In the past, the Rebbe—the Rosh Yeshiva and Gaon—was alone, and the students were alone. The path of Chassidus that the Rebbe founded is the great Divine accomplishment: that the Rebbe is not alone, and Chassidim are not alone.

## 23 Iyar, 38th Day of the Omer

The beginning of the descent, G-d forbid, is the lack of avodah in tefillah. Everything becomes dry and cold; even the performance of mitzvos as a matter of rote habit becomes difficult. One rushes, one loses the geshmak in Torah, and the atmosphere becomes coarsened. It is understood that such a person is completely unable to influence others.

## 24 Iyar, 38th Day of the Omer

We bless the new month of Sivan. It is customary to recite the entire Tehillim at daybreak. It is a day for farbrengen. For the third aliyah, the Baal Koreh is called up, and he does so on his own. We recite Av Harachamim.

"If you walk in My statutes" (Im bechukotai teilechu) — the word "if" (im) is an expression of supplication, as our Sages of blessed memory have said, meaning "Would that you walk in My statutes." The fact that Hashem, so to speak, pleads with them to keep the Torah itself assists and empowers a person to stand firm in his good choice. Furthermore, "you walk in My statutes" implies that the neshamah becomes a "walker" (one who progresses).

With the coming of Moshiach, the virtue of the simplicity and sincerity found in the avodah of simple people—who daven and recite Tehillim with sincerity—will be revealed.

## 25 Iyar, 40th Day of the Omer

From the maamarim of the Alter Rebbe in the year 5555 in Liozna: "The reward of a mitzvah is the mitzvah itself"—the mitzvah in its very essence is the reward, and the revelation of that essence will be in the future. However, this is the *keren kayemes* (the principal) of the mitzvah; the "fruits," however, a person consumes in this world. This applies to each and every mitzvah according to its nature: when one is in need of that specific thing, he is answered.

## 26 Iyar, 41st Day of the Omer

Before the commandment "You shall surely rebuke your neighbor" (*Vayikra 19:17*), it is written, "You shall not hate your brother in your heart," for this is a prerequisite for rebuke. And immediately afterward it is written, "And you shall not bear sin because of him," implying that if the rebuke did not succeed, you are certainly to blame, for the words did not emerge from the heart.

## 27 Iyar, 42nd day of the Omer

My father, the Rebbe, related that he heard from his father in the name of the Tzemach Tzedek, who heard from Rabbeinu HaZaken, that he would refer to himself as a son to the Maggid and as a grandson to the Baal Shem Tov.

## 28 Iyar, 43rd Day of the Omer

In one of the nightly visions in which the Tzemach Tzedek saw the Alter Rebbe, within thirty days of his passing, the Alter Rebbe recited before him the maamar on the three things upon which the world stands. After the maamar, he said to him: "If the man emits seed first, she bears a female — this is your mother. If the woman emits seed first, she bears a male — this is you."

## 29 Iyar, 44th day of the Omer

The ascent of the neshamah occurs three times every day during the three tefillos. This is especially true for the neshamah of the tzaddikim who go "from strength to strength." It is a certainty that at all times and in every holy place where they are, they carry a song and prayer on behalf of those who are connected to them, those who are mindful of their instructions, and those who observe their directives—and especially on behalf of their students and their students' students—that they be saved both materially and spiritually.

## Sivan

The month of Sivan is the month of the giving of the Torah. The service of the month of Sivan is to draw down the light of the Torah into the world, specifically into the realm of the mundane, so that the world itself becomes a dwelling place for Hashem. This is achieved through the study of Torah and the performance of mitzvos with bitul, thereby revealing that the essence of the Torah is not merely a set of instructions, but the very wisdom and will of the Ein Sof, which transcends the limitations of Seder Hishtalshelus. Through this avodah, one transforms the darkness of the world into light, bringing about the revelation of the ultimate purpose of creation.

## 1 Sivan, 46th day of the Omer, Rosh Chodesh

"A branch follows its root." Our holy Rebbes, our forefathers, bequeathed an inheritance without boundaries to the early Chassidim. Their descendants, sons and daughters throughout the generations—in whatever country or environment they may find themselves—should possess that same "root": the drawing down of the inner heart toward the Rock from which it was hewn. Sometimes this root is covered and hidden by many garments. It is the task of one who desires life to remove these coverings, to set aside fixed times for the study of Chassidus, and to conduct oneself according to the customs of the Chassidic community.

## 2 Sivan, 46th Day of the Omer

The *Ana B'choach* of *Kabbalas Shabbos* is recited in a whisper.
*U'fros Aleinu* of *Kabbalas Shabbos* is recited while standing.

In the year 5589 (1829), *Parshas Bamidbar* fell on 5 Sivan. Before lighting the Shabbos candles, the Tzemach Tzedek recited a *maamar* on the verse, "Take the sum (*seu es rosh*) of all the congregation of the children of Israel, by their families..." He explained the word *seu* according to the commentary of the Ibn Ezra, as being related to the phrase "when you take the sum (*ki sisa es rosh*)." The explanation of the matter is that through the *avodah* of illuminating the *neshamah* within the body, an elevation is achieved in the "head"—the essence of the *neshamah* as it is above. He explained several verses and passages from the Zohar and Midrash.

On Shabbos afternoon, our Rebbe recited the *drush* "And I will betroth you to Me forever," and its explanation, which are printed in *Likutei Torah*.

On the first day of *Chag HaShavuos*, our Rebbe recited the *drush* "And you shall count for yourselves," and its explanation, which are printed in *Likutei Torah*.

On the second day of *Chag HaShavuos*, at the *Yom Tov* meal, our Rebbe recited the *drush* "But wisdom, where shall it be found," which is the second explanation of the subject of "you shall count fifty days," printed in *Likutei Torah*.

## 3 Sivan, 47th Day of the Omer

When people would get a haircut during the Yemei Hagbalah, before Shavuos, my father, the Rebbe, was not pleased with this.

One who studies the Tosafos in the beginning of the entry "Torah" (Shabbos 89b) will understand that Shavuos is a time of *eis ratzon* Above, and Hashem occupies the *mekatreg* (accuser) so that he cannot speak against the Jewish people, similar to how He occupies him during the *tekiyos* on Rosh Hashanah and on the holy day of Yom Kippur.

## 4 Sivan, 48th Day of the Omer

The days of Shavuos are a time uniquely suited for accomplishing everything regarding the study of Torah and avodah in yiras shamayim, as well as for engaging in teshuvah concerning Torah without interference from the prosecuting Satan, similar to the time of the tekios on Rosh Hashanah and the holy day of Yom Kippur.

## 5 Sivan, Erev Shavuos, 49th Day of the Omer

On Erev Shavuos 5557, the Alter Rebbe said a Torah discourse: "And you shall sanctify them today and tomorrow..." (Shemos 19:10). "And you shall sanctify them today and tomorrow" comes from Above, but "and they shall wash their garments"—that, one must do oneself. This was the entire discourse. The Tzemach Tzedek explained: "And you shall sanctify them" was said to Moshe, and [it applies to] the *atpashuta d'Moshe* (the extension of Moshe) in every generation, who are able to make "today" and "tomorrow" holy. However, for this, one requires "and they shall wash their garments"—the garments of thought, speech, and action—and that, one must do oneself.

## 6 Sivan, First Day of Yom Tov

During the reading of the Aseres HaDibros, one stands facing the Sefer Torah; we do not recite Akdamus.

The histalkus of the Baal Shem Tov occurred on the fourth day of the week, the first day of Yom Tov, 5520, and he is buried in Mezhibuzh. Regarding this, the Alter Rebbe said—on Wednesday, 20 Kislev 5559, in Petersburg—"On the fourth day, the luminaries were taken."

## 7 Sivan, 2nd Day of Chol HaMoed

**Priestly Blessing (Nesias Kapayim):** When saying the word *Yevarechecha*, the person receiving the blessing keeps their head facing forward. At *Hashem*, they turn their head to their right, which is the left side of the Kohen. At *Veyishmerecha*, they face forward. At *Ya'er*, they turn to their left, which is the right side of the Kohen, and so on. At *Shalom*, they face forward. The *Ribono shel Olam* prayer should be recited specifically while the Kohanim are chanting the melody; however, when they are chanting the actual words of the blessing, one must listen. When they chant the melody for *Veyasem*, one recites *Ribono shel Olam... HaTzaddik*. When they chant the melody for the word *Lecha*, one recites *Ve'im... Elisha*. For *Shalom*, one recites *U'k'shem... LeTovah*. When the Kohanim say *Shalom*, one recites *V'tishmereini V'techuneni V'tirtzeni*. *Addir BaMarom* is recited after answering "Amen," while the Tallis is still covering one's face.

## 8 Sivan, Isru Chag

This physical world is a mixture of *ginunim* of the Ribono shel Olam (a gathering where Hashem, *kivayachol*, comes together with man, the choicest of creations) and *ginat egoz* (which has the same gematria as *chet*). Hashem grants man *bechirah chofshis*, so that a person can choose his own path in life.

## 9 Sivan

The world requires *taharas ha-avir* (purity of the atmosphere). This *taharas ha-avir* is achieved only through the *osiyos* (letters) of the Torah. The *osiyos* in the Torah serve as both a *shemirah klalis* (general protection) and a *shemirah pratis* (individual protection). The *chalukas hashta* (this year’s division) of *Sidrei Mishnah* (the orders of the Mishnah) is a way of fulfilling "and when you walk on the way." The *Mishnah* that one reviews wherever one may be—in whatever place one finds oneself—will illuminate the connection between *Yisrael* and *Kudsha Brich Hu*. *Mishnah* is *osiyos neshamah* (the letters of the soul). It is very difficult to find the words to express the immense benefit that will, with the help of *Hashem*, result from the *shemirah klalis* and *shemirah pratis* that the constant reviewing of *Mishnayos* will bring. Furthermore, words are lacking to describe the great *nachas ruach* that one will provide to the *Borei Olam*, *Baruch Hu*.

## 10 Sivan

The Alter Rebbe once replied to someone entering for yechidus: "...Bnei Yisrael are called 'candles' (neiros). A candle consists of a vessel, a wick, oil, and fire. ## 11 Sivan

From the sichos of my father, the Rebbe: In the avodah of serving Hashem according to the path of Chassidus, there are all levels... The level of a *mes* (a dead person) does not require much explanation. However, there is also—thank G-d—*techiyas hameisim* (resurrection of the dead) in spiritual avodah. A *mes* is cold; there is nothing as cold as *sechel hativ'i* (natural intellect) and *sechel enoshi* (human intellect). Yet, when this *sechel hativ'i* grasps an *hasagah Elokis* (Divine comprehension), and the *middos* within the *sechel* become *nisp'al* (aroused) and stirred by this intellectual delight, that is the true *techiyas hameisim*.

## 15 Sivan

Initially, the Alter Rebbe would deliver very short maamarim that were deeply stirring and highly inspiring; these were called "derachim" (ways). Later, they were called "igros" (letters), which were longer. Subsequently, they evolved into what were called "toros" (teachings), which are the roots of the maamarim found in Torah Or and Likutei Torah. Later still, they became somewhat longer and were called "kesavim" (writings), which provided explanations with broader conceptual depth, commensurate with the subject matter.

## 16 Sivan

The early sages, who were like angels, of blessed memory, already determined that the healing of the soul is like the healing of the body.

First and foremost, one must identify the site of the illness: whether it stems from the fact that the material of one’s body is coarse, thick, and corrupt; or whether the deficiency lies in the powers of the soul, which has inclinations toward negative matters such as pride, falsehood, and the like; or whether the source of the illness is habit, where, due to poor upbringing or a negative environment, one has acquired bad habits.

Until the details of the site of the illness and the cause of the affliction are clarified, it is impossible to begin its cure, other than by establishing a path of upright conduct in all matters—what to do and what to refrain from: in "do good" through the fulfillment of mitzvos, setting fixed times for Torah study, and acquiring good character traits; and in "turn from evil." However, what is most necessary is that the patient himself must awaken two things: 1) To know that he is ill, and to yearn and long to be healed of his illness. 2) The knowledge that he can be healed, and the hope and absolute trust that, with the help of Hashem, he will be healed of his illness.

Not deriving pleasure from Olam Hazeh in the full sense is merely good preparation for avodah. The essence of avodah is to make the physical into a vessel for Elokus.

## 23 Sivan

The response of my grandfather, the Rebbe [Maharash], to my father, the Rebbe [Rashab], during a yechidus in the winter of 5635 (1875): The *nefesh habehamis* is called a "beast" not because it is necessarily a literal animal; sometimes it is a fox, the craftiest of beasts, and one requires great wisdom to understand its schemes. At other times, it cloaks itself in the guise of a *tzaddik tamim*—humble and possessing fine character traits.

In every individual, their *nefesh habehamis* corresponds to their own specific nature. There are times when a great desire awakens to study Chassidus or to contemplate deeply some intellectual concept, yet in truth, this is merely a ruse of the *yetzer* and one of the schemes of the *nefesh habehamis* to prevent one from the *avodah* of *tefillah*, and the like.

Take this rule into your hand and remember it always: any matter that is beneficial or leads to *avodah* in practice—any obstacle that arises to this matter, even if the obstacle is in the name of something most sublime, is only one of the schemes of the *nefesh habehamis*.

And my father, the Rebbe [Rashab], concluded: Until then, I did not know that there could be a pious *nefesh habehamis*, let alone a Chassidic *nefesh habehamis*.

## 26 Sivan

"And they went down alive into the grave" — meaning that even in the grave, they consider themselves alive.

The blessing in "and they went down alive into the grave," as with the sons of Korach who did not die, is that a place was secured for them and they performed teshuvah. ## 27 Sivan

It is written, "And Hashem your G-d will bless you in all that you do" (Devarim 15:18). A person’s only obligation is to create a vessel for his livelihood and to exert all his strength to ensure that this vessel is pure, free from any dross or impurity of dishonesty, etc. This means that everything must be conducted according to the laws of the Torah. When this is the case, it becomes a vessel worthy of the Heavenly blessing in two ways: that the livelihood comes with abundance, and that the livelihood is channeled to the appropriate place.

## 6 Tammuz

The mitzvah is drawn down through the *makif* (encompassing light) that is inherent in the *mitzvos* themselves. The mitzvah of *tzedakah*, for example, is a comprehensive mitzvah, such that all *mitzvos* are referred to as *tzedakah*. For this reason, it is highly appropriate to give a *prutah* to *tzedakah* before every mitzvah, as this draws the *makif* into the *pnimiyus* (internal dimension). Nevertheless, this is a "near" *makif*, unlike Torah, which is a "distant" *makif*, and its effect is on a higher level.

My revered father and teacher said: The beginning of Chassidus is rooted in its end, and its end in its beginning—the level of *igulim* (circles) where no beginning or end is found. Yet, after all this, the *ikar* (essential point) is Torah.

## 17 Tammuz

A fast day, Selichos, Avinu Malkeinu.

Regarding the distinction between the first Tablets and the second Tablets:

In the Tablets themselves: The first were the work of Elokus; the second were "Carve for yourself" (*Pesal lecha*). As for the script—"Engraved upon the Tablets" (*Charut al haluchos*), which our Sages of blessed memory explain in Eiruvin (54a)—this applies only to the first Tablets.

Regarding the stature of Bnei Yisrael: With the first Tablets, they were *tzaddikim*; when they stood at Har Sinai, their spiritual impurity (*zohaman*) ceased. With the second Tablets, they were *baalei teshuvah*.

Regarding the stature of Moshe: At the giving of the Torah, Moshe was gifted one thousand lights. Through the sin of the golden calf, these were taken from him and were not returned with the second Tablets (they are only granted to him on Shabbos, as is explained in *Pri Etz Chaim*).

The virtue of the second Tablets is that they also included the *halachos*, *midrash*, and *aggados*, etc.—"double in wisdom" (*kiflayim l'tushiyah*), as is explained in Nedarim (22b)—and the radiance of the skin of Moshe’s face.

## 20 Tammuz

There are three types of *hisbonenus*: 

1) Intellectual *hisbonenus*: After understanding a concept thoroughly, one contemplates the depth of that matter until the intellectual content becomes illuminated within one’s mind. 

2) The *hisbonenus* that precedes *davening*: Its purpose is to feel the vitality of the subject one has studied, rather than the intellectual grasp of it as in the first type. 

3) The *hisbonenus* during *davening*: Its purpose is to feel the *Elokus* within the subject one has studied. 

These three are the rungs of the ladder of *hargashah* (feeling). It is only through the kindness of the *Ribono shel Olam* toward us that we sometimes feel—*derhert men G-tlichkeit* (one perceives Divinity)—without any *avodah* at all. This is due to the inherent greatness of the *atzmus* (essence) that exists within the *neshamah*. However, in terms of the *avodah* that one must perform through one’s own strength, these three stages are mandatory.

## 21 Tammuz

We do not recite the *Shehecheyanu* blessing during the *Bein HaMetzarim* period, not even on *Shabbos*.

It is written, "And they shall make Me a sanctuary, and I will dwell among them" (*Shemos 25:8*)—"among them" (*betocham*) meaning within each and every Jew. For within every Jew, the innermost point of their heart is a sanctuary for Him, *yisbarach*, to dwell. Now, the site of the *Beis HaMikdash* remains holy even during the time of exile and desolation. As it is stated in *Shemos Rabbah* (ch. 2), "Rabbi Acha said: The *Shechinah* never departs from the Western Wall." The entire state of desolation applies only to the structures. This is likewise true regarding the personal sanctuary within each and every Jew: the foundation remains whole, clear, and pure. As it is written, "I sleep, but my heart is awake" (*Shir HaShirim 5:2*). The *Midrash Rabbah* explains: "I sleep from the *mitzvos*, but my heart is awake for *gemilus chasadim*; I sleep from *tzedakah*, but my heart is awake to perform them." Thus, any state of desolation, *rachmana litzlan*, that may exist within a Jew is only comparable to the structures outside the foundation; the foundation of the personal sanctuary remains in its holiness.

## 25 Tammuz

In Chassidus, there are two very characteristic expressions: 1) A Yid recognizes Elokus and feels its supernatural nature, and requires no proofs for this. 2) A Yid neither wants to be, nor can he be, severed from Elokus.

The truth is that these two expressions are one and the same: because a Yid recognizes Elokus and feels that which is *lema'alah min hateva*, therefore he neither wants to be, nor can he be, severed from Elokus.

## Menachem Av

The month of Av is called "Menachem Av." The name implies that the consolation (nechamah) is already contained within the month itself. This is similar to the concept of "the cure precedes the illness." The very essence of the month contains the power of the consolation, which will be revealed in its entirety with the coming of Moshiach, may he arrive speedily in our days.

## 1 Menachem Av, Rosh Chodesh

The greatness of Moshiach is that he will be humble. Even though he will be at the pinnacle of greatness, and will study Torah with the Patriarchs and Moshe Rabbeinu, he will nevertheless possess the ultimate humility and bitul, such that he will also study with simple people.

## 2 Menachem Av

A directive for the public: One should begin wearing tefillin two months before the Bar Mitzvah. At first, this is done without a blessing, and after a few weeks, with a blessing.

## 3 Menachem Av

From a sicha of my father, the Rebbe: When Moshiach comes, may it be speedily in our days, only then will we yearn for the days of golus. Only then will we regret why we did not engage in avodah. Only then will we truly perceive the great pain of the lack of avodah. Now, in these days of golus, are the days of avodah, to prepare ourselves for the coming of Moshiach, may it be speedily in our days.

## 4 Menachem Av

With the greatest of toil, one cannot earn a single cent more than what Hashem has decreed that a specific person should earn. One must do as much as is necessary, but one must remember that all the work is nothing more than a secondary means; the primary factor is the blessing of Hashem. And one earns that blessing by being an ehrlicha Yid: tefillah b’tzibur, meticulous shmiras Shabbos, kashrus with strict hashgachah, and chinuch habanim by hiring ehrlicha Yidden as melamdim.

## 5 Menachem Av

"Turn away from evil and do good; seek peace and pursue it" (*Tehillim 34:15*). The Baal Shem Tov said: In every physical thing among the permitted matters, there is both *tov* and *ra*. The physical aspect is *ra*, and the *Chayus Eloki* that gives life to that physical thing is *tov*. A person who utilizes the physical must therefore be a "turn away from evil"—not desiring the *taanug* that exists within the physical—and a "do good"—desiring to be nourished and sustained by the *Chayus Eloki* that is within that physical thing. "Seek peace and pursue it"—a person who is a "turn away from evil" and a "do good" must seek and pursue making peace between the physical and the *Chayus Eloki* that gives it life.

## 20 Menachem Av

Just as the mitzvah of tefillin, for example, has a fixed place on the head and the arm—where one feels the weight of the head-tefillin and the constriction of the hand-tefillin—so too is it with the mitzvah of ahavah and yirah. The measure of these mitzvos is that they must be felt with a physical sensation, literally in the flesh of the heart. It is like a person meeting their true, loyal friend. And not only does one feel good from this—to the point that they forget all the things that weigh upon them—but furthermore, an inner vitality is awakened within them, filled with good hope, because their heart is gladdened. Similarly, regarding the attribute of yirah: a great fear and awe falls upon them, for in that moment they recall all their unfavorable matters in thought, speech, and action. Their heart aches with a palpable pain from the fear of punishment—fear of Heaven—and at times it is a feeling of the fear of shame, or even the fear of transcendence.

## 27 Menachem Av

We bless the new month of Elul. It is customary to recite the entire Tehillim early in the morning. It is a day of farbrengen.

The month of Elul is the month of *cheshbon* (accounting). Just as in material matters, a businessman—in order for his business to be conducted properly and to yield a significant profit—must from time to time make an accounting and rectify all deficiencies, so too is it in spiritual *avodah*, in the service of Hashem. Throughout the entire year, all of Klal Yisrael are occupied with Torah, *mitzvos*, and refined *midos*. The month of Elul is the month of *cheshbon*, when every single Jew—each according to his own measure, whether he is a *yoshev ohel* (one who dwells in the tent of Torah) or a businessman—must make a true accounting in his soul of everything that has passed over him during the year. He must identify the strengths in his *avodah* and reinforce them, and identify the deficiencies in himself and his *avodah* and rectify them. For through this proper preparation, one merits a good and sweet year, both materially and spiritually.

## 2 Elul

The Jewish people are called *Eretz Chefetz* (a land of desire), for within them are many precious desires for the love and fear of Hashem, and for good *midos*. The revelation of these good *midos* depends solely upon the one who awakens them. It is a clear fact that in every part of the earth, springs of living water can be found; the only difference lies in their proximity to the surface. Thus, everything depends upon the one who digs, and upon his power of patience and moderation.

Since the *ratzon* (will) is the supreme power that commands and directs all other faculties, compelling them to act and perform according to its dictates, it follows that the essence of *avodah* is to awaken the *ratzon* to act and perform—both within oneself and within others.

## 3 Elul

Likewise, that very neshamah, from the moment it was emanated and created, has been waiting for the time of its descent to clarify and rectify that which was placed upon it.

## 4 Elul

One turns their face slightly to the right and recites the blessing. One then opens the Torah scroll and reads.

Regarding the excellence of the human species, there are four designations: *Adam*—the excellence of the intellect and the mind; *Ish*—the excellence of the heart and the *midos*; *Enosh*—a weakness in one of these, either in the intellect or in the *midos*, or in both; *Gever*—one who overcomes themselves to remove the obstacles and hindrances to attaining one of these levels, whether in intellect or in *midos*. That is to say, the *Gever* engages with the *Enosh* to elevate them to the level of *Ish* or *Adam*.

And since it is possible to transform an *Enosh* into an *Ish* or an *Adam*, it must necessarily be that even within the *Enosh* there exist all those excellences that are found in the *Ish* and also in the *Adam*.

When Reb Moshe, son of the Alter Rebbe, was studying the *sugya* regarding the praise of the Sages in *Gittin* (67a) — he was then about eight to eleven years old — he was puzzled by the statement of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai there: "They taught me my *midos* (character traits/measures) according to their literal meaning." In the midst of this, the Alter Rebbe entered and said, in a *nigun* as was his holy custom: "The Torah that was given to us is entirely *midos tovos* (good character traits). Even the punishments within it are, in truth, *chesed* and good. Both interpretations are one, and the one is dependent upon the other: it is impossible to have *midos tovos* without Torah, and it is impossible to have Torah without *midos tovos*." The same incident occurred to the Tzemach Tzedek when he was eight to eleven years old.

## 25 Elul

Recite the entire Tehillim early in the morning.
It is a day of farbrengen. Selichos are recited after chatzos halailah and close to it, whereas on other days they are recited during the early morning watch (*ashmores haboker*).
When the parshiyos of Nitzavim and Vayelech are read separately, one begins at *maftir*; the same applies to the seventh aliyah of Shabbos Parshas Nitzavim, [beginning with the words] *Re’eh nasati lefanecha*, etc.

The Alter Rebbe related: While I was in Mezritch, I heard from my teacher and Rebbe, the Maggid, in the name of his teacher and Rebbe, the Baal Shem Tov: The seventh month—which is the first of the months of the year—is blessed by the Ribono shel Olam Himself on Shabbos Mevarchim, which is the final Shabbos of the month of Elul. Through this power, Yisrael blesses the months eleven times throughout the year. It is written, "You are standing today" (*Atem nitzavim hayom*); "today" refers to Rosh Hashanah, which is the Day of Judgment, as it is written, "And it was the day," which the Targum renders as "the day of the great judgment." "You are standing" (*nitzavim*) means you are established and firm—meaning that you are victorious in the judgment. On the Shabbos before Rosh Hashanah, which is the final Shabbos of the month of Elul, we read Parshas *Atem Nitzavim*; this is the blessing of the Ribono shel Olam on Shabbos Mevarchim for the seventh month, which is "the satiated one" (*hamushba*) and "the one that satiates" (*hamashbia*) with an abundance of goodness for all of Yisrael for the entire year.

## Tishrei

The month of Tishrei is the "head" of all the months of the year. Just as the head contains the life-force for all the limbs of the body, so too does Tishrei contain the life-force for the entire year. Through the avodah of the Yomim Tovim of Tishrei—Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Sukkos, and Simchas Torah—we draw down the light and life-force for all the days of the year that follow.

## 1 Tishrei, 1st Day of Rosh Hashanah

The blessing for lighting the candles: *Shel Yom HaZikaron*. We recite *Shehecheyanu*. We do not repeat the word *le'eila* in Kaddish, except during *Ne'ilah*. *LeDovid... Ha'aretz u'mlo'ah...* is recited before *Aleinu*. In *Kiddush HaYom*: *Tik'u* and *Ki Chok*. We do not recite *Eileh Mo'adei* or *V'yidabeir*. For *Tashlich*: we shake the corners of the *tallis katan*.

The *Yehi Ratzon* for the apple is recited after the blessing and before eating. We eat the head of a ram, but we do not recite *Yehi Ratzon* except over the apple.

## 2 Tishrei, 2nd Day of Rosh Hashanah

"La-el Orech Din" is recited during the Musaf Amidah.
At Mincha, "Hodu" is not recited, but "Posa'ach Eliyahu" is said.

Regarding what is written in the Siddur Shaar HaKollel (page 71a), in the discourse titled "Lehavin HaHeferesh," that one recites "Ana B'choach" before the blowing of the Shofar: the meaning is that these are generally the kavanos derived from the verses whose acronyms form the Names of the forty-two-letter Name. However, there are those who focus on the words "Ana B'choach," etc., in their thoughts, but they do not recite the letters or the words aloud. One who engages in kavanos must study the matters clearly in the writings of the Arizal, and then their kavanah is acceptable.

## 3 Tishrei

We begin [the daily Tehillim with] *Mizmor L’Dovid* and not *Lechu Neranena*, and this applies to every Shabbos and Yom Tov. The Haftorah is *Shuva* [until] *yikashlu vam*, [and] *Mi Eil Kamocha* [until] *mimei kedem*.

During a *yechidus* with the Tzemach Tzedek in the presence of the Alter Rebbe on the 6th of Elul, 5564, the Alter Rebbe related: On Shabbos Parshas Tavo, 5528, my teacher and master (the Maggid of Mezeritch) said a *maamar* on the verse, "And you shall return unto Hashem your G-d." He explained that the *avodah* of *teshuvah* must reach the point where "Hashem"—who is above the worlds—becomes "Elokecha" (your G-d). *Elokim* has the same *gematria* as *hateva* (nature), as in "In the beginning, *Elokim* created." The entire *chavraya kadisha* (holy fellowship) was in a state of great *hisorerus* from that *maamar*. The holy Rabbi Zusha of Anipoli said that he could not attain such a level of *teshuvah*, and therefore he would divide *teshuvah* into parts, for *teshuvah* is an acronym: *Tav*—"You shall be wholehearted with Hashem your G-d"; *Shin*—"I have set Hashem before me always"; *Vav*—"And you shall love your neighbor as yourself"; *Beis*—"In all your ways know Him"; *Hei*—"Walk humbly with your G-d."

When my father, the Rebbe, related this to me, he concluded: The word *teshuvah* is composed of five letters, each of which represents a path and manner of *avodah* in *teshuvah*—he then explained these five manners at length—and all of them are brought from potential into action through the *avodah* of *tefillah*.

## 4 Tishrei

The Fast of Gedaliah is postponed. Avinu Malkeinu. Selichos.

A brief explanation of my revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, regarding the first approach:

"Tamim" (wholehearted) — "You shall be wholehearted with Hashem your G-d." This refers to the avodah of teshuvah that comes through temimus (wholeheartedness). There are several levels and gradations in the matter of temimus, and specifically in relation to the teshuvah that transcends them all, which is the temimus of the heart — known as ernstkeit — as it is said regarding Avraham, "And You found his heart faithful before You."

## 5 Tishrei

A summary of the explanation of the second approach: "I have set Hashem before me always" (*Tehillim* 16:8). *Havayah* (the name of Hashem) indicates the bringing into being of the world and the creation of all beings, for the creation of the entire universe and its vitality occurs in a manner that is beyond comparison—*me'ayin l'yesh* (from nothingness into somethingness). This is the *avodah* of *teshuvah* that results from having the concept of the *Havayah* of the world and all that fills it constantly before one's eyes.

## 6 Tishrei

A summary explanation of the third method: "And you shall love your fellow as yourself." As the Alter Rebbe stated, this is the vessel for "And you shall love Hashem your G-d." This is explained in the statement, "Whoever is pleasing to his fellow creatures, the Spirit of the Omnipresent is pleased with him." The avodah of teshuvah through the cause of a good heart.

## 7 Tishrei

A summary explanation of the fourth method: "In all your ways know Him" (*Mishlei 3:6*). A person who sets their heart and mind upon everything that happens to them and around them sees Elokus tangibly. This is as the Mitteler Rebbe explained regarding the advantage that businesspeople have over those who sit in the *ohel* (those who study Torah exclusively) in the matter of seeing Elokus tangibly—the *avodah* of *teshuvah* that comes from seeing *hashgacha pratis*.

## 8 Tishrei

A brief explanation of the fifth way: "Walk humbly with Hashem your G-d." This means being careful that there be no outward display, as the Sages said, "A person should always be crafty in fear." The concept of this craftiness is that it should not be noticeable at all. It is well known that many of the early Chassidim would conceal themselves, and if they were discovered, they would be distressed. This is the avodah of teshuvah that comes from "walking humbly."

## 9 Tishrei, Erev Yom Kippur

Kapparos: Recite "Bnei Adam" three times, and each time rotate the kapparah three times, for a total of nine rotations.

Tachanun is not recited until the end of Tishrei. Malkos: Both the one administering the lashes and the one receiving them recite "V'hu Rachum" three times.

On Erev Yom Kippur, the avodah is regret for the past; on Yom Kippur itself, it is commitment for the future.

## 10 Tishrei, Yom Kippur

On the night of Yom Kippur, we begin with *Hashem Malach*, *Tagel*, etc. The verse *Or Zarua* is recited once, in a loud voice. *Al Da'as HaKahal*, etc., is recited three times, in a low voice. We do not repeat the stanza *Ki Hinei Kachomer*. The *Krias Shema* is recited as on Yom Tov. *Avinu Malkeinu*, *Zechor Rachamecha*, etc., are recited in all the prayers of the day. On Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, we recite the *Yud-Gimmel Midos HaRachamim* when taking out the Torah scroll, even when it falls on Shabbos. The *Tehillim* quota is recited after *Mussaf*.

The *Haftorah* for the *Mincha* of Yom Kippur is *Vayehi Dvar* through *U'vehemah Rabbah*, followed by *Mi E-l Kamocha*, etc. We also recite *LeDovid Hashem Ori* during *Mincha*. The Ark is opened for *Ne'ilah* at *Ashrei*, and it remains open until after the entire *Ne'ilah* prayer. In the *Kaddish* of *Ne'ilah*, we say *Le'eila Le'eila*. Then *Ein K'Elokeinu* and *Aleinu*. There is no *Nesias Kapayim*.

We recite *Hayom Yifneh*, even if the day has already drawn to a close. We do not recite the blessing *She'asah Li Kol Tzorki* until tomorrow.

Yom Kippur entails fasting for twenty-six hours.

## 11 Tishrei

On the night following Yom Kippur of the year 5556 (1795) in Liozna, the Alter Rebbe delivered a public discourse—as was the custom then, these were brief *maamarim*—based on the verse, "For what great nation is there that has G-d so close to it, as Hashem our G-d is, whenever we call upon Him" (*Devarim 4:7*). The verse says "to Him" (*eilav*), and not to His *middos*. The *Pardes* explains that "to Him" refers to the *oros* that clothe themselves in the *keilim* of the *Sefiros* of *Atzilus*. The Baal Shem Tov says that "to Him" refers to the *Elokus* that is within the *keilim* of the *Sefiros* of *Atzilus*. The simple interpretation is: "to Him" refers to the *Atzmus* of *Ein Sof*, which is present within every simple Jew through innate knowledge by way of *emunah peshutah*. And this is what is meant by "so close to Him" and "whenever we call upon Him"—to the *Atzmus* of *Ein Sof* and the *Atzmus* of the *neshamah*. *Tefillah* with *emunah peshutah* connects the *Atzmus* of the *neshamah* with the *Atzmus* of *Ein Sof*, so that the *Atzmus* of *Ein Sof* should be the one who heals the sick and blesses the years.

Regarding the conduct during Birkas Kohanim (as mentioned above regarding the second day of Sukkos), we know through this that the Alter Rebbe would take the Tzemach Tzedek, before his wedding, under his Tallis for Birkas Kohanim.

The Tzemach Tzedek related: In 5569 (1808), we heard from the Rebbe (the Alter Rebbe) for the sixth time the *maamar* "Ushavtem Mayim" — the first one — from *Likutei Torah*. It was in the vein of what the Gemara says (*Yerushalmi Sukkah* 5:1), "that from there they draw [the Holy Spirit]." Yonah received the revelation of prophecy during Simchas Beis HaShoeivah; we have received [revelations] *ad ein sof* (to infinity). The *sof* (end) is that Moshiach will come, *shene'utz techilatan besofan* (their beginning is rooted in their end).

## 17 Tishrei, First Day of Chol Hamoed Sukkos

Also with joy and exultation.
*Shalom Aleichem*, *Eshes Chayil*, *Mizmor L’Dovid*, *Da Hi Se’udata*, and *Veyiten Lecha* are recited in a whisper.
In *Mussaf*, [we recite] *V’nichsam V’nischehem* after the verses of each day. On Shabbos, we do not recite *Hoshanos*.

Through the *avodah* of *nisyonos* (trials), the level of *yechidah* in the soul is revealed, as the expression goes: "Embraced and attached to You"—the connection of the *neshamah* to *Elokus*. Through this, "it bears Your yoke"—in the fulfillment of Torah and *mitzvos* with *mesiras nefesh*. Thus, "a *yechidah* to unify You"—the level of *yechidah* in the soul is revealed. Through this revelation, the *yechidah* of the *nefesh habehamis* is transformed into *Elokus*, which is the matter of strength in the fulfillment of Torah and *mitzvos* with internal vitality, and with a feeling of great *taanug* and abundant pleasure in the *avodah* of Hashem. Regarding all worldly matters, "yes" and "no" are equal to him in true equanimity, for the *taanug* and the "good taste" in physical matters have been taken from him.

## 18 Tishrei, 2nd Day of Chol Hamoed Sukkos

We recite *Om Ani Choma* and also *Adon HaMoshia*, but we only perform the *hakafos* once. We recite the word "Hoshana" — both at the beginning and at the end — only for those letters that the *shaliach tzibbur* recites aloud; for the others, we recite it only once.

Our custom is to begin reciting aloud from the letter *samech* or *ayin*.

We are careful to complete the full circuit and conclude with the letter *tav*.

In one place, it is mentioned that in addition to the aforementioned, one should also have in mind wine for *Kiddush*—and then, automatically, all of the produce will be for good.

My revered father and teacher said at one of the *farbrengens* that one could say this is similar to the concept that it is specifically through the *koved rosh* prior to *davening*, where one feels bitterness in his soul, that there can subsequently be a *hispaalus* of the *midos* during *davening*.

During the Tikkun for the night of Hoshana Rabbah, one recites the parsha of V’Zos HaBerachah only once as Mikra. There were individuals whom my father, the Rebbe, instructed to recite it on Hoshana Rabbah with Rashi’s commentary, but this is not a general instruction for the public. On the eve of Simchas Torah, one recites it as Shnayim Mikra V’Echad Targum.

Before Hallel, one removes the two bindings from the lulav, leaving only the three that bind the three species together. On Hoshana Rabbah, it is customary to dip the slice of Hamotzi into honey, but this is not done on Shemini Atzeres or Simchas Torah.

## 22 Tishrei, Shemini Atzeres

We make Kiddush and eat, both at night and during the day. Hakafos take place on the night of Shemini Atzeres. The Haftorah is "Vayehi kechalos" — "and to Yisroel His people."

We do not recite the "Yehi Ratzon" for leaving the Sukkah.

Shemini Atzeres and Rosh Hashanah are equivalent in many matters of *kavanos* and supernal *yichudim*, except that on Rosh Hashanah they are in the mode of *aliyah* (ascent), while on Shemini Atzeres they are in the mode of *hamshachah* (drawing down). On Rosh Hashanah, the *avodah* is through supplication, with *hachna'ah* (submission) and intense bitterness, whereas on Shemini Atzeres, it is along the line of *simcha* (joy).

## 23 Tishrei, Simchas Torah

We do not read from the Torah at night. We do not perform Nesias Kapayim at Shacharis. It is not the custom to spread a tallis over the heads of those called up for Chasan Torah or Chasan Bereishis. Also, the one called to the Torah is supposed to say "Chazak" [and the congregation responds] "V'nischazeik."

On Simchas Torah of the year 5540 (1779), the Alter Rebbe said: "It is a tree of life to those who grasp it" — these are the *baalei hasagah* (those with intellectual attainment) who study Torah. "And those who support it are happy" — these are the *baalei avodah* who occupy themselves with Torah. "Happy" (*me'ushar*) means *glicklach* (fortunate/blessed), as it is written in the Zohar (Vol. III, 53b): Do not read *me'ushar* (happy), but rather *me'rosho* (from its head), for through their *avodah* they draw down *me'rosho*—that is, from the inwardness of the *Ein Sof Baruch Hu*—and this is like the virtue of the foot, which sustains the head specifically.

## 24 Tishrei, Isru Chag

A farbrengen in public, and especially on Shabbos or Motzaei Shabbos, is one of the foundations of the path of Chassidim and Chassidus, and an opening and gateway to the fundamental mitzvah of ahavas Yisrael.

In the vast majority of farbrengens, the speakers demand of those gathered that they improve their conduct and ways, that they set fixed times for the study of Chassidus, that they maintain that consistency, and that the study should be for the purpose of learning and fulfilling. The general concept and manner of rebuke is explained well in the maamar *Ve'im Ruach HaMoshel* (Kuntres 30), which is fitting for every single member of anash to study well for his own benefit. However, this rebuke at the time of a farbrengen is only regarding matters and issues that involve no element of embarrassing another person whatsoever, just as it has been from time immemorial, when one would rebuke his fellow with love and great affection.

## 26 Tishrei

The concept based on the verse, "Forever, Hashem, Your word stands in the heavens" (Tehillim 119:89), is cited by the Alter Rebbe (Shaar HaYichud VeHaEmunah, ch. 1; Iggeres HaKodesh, ch. 25) in the name of the Baal Shem Tov, even though this matter is also found in Midrash Tehillim. For one who conducts their life according to the Torah and the instructions of Chazal lives a life of happiness, both materially and spiritually.

## 28 Tishrei

## 29 Tishrei

One should recite the blessing over a rainbow, and not as some doubtfully suggest.

A certain householder in Mezhibuzh had a dispute with another householder. Once, while in the Baal Shem Tov’s shul, he shouted that he would tear his opponent apart like a fish.

The Baal Shem Tov instructed his talmidim to take each other’s hands and stand beside him with their eyes closed. The Baal Shem Tov placed his holy hands on the shoulders of the two talmidim standing next to him. Suddenly, the talmidim began to scream in great terror: they had seen the householder tearing his opponent apart like a fish.

## Cheshvan

The month of Cheshvan is often called Mar-Cheshvan. The word "Mar" (bitter) is actually an abbreviation for *Mar-Cheshvan*, because the month of Cheshvan is the month of the *Mar* (the Master) of the world, Hashem, as it is the month in which the Beis Hamikdash was completed.

The month of Tishrei is filled with the *yamim tovim* of Hashem. In Cheshvan, we begin our own *avodah*—the *avodah* of the individual, which is to draw the light of the *yamim tovim* of Tishrei into the days of the mundane year. This is the concept of "the righteous shall flourish like the palm tree" (*Tehillim 92:13*)—the palm tree grows slowly, but it is sturdy and bears fruit. This is the *avodah* of the entire year, beginning in Cheshvan.

## 1 Cheshvan, Rosh Chodesh

At Mincha—and likewise on the following Monday and Thursday—we read for the Yisrael from the verse, "And Hashem appeared to Avram, etc."

My revered father, the Rebbe, once said to someone during yechidus: From the moment the Ribono shel Olam said to Avraham Avinu, "Go for yourself from your land, etc.," and it is written, "And Avram journeyed, journeying toward the south," the secret of the birurim began. By the decree of Divine Providence, a person travels to his destinations to the place where the sparks that need to be refined by him are awaiting their redemption.

The tzaddikim, who possess the power of vision, see where their birurim are waiting for them and go there themselves. And as for the rest of the people, the Cause of all causes and the Reason for all reasons brings about various causes and reasons so that they will arrive at that place where the avodah of birurim has been assigned to them.

## 2 Cheshvan

From a sicha of my father, the Rebbe, Motzaei Shabbos Parshas Lech Lecha, 5651: In the early years of his leadership, the Alter Rebbe would say in public, "One must live with the times." Through his brother, the Maharil, the elder Chassidim discovered that the Rebbe meant one must live with the parsha of the week and the parsha of the day from that week's sedra. This means not only studying the weekly parsha every day, but living with it.

## 3 Cheshvan

(Continuation of the aforementioned sicha): Bereishis is a joyous parsha, even if the end is not quite as pleasant. Noach contains the Mabul, but it has a joyous end—the birth of Avraham Avinu. The truly joyous week is Parshas Lech Lecha; throughout all the days of the week, one lives with Avraham Avinu, the first to engage in mesiras nefesh to publicize Elokus in the world. And this mesiras nefesh, Avraham Avinu bequeathed as an inheritance to all Yidden.

## 4 Cheshvan

The study of Torah each and every day affects souls in a literal sense—not only the soul of the one studying, but also the souls of his household, for then the atmosphere of the home becomes an atmosphere of Torah and yiras shamayim.

## 5 Cheshvan

Yisrael are compared to the stars that twinkle in the firmament, by whose light even one who walks in the darkness of night will not go astray.
Every single one of Yisrael, whether man or woman, possesses sufficient moral and spiritual strength to influence and set even his acquaintances and those he knows upon the right path.

## 6 Cheshvan

When the Alter Rebbe wished to bless R' Yekusiel Liepler with wealth, he replied that he did not desire it, lest the wealth distract him from the study of Chassidus and engagement in avodah. And when the Rebbe wished to bless him with long life, he said: "But not with 'peasant years'—years where 'they have eyes but do not see, they have ears but do not hear'—where one sees no Elokus and hears no Elokus."

## 7 Cheshvan

The order following the giving of the Torah is: the removal of the *orlah* (foreskin) of the body, the tongue, and the heart — corresponding to action, speech, and thought. Avraham Avinu, who lived before the giving of the Torah, first recognized his Creator — thought; afterwards, he publicized His *Elokus* — speech; and subsequently, he performed the *milah* — action.

## 8 Cheshvan

The word *mitzvah* is related to the term *tzavta* (connection) and *chibur* (joining). One who performs a *mitzvah* connects with the *Atzmus* of the Holy One, blessed be He, who is the One who commands that *mitzvah*. This is the meaning of the statement, "The reward of a *mitzvah* is a *mitzvah*"—for the very fact that one has connected with the *Atzmus* of *Ohr Ein Sof* who commands the *mitzvah*, that is his reward.

This can be understood through a physical analogy: A very simple person may have an inner *bitul* toward wisdom and the stature of a wise man, a *bitul* of complete self-effacement. From the perspective of the wise man, this simple person does not enter into his category of "human" at all—and this is not because he dismisses or rejects him, *chas v'shalom*, which would be a negative trait; rather, he simply does not consider there to be any connection or relationship between them.

However, when the wise man commands the simple person to do something for him, through this command, the existence of the simple person is born. Both for himself—as he now senses his own existence, for he is capable of fulfilling the *mitzvah* of the wise man who commanded him to do something—and in the eyes of the wise man, he is now considered an entity to whom he can speak and issue a command. Through this, the command unites the exalted, lofty wise man with the simplest of people. The analogy is understood.

It is also understood that in relation to the above, there is no difference at all in what the command might be, whether it is a great and lofty matter or a small and simple one.

## 9 Cheshvan

When my grandfather, the Rebbe (the Tzemach Tzedek), was four or five years old, he entered his grandfather’s room on Shabbos, Parshas Vayeira, and began to cry, saying: "Why did Hashem appear to Avraham Avinu, yet to us He does not appear?" The Tzemach Tzedek replied: "When a Yid, a tzaddik, at ninety-nine years old, decides that he must undergo bris milah, he becomes worthy of Hashem appearing to him."

## 10 Cheshvan

The Rebbe (the Alter Rebbe) used to refer to Atzilus as "above." It is told that when writing it, he would be so overcome with hispaalus that he could not write more than the letters "Atzi."

## 12 Cheshvan

One of the first teachings of the Alter Rebbe — which were then called "verter" (words): *Shema Yisrael* — a Yid perceives — *Hashem Elokeinu* — that our strength and our vitality is that which is above nature, and — *Hashem Echad*.

## 13 Cheshvan

The Baal Shem Tov used to conduct a Gemara shiur with his talmidim. The method of study was with great charifus and gaonus, and they would study the Rambam, Alfasi, Rosh, and other Rishonim relevant to the Gemara being studied. The Baal Shem Tov would translate the words into Yiddish. When they were studying Arachin and reached the statement of our Sages (15b), "Lashon hara kills three," the Baal Shem Tov explained: Lashon hara kills all three—the one who conceives it, the one who tells it, and the one who listens to it. And this is in spiritual garments, which is more severe than physical murder.

## 14 Cheshvan

"The steps of a man are established by Hashem," and every individual Jew has a spiritual mission in life: to engage in the work of building a dwelling place for Him, may He be blessed. Everyone, wherever they are and in whatever situation they find themselves, must exert all their strength to search and investigate, to find their "spiritual livelihood" just as they search for physical livelihood. This is because "He desires his way"—the way of the Holy One, blessed be He—as it is written regarding Avraham, "For I have known him... that they shall keep the way of Hashem." There are two ways: the way of nature and the way that is above nature. The Holy One, blessed be He, created the world to appear as it does through the way of nature—to the physical eye—and this is the "way of Elokim." Torah and mitzvos are the "way of Havayah," which is the drawing down of that which is above nature into nature; it is through this that the Holy One, blessed be He, bestows upon the Jewish people [blessings] that are above nature, within nature.

## 15 Cheshvan

The neshamah waits above for the time when it will merit to descend into a guf, because the neshamah senses what it can accomplish while being here below. It can attain the level of "to take pleasure in Hashem." So, why would one put that off?

## 16 Cheshvan

Thought is the garment and servant of the intellect and the emotions. At times when it has no service to perform for the intellect or the emotions, it still continues its activity of thinking and contemplating; however, its activity is not only unproductive, but it is also unrestrained and shameful. It is explained that the cause of foreign or evil thoughts is "an empty head"—for when the intellect is occupied, the thought has something to serve, and there is no room for thoughts of foolishness and vanity that have no substance.

## 17 Cheshvan

One must be mindful of time; one must accept the yoke of Torah. Every period of time, every day that passes, is not merely a day but a matter of life itself. The days pass, as the statement says (Yerushalmi, Berachos 1:5): "A day enters and a day departs, a Shabbos enters, etc., a month, etc., a year, etc." My father said in the name of the Alter Rebbe: A summer day and a winter night constitute a year.

## 18 Cheshvan

In *Torah Or*, *Parshas Chayei Sarah*, in the *maamar* beginning with the words "Lehavin" (To Understand), there is a typographical error in the statement of our Sages, "Better is one hour...". It should read: "It will be in a state of *bitul* (but now... this is the level of *iser*—see what is written further on regarding the explanation of 'binding his foal to the vine'), which is the level of *chefetz* (desire), and it is written, 'Give thanks...' within *chochmah*, *binah*, and *da'as*, but the ultimate intent..."

## 19 Cheshvan

The Alter Rebbe’s response in yechidus: Chassidus is *Shema Yisrael*. *Shema* is an acronym for *Se’u Marom Eineichem* (Lift your eyes on high). And it says *Marom* (on high), not *Shamayim* (heavens); *Marom* means higher and higher, to reach beyond the intellect. And this itself is to be understood through the intellect, just as it says, "And see who created these."

## 20 Cheshvan

The birthday of the Rebbe Rashab, of blessed memory — in the year 5621 (תרכ"א). As the Tzemach Tzedek expressed it, he was named after the Mitteler Rebbe, with half of his own father’s name.

Every year on his birthday, my father, the Rebbe, would recite a *maamar* of *Chassidus*, though it was done privately, except when it fell on *Shabbos*. On his final birthday in this world, he recited the *maamar* beginning with the words, "You have given a banner to those who fear You, to be displayed because of the truth" (*Tehillim 60:6*). When he finished, he said to me, "On one’s birthday, one should recite *Chassidus*. May Hashem grant you the gift that you will recite *Chassidus* on your own birthday, but may it be with kindness and mercy." It took seven years for this to come to pass.

## 21 Cheshvan

The avodah of tefillah is what brings the comprehension of the intellect into the feeling of the heart, and both together [are expressed] in the actual avodah of performing mitzvos with yiras shamayim and the acquisition of refined middos.

## 22 Cheshvan

One of the teachings of the Maggid of Mezeritch that our Rebbe the Alter Rebbe heard during his first time there—from the end of the summer of 5524 until after the holiday of Pesach in 5525:

"I have made the earth, and created man upon it" (*Yeshayahu 45:12*). "I"—the One who is "I," who is concealed and hidden even from the most exalted *Natzilim* (emanated beings)—clothed His essence, *yisbarach*, in many *Tzimtzumim* in order to emanate the *Natzilim* and create the created beings: *serafim*, *chayos*, *ofanim*, angels, and worlds without number. And through *Tzimtzumim* without measure, I made this physical earth. "And created man upon it"—man is the purpose of the entire creation. "And I created [man] upon it"—the 613 *mitzvos* are the purpose of man.

As it is brought in the *Pardes* in the name of *Sefer HaBahir*: The *middah* of *Chesed* said before the *Ribono shel Olam*, "Master of the Universe, from the days that Avram was on earth, I have not needed to perform my work, for Avram stands and serves in my place." For Avraham, who is a *neshamah* in a body, and who engages in *hachnasas orchim* to publicize His divinity, *yisbarach*, in this lowly world, is higher in stature and level than the *middah* of *Chesed* of *Atzilus*. And when the *middah* of *Chesed* said this before the *Ribono shel Olam*, it was an expression of the jealousy of the *middah* of *Chesed* of *Atzilus*, which was jealous of the *avodah* of Avraham, *a.h.*

## 23 Cheshvan

The Tzemach Tzedek was arrested twenty-two times during the Rabbinical Conference in Petersburg in 5603 (1843), for his opposition to the government’s demands regarding changes in education, and so on. The minister asked him, "Isn't this rebellion against the monarchy?" The Tzemach Tzedek replied, "A rebel against the monarchy is liable to the death of the body, but a rebel against the Kingdom of Heaven is liable to the death of the soul. So, which is greater?"

## 24 Cheshvan

In material matters, one should look at those who are below oneself and thank Hashem for His kindness toward him.

In spiritual matters, one should look at those who are above oneself and plead with Hashem to grant him the proper insight to learn from them, and the strength and fortitude to be able to ascend in his own spiritual growth.

## 25 Cheshvan

Divine Providence brings everyone to their specific location for the sake of strengthening Yiddishkeit and disseminating Torah.
When one plows and sows — it grows.

## 26 Cheshvan

The true path is that one must know one’s own essence, with a genuine recognition of one’s own shortcomings and one’s own virtues. And when one knows these shortcomings, one must rectify them through actual avodah, rather than fulfilling one’s obligation with mere sighs.

## 27 Cheshvan

R' A. HaLevi of Homel related: When I came to Liozna, I found among the elder Chassidim—those who had been Chassidim of the Maggid and the holy Rabbi Menachem Mendel of Horodok—that it was common for them to say: "Hold onto a Yid, and Hashem will hold onto you; do a favor for a Yid, and Hashem will do a favor for you; bring a Yid closer, and Hashem will bring you closer."

## 28 Cheshvan

The concept of Hashgacha Pratis is that it is not merely that every specific movement of all created beings is under Hashgacha Pratis—which is the very life-force and existence of the creature—but furthermore, that the specific movement of a creature has a general connection to the overall purpose of creation. For through the combination and unification of all these individual actions, the supreme purpose in the mystery of the entire creation is fulfilled.

A person should contemplate this: If the movement of a blade of grass comes about through Hashgacha Pratis and impacts the fulfillment of the purpose of creation, how much more so is this true for the category of *medaber* (speaking beings) in general, and for *Yisrael am kerovo* (the Jewish people, His close ones) in particular.

## 29 Cheshvan

Regarding the holy avodah of reciting Tehillim in a tzibbur: it is impossible to fully describe the magnitude of the zechus of those who participate in this, nor the magnitude of the nachas ruach caused Above, as is explained in the sefarim hakedoshim—and a glimpse of this is found in the Kuntres Takanas Amiras Tehillim B'rabim (Kovetz Michtavim, 1). Ashreichem Yisrael! Because of this, may you, your wives, your sons, and your daughters—Hashem yichyehem—be blessed with robust health and with parnassah in great abundance. And through your efforts, may the entire congregation of Yisrael—sheyichyu—in your respective communities, and indeed all of our brethren, be saved with whatever they require, both in gashmius and in ruchnius.

## Kislev

The month of Kislev is the month of *geulah*. The *geulah* of the Mitteler Rebbe began on the 10th of Kislev, and his *geulah* was completed on the 19th of Kislev. The 19th of Kislev is the *Rosh Hashanah* of Chassidus. It was then that the light of Chassidus was revealed, and the *neshamah* of the Baal Shem Tov was revealed in the world.

The *avodah* of the month of Kislev is *hiskashrus* to the Rebbes, and to study their *maamarim* and *sichos*. This brings about *hispaalus* and *hisorerus* in *avodas Hashem*.

The *geulah* of the 19th of Kislev is not merely a personal *geulah* for the Alter Rebbe, but a *geulah* for all of *anash*, and for the entire Jewish people. It is a *geulah* that affects the *neshamah* and the *guf*. Through the study of Chassidus, one attains a level of *dveikus* in Hashem that transcends the limitations of the world. This is the essence of the *avodah* of the month of Kislev: to bring the light of *Ein Sof* into the world, and to transform the world into a dwelling place for Hashem.

## 1 Kislev, Rosh Chodesh

There were times when R. Yekusiel Liepler (a Chossid of the Alter Rebbe) would daven Shacharis, Mincha, and Maariv one after the other, without any interval between them.

## 2 Kislev

My grandfather, the Rebbe, once explained in a maamar the statement that Rabbi Elazar would give a coin to a poor person and then daven. Since davening must be performed with vitality, by giving tzedakah to a poor person before davening and thereby sustaining him, one adds much vitality to the davening. He gestured with his hand from below to above to hint that this increase is beyond measure. Many times before davening, my father, the Rebbe, would search for a poor person to provide him with food.

## 3 Kislev

Regarding the difference between the conduct of the Baal Shem Tov and the conduct of the Maggid: the Baal Shem Tov was occupied with various travels, whereas the Maggid remained in his home. Furthermore, during the years of the Maggid’s leadership, the concept of Chassidus was already well-known even in distant places, through the travels of the *Chavraya Kadisha*, and many average *baalei batim* became connected to the teachings of Chassidus and would travel to Mezritch.

## 4 Kislev

For *mayim acharonim*, one washes the tips of the fingers, and then, while they are still moist, passes them over the lips.

## 5 Kislev

"And behold, a ladder set up on the earth" — tefillah is the ladder of the soul’s hiskashrus to Elokus. And even though it is "set up on the earth"—for the beginning of tefillah is merely through gratitude—yet "its top reaches to the heavens," to the level of bitul. However, one arrives at this only through the prerequisite of hasagah and havana during Pesukei D’zimra, the berachos of Krias Shema, and Krias Shema itself.

## 6 Kislev

My revered father and teacher said: Krias Shema al Hamitah is, in miniature, like the Viduy recited before the soul departs from the body. The difference is that at the time of death, one is departing the marketplace entirely, and the business of "today to do them" has already concluded. But at the time of Krias Shema al Hamitah each night, one is still in the midst of the marketplace, and one can still accomplish.

## 7 Kislev

There are three types of wisdom:

The wisdom of nullifying the material, through teaching the loathsomeness and detestability of all physical and material matters — this is the wisdom of *Mussar*.

The wisdom of recognizing the superiority of the form and the spiritual, in character traits and intellectual concepts, and teaching the ways of drawing close to them — this is the wisdom of *Chakirah* (philosophy).

The wisdom of the form prevailing over the matter, teaching the superiority of refined matter and the superiority of the form when it has been established within the material, and their integration together such that no beginning or end is found, for "their beginning is wedged in their end and their end in their beginning." One G-d created them, and for one purpose were they created: to reveal the light of the holiness of the hiddenness of His strength, may He be blessed. And it is specifically the two of them together that complete that perfection which arose in His will, may He be blessed — this is the wisdom of the teachings of *Chassidus*.

## 5 Kislev

A person should contemplate how great the kindnesses of the Creator, blessed be He, are—that such a small creature as man can provide great *nachas ruach* to the Greatest of the Great, as it is written, "And His greatness is unsearchable." Therefore, a person must always be inspired and perform his *avodah* with heart and a willing soul.

On Sunday, Parshas Noach, 28 Tishrei, he departed from Lubavitch accompanied by officials. At midday, he arrived in Dobromysl, where he delivered a maamar of Chassidus on the verse "Many waters..." ## 12 Kislev

The blessing of *Shepatrani* is recited without mentioning Hashem’s name or His sovereignty. It is recited on Mondays, Thursdays, and Rosh Chodesh, and not exclusively on Shabbos.

It is a tradition handed down to us from the Baal Shem Tov: When one hears something negative about a fellow Jew, even if one does not know the person, one must feel great distress. For in either case, someone is certainly not in a good state: If what is being said about this person is true, then he is not in a good state; and if it is not true, then the person telling the story is in a state of "not good."

## 13 Kislev

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, said: Chassidus transforms reality and reveals the essence. The essence of a Yid cannot be evaluated or measured, for he is a part of the Essence (Atzmus). Just as one who grasps a part of something is considered to be grasping the whole, and just as the Essence has no measure, so too the part has no measure—similar to the concept of "a wing is a type of wing." It is simply that Atzmus created a neshamah so that it would be a created being—and Chassidus reveals this essence.

## 14 Kislev

One recites Bircas Hamazon over a Kos Shel Bracha, even when there are not ten people present. The cup is held on the palm of the hand with the fingers upright. One holds the cup from the recitation of "Rabosai mir velen bentshen" until after the conclusion of the third blessing, at which point it is placed on the table.

In one of his sichos, my revered father, the Rebbe, explained the following, and this is the essence of his words: There is a *tzaddik*, a *yashar*, a *tamim*, and a *chossid*. A *tzaddik* is defined by the fulfillment of positive *mitzvos*—he draws down the revelations of *Seder Hishtalshelus*. A *yashar* is defined by the fulfillment of negative *mitzvos*—he draws down revelations that are above *Seder Hishtalshelus*. *Tamim*—earnestness—is the revelation where "the taste of the tree is the same as the taste of its fruit," the unification of *sovev kol almin* and *memalei kol almin*. Above all of these is a *chossid*, and there are three levels within this: 1) That the affairs of the world do not disturb or confuse him. Every person can reach this, and every person is obligated to reach this. 2) That all of his affairs are *Elokus*. And although "it is not far from you," etc., nevertheless, this does not apply to everyone. 3) What is written in *Tikkunei Zohar*: "Who is a *chossid*? He who acts with loving-kindness (*mit-chaseid*) with his Creator, with His 'nest' (*kaneh*), which is His own." In *Sefer Shel Beinonim* it is explained that this means to unify *Kudsha Brich Hu* and His *Shechinah* in the lower worlds, and not merely to satisfy his own thirsty soul.

## 15 Kislev

In *Torah Or*, in the *maamar* beginning with the words *Vayeshev*: the phrase "and it cannot see in love" (*ve'einah yecholah lir'os be'ahavah*) should read "and it cannot be in love" (*ve'einah yecholah lihyos be'ahavah*).

The Alter Rebbe related to his son, the Mitteler Rebbe: The *Zayde* (the Baal Shem Tov) says that one must have *mesiras nefesh* for *ahavas Yisrael*, even for a fellow Jew whom one has never seen.

## 16 Kislev

A fundamental principle in the Chabad approach is that the mind, which is naturally dominant over the heart, must subjugate the heart to the avodah of Hashem through intellectual comprehension, understanding, and deep contemplation of the greatness of the Creator of the world, blessed be He.

## 17 Kislev

Chabad Chassidus opens the gates to the chambers of Chochma and Binah, to know and recognize the One who spoke and brought the world into being through intellectual comprehension. It awakens the emotions of the heart to experience hispaalus in the measure corresponding to that intellectual understanding, and it serves as a guide, showing that every individual—each according to his own capacity—can approach the holy to serve Hashem with his mind and heart.

## 18 Kislev

The Tzemach Tzedek writes: The love that is of the level of "And with You, I desire nothing" (meaning) is to desire nothing other than Him, may He be blessed—not even the Heavens and the Earth, which are the Upper and Lower Gan Eden, for they were created only by the letter *yud*, etc. Rather, the love should be only for Him, may He be blessed, alone—that is, for the *Mahut V'atzmut* of Hashem, may He be blessed, literally. This is how the language was heard from my teacher and master, of blessed memory (the Alter Rebbe), in his *dveikus*, when he would say in these words:

I do not want anything; I do not want Your Gan Eden, I do not want Your Olam HaBa, etc. I want nothing but You alone.
