# The Essence of Toras HaChassidus

## 1

The Essence of the Teachings of Chassidus

B"H.
The Essence of the Teachings of Chassidus

**1.** Regarding the essence of the teachings of Chassidus—and the innovation it brings to the parts of Torah revealed before it—many explanations have been offered. Among them:

a) In the time of the Baal Shem Tov, the world was in a state of fainting, and through the revelation of the Baal Shem Tov and the teachings of Chassidus, the world was awakened from its faint.

b) "A Chassid is one who acts beyond the letter of the law" (as our Sages of blessed memory said, "A Chassid burns it—even though it causes him a loss").

c) "The virtue of Chassidus is that natural powers become Divine powers". As our Rebbe, the author of the Tanya and the Shulchan Aruch, stated, "The entire matter of Chassidus is to change the nature of one's character traits": not merely to change one's natural character traits, but rather—"the nature of one's character traits".

d) The teachings of Chassidus introduced the innovation that every single person—even one who does not possess a lofty neshamah and has not refined himself—can grasp Elokus. Because by explaining the matters of the "secret" part of Torah and bringing them closer to the intellect through examples and analogies from the powers and attributes of the soul—"from my flesh I perceive Elokah"—the possibility was granted to every person to grasp even this part of Torah; and not only through the intellect of his nefesh Elokis, but also through the intellect of his nefesh sichlis, and even—through the intellect of his nefesh habehamis.

## 2

**B.** It is understood and simple, as follows: that Toras HaChassidus is a single point that is not composed of various disparate details. Since all the virtues mentioned in the aforementioned explanations (and in other explanations) are distinct virtues, it is therefore necessary that these virtues do not define or exhaust the *essence* of Toras HaChassidus. The essence of Chassidus is an essential point, abstracted from specific details; rather, it is from this essential point that the aforementioned virtues are found and branch out.

The essential point of Chassidus is—as discussed above in the maamar, which is fundamentally a maamar of the Rebbe (Rashab) —the drawing down of a new light from the level of the *pnimiyus* of Keter, and even higher: the drawing down of the level of the *pnimiyus* of Atik itself, the level of Ein Sof that is found in *Radla* (Reisha D'Lo Isyada).

This matter itself (as stated in the maamar) necessitates that all the virtues of Chassidus explained in various places are merely branches of its essential point. For since Chassidus is the drawing down of the level of Ein Sof, it is understood and simple that "Ein Sof" is the essence, and all other matters are merely existences and consequences derived from it.

**C.** What was stated above regarding Toras HaChassidus applies *in general* to all parts of the Torah: The Torah includes all types of perfection and virtues found in the world [which are divided in general—regarding the matters of the "small world," which is man—into the virtues of the *mochin* and the virtues of the *midos*:

Regarding moral conduct and good character traits: the *mishpatim* and paths of *mussar* in the Torah (such as in Maseches Avos and the like) are the ultimate good and truth. All the systems of *mussar* that human beings have invented from their own hearts are mixtures of good and evil, truth and falsehood. And the portions of good that exist in each of those systems have their source in the Torah.

## 3

(As is known from the story of the Rebbe Rayatz, during one of his travels, several people were arguing and expressing different opinions regarding the Torah’s attitude toward various political systems and which one it aligns with, with each of them citing a source in the Torah for their position. When they asked for the Rebbe Rayatz’s opinion on the matter, he replied: "Because the Torah is the ultimate truth and goodness, it contains all the good elements found in every system.")

Regarding wisdom—the Torah is the most sublime wisdom, as it is written, "For it is your wisdom and your understanding in the eyes of the nations."

Not only that, but the entire flow of vitality into the world (even in the higher worlds) depends on (even the slightest detail of) the Torah.

However, this is not the essence of the Torah. The essence of the Torah is that it is "completely unified with the Ohr Ein Sof, which is clothed within it in a state of absolute unity." Therefore, since all the worlds are as nothing in relation to the Ein Sof, so too are they in relation to the Torah. For this reason, "it is not to be praised at all with the praise of the vitality of all the worlds, since they are considered as nothing". Rather, from the perspective of its essential point—that it is unified with the Ohr Ein Sof—it naturally includes all the various virtues and perfections in the world, and through it comes the flow of vitality, and so on.

Indeed, although the essential point of *all* parts of the Torah is that they are unified with the Ohr Ein Sof, nevertheless, this point is expressed primarily in the teachings of Chassidus (as stated in the maamar regarding "Chassidus," that it is (specifically) the drawing down of the Ein Sof that is found in *Reisha D'lo Yada*).

For in all parts of the Torah, the aspect of the "Ein Sof" within them is clothed in some form—a form that defines and "expresses" the essence of that part. This form of that specific part of Torah [Peshat, Remez, Drush, Sod] conceals the simplicity of the Ein Sof clothed within it—a concealment of the garment that, as it were, cannot be exchanged. In contrast, the teachings of Chassidus, since they are not defined by any specific form, and the "forms" through which the subject matter of Chassidus is revealed are merely garments that can be changed and *will be changed* (which is why it includes all four parts of *Pardes* in the Torah and gives them life, as will be explained below), the form does not truly conceal the simplicity of the Ein Sof within it.

## 4

4. The essence of Toras HaChassidus can be clarified by explaining the inner content of Moshiach. For "from the reward [of a mitzvah] we know its essence", and since it is through the [dissemination of the wellsprings] of Chassidus that "the Master comes"—this refers to Melech HaMoshiach—we can therefore know the essence of Chassidus from the matter of Moshiach, which is its reward.

With the coming of Moshiach, many lofty matters will be renewed. Such as: the redemption of Yisrael (and the Shechinah) from galus; higher than that: "Yisrael will be great sages and know hidden things, and they will attain the knowledge of their Creator, etc., 'for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of Hashem, etc.'"; and higher still: a change and renewal in the act of Creation.

(And that which they said, "There is no difference between this world and the days of Moshiach except for the subjugation of the kingdoms alone", refers only to the beginning of the era of the days of Moshiach. And specifically according to what is written in Ra'aya Mehemna, this statement was said regarding the unlearned, whereas it is not so regarding the talmidei chachamim.)

The aspect of Elokus that transcends nature will then be revealed (which is why they will *read* the Name as it is written, for throughout the entire world there will be a revelation of the aspect of the Name Havayah—He was, He is, and He will be as one —which transcends time and space, similar to the revelation that existed in the Beis Hamikdash).

The essence of these matters will be renewed not only in this world, but in all the worlds. For the conduct in the higher worlds follows the order of conduct in this world [and is similar to the fixing of Rosh Chodesh and the Mo'adim (during which additional light is drawn down) above, which corresponds to the manner of their fixing below]. And since, with the coming of Moshiach, the general state of this world and its particulars will change, a corresponding change will also take place in the higher worlds.

However, after all the above, all these matters—even though their elevation and wonder are great and vast—are merely a branching out from the primary point of Moshiach.

**5.** The primary essence of Moshiach is—Yechidah. As is known, Dovid attained the level of Nefesh, Eliyahu—Ruach, Moshe—Neshamah, Adam HaRishon—Chayah, and Moshiach will attain the Yechidah.

The superiority of the Yechidah over the four levels of Naran-Ch [within the five levels of every soul]: the four levels of Naran-Ch are specific levels, while the aspect of Yechidah is the essence of the soul that transcends the category of particulars, as its name implies. And although the four levels of Naran-Ch are drawn down from the aspect of Yechidah—the essence of the soul—it is not that the aspect of Yechidah is in the category of a source and a "generalization" for these particulars—for the term "Yechidah" indicates a simple unity that is negated from any connection to particulars, even from the category of being a source for particulars (as is known, the difference between "Yachid" and "Echad")—rather, it is an essential point of the soul, from which the four levels of Naran-Ch are found.

And just as in every individual soul, the aspect of Yechidah is the essential point of the soul, so too is it regarding the (vitality and) soul of the entirety of Seder Hishtalshelus, that the aspect of Yechidah within it (the aspect of

Moshiach) is the very point of vitality that transcends the category of definition. From this point, all individual levels branch out—the general nefesh, ruach, neshamah, and chayah—for the essence of vitality is unlimited. Its limitlessness is not merely a matter of eternity, in that it is not captured by the changes of time, for every essence is unchanging [which is also one of the reasons why in the time to come there will be eternal life: currently, as only the aspect of the diffusion of vitality is drawn into the world, the phenomenon of death exists. Rather, it is also a matter of quality and level, for it is complete with the ultimate perfection. Therefore, when the aspect of yechidah is drawn into the worlds and they live with essential vitality, they will, by definition, be in a state of ultimate excellence and perfection.

Rather, Chassidus is a new vitality, an essential vitality of the aspect of yechidah. And when this new vitality of Chassidus is drawn into the world [the general Torah of Chassidus

**7.** For this reason, too, the teachings of Chassidus are compared to oil, the "secret of secrets":

Oil is the essential point—the "essence"—of everything. From this it follows that, in its own right, it is distinct from everything (for if it had a connection to a *specific* thing, it could not be the essential point of *everything*). Rather, it is in the category of "essence," and because it is essence, it is found in everything, for the essence is found in all.

This matter, like all things in the inner dimensions of Torah, is also expressed in the revealed dimensions of Torah, even down to *halacha lemaaseh*: on one hand, oil does not mix with other liquids, and on the other hand, it permeates everything.

Therefore, the teachings of Chassidus are compared to oil, for they, too, possess these two qualities: on one hand...

Its essential nature—which does not become involved in anything—is the very point of vitality that transcends expansion; yet, on the other hand, it expands and permeates every single thing, as mentioned above.

**8.** The vitality that the teachings of Chassidus infuse into all matters of the world (the "small world" which is man, and the world in its literal sense) is drawn from the vitality that Chassidus infuses into all matters of Torah, and comes about through this vitality. For all matters of creation are drawn from the Torah and come into being through it.

In Torah, there are four parts: *Peshat*, *Remez*, *Derush*, and *Sod*. The teachings of Chassidus infuse vitality into each one of them. For—in addition to the fact that the teachings of Chassidus provide explanations for various matters *in all* four parts of the Torah (which itself refutes the notion that Chassidus came only to explain the "Sod" part of the Torah)—the study of Chassidus infuses vitality into every single matter (in all parts of *Pardes* within it) that one studies in Torah, such that it is "alive" in an entirely different way, with essential vitality. This vitality also adds greater clarity and depth to the understanding of the matter.

An example of this in the *Nigleh* of Torah and in *Halacha*:

"*All* the services of Yom Kippur are valid only when performed by him" (the *Kohen Gadol*): Just as the holiness of Yom Kippur is effective for the unique service of Yom Kippur, it also acts upon the *Temidin* and *Musafin* (of Shabbos) which, in their own right, belong to all days of the year, such that they, too, are sanctified by the holiness of Yom Kippur.

And so it is also regarding the teachings of Chassidus—the aspect of "Shabbos Shabbaton" in the Torah—

## 9

It operates within all parts of the Torah. For the four parts of the Torah—Pardes—are the four levels of *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah* within the Torah, and the teachings of Chassidus are the level of *yechidah* within the Torah. Through the revelation of the *yechidah*, the *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah* are also elevated, as mentioned above.

9. The matters within the Torah are infinite in number. We shall explain—to the extent possible—one matter in the Torah as it is according to *peshat*, *remez*, *derush*, and *sod*, and the vitality, clarity, and depth that the teachings of Chassidus infuse into all four of these parts. From this, we will learn that this is the case for all matters in the Torah.

The matter we have chosen as an example is "Modeh Ani Lefanecha...". For:

a) Since a person must accustom himself to say "Modeh Ani" immediately upon waking from his sleep, "and thereby remember Hashem standing over him and arise with alacrity," this declaration is the foundation and beginning of a person’s *avodah* (the fulfillment of *all* the instructions in the Torah) and of his life—throughout the entire day.

b) The content of this declaration—which is "to contemplate immediately before Whom he is lying, before the King of Kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is written, 'Do I not fill the heavens and the earth?'"—"so should he think in *all* his business and affairs," "and this is a great principle in the Torah and in the levels of the *tzaddikim* who walk before Elokus, as it is written, 'I have set Hashem before me *always'."* That is to say, in this manner, the entire day must be.

c) All of the above—a person’s *avodah* to his Creator—is achieved by first *awakening from his sleep* in the vanities of the world—in the language of the well-known Rambam.

(And with this, one can understand the saying of the Rebbe (Rashab), of blessed memory, who said in his childhood years that the "point" [the colon] after the word *bechemlah* in "Modeh Ani" must be spread out (*farnendershpreiten*) over the entire day—for in every single moment throughout the entire day, there must be the *avodah* of "Modeh Ani.")

## 10

10. The concept of "Modeh Ani" according to the *peshat* (literal) dimension of Torah is an expression of gratitude to Hashem for "returning my soul to me." Although we recite the *bracha* of "Elokai Neshamah" to acknowledge the return of the soul, we must also recite "Modeh Ani," because the obligation to express gratitude for the soul's return arises the moment one awakens from sleep. Just as the obligation to recite a *bracha* on physical pleasure arises the moment one experiences the pleasure (and even before one actually enjoys it), so too, the obligation to recite a *bracha* of gratitude for the return of the soul—which is the greatest pleasure of all, encompassing all individual pleasures in the world—arises the moment one awakens from sleep.

Since the *bracha* of "Elokai Neshamah" includes the mention of Hashem’s Name, it cannot be recited (at that moment) before washing one's hands. Therefore, one must express gratitude for the return of the soul immediately upon waking by reciting at least "Modeh Ani." (Nevertheless, one must still recite the "Elokai Neshamah" *bracha* afterward, because—in addition to the fact that "Elokai Neshamah" contains details not found in "Modeh Ani"—"Modeh Ani" lacks the mention of Hashem’s Name, and any *bracha* that does not include the Name and Kingship of Hashem is not considered a *bracha*.)

Based on this, we can understand why, in the time of the Gemara, they did not recite "Modeh Ani" and only recited "Elokai Neshamah," for "Modeh Ani" adds nothing new to the "Elokai Neshamah" *bracha* (and, as mentioned, is actually lesser). The reason we are required to recite "Modeh Ani" nowadays is solely so that the expression of gratitude occurs immediately upon waking; in their time, since they recited "Elokai Neshamah" the moment they awoke, there was no need to recite "Modeh Ani."

The concept of "Modeh Ani" according to the *remez* (allusive) dimension of Torah: The return of the soul each morning *alludes* to the resurrection of the dead. Sleep is one-sixtieth of death, and consequently, the return of the soul is a microcosm of the resurrection of the dead. This is the meaning of "You have returned my soul to me... great is Your faithfulness": from the fact that "You have returned my soul to me," we know that "great is Your faithfulness" regarding the resurrection of the dead.

## 11

The concept of *Modeh Ani*, etc., according to the *Drush* (homiletical) dimension of Torah: The return of the soul every morning—"new every morning"—where the Ribono shel Olam returns to man the soul he deposited with Him, and does not withhold it on account of the debts that man owes Him, demonstrates the greatness of the Ribono shel Olam’s faithfulness—"great is Your faithfulness" (*rabbah emunatecha*). From this we *derive* and learn that a person, too, must be a person of faithfulness in this same manner; it is forbidden for him to withhold his fellow’s deposit on account of debts that the depositor owes him.

The concept of *Modeh Ani*, etc., according to the *Sod* (mystical) dimension of Torah: "King who lives and endures" (*Melech chai v'kayam*) refers to the level of *Malchus* as it is unified with the level of *Yesod* [for "King" points to the *Sefirah* of *Malchus*, and "lives and endures" points to the *Sefirah* of *Yesod*]. This is the meaning of "King who lives and endures, who has returned my soul to me"—that the return of the soul comes from the level of *Malchus* as it is unified with the level of *Yesod*.

**11.** The aforementioned explanation, which follows the four dimensions of *Pardes*—*Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah, Chayah*—within Torah, explains specific matters (relative to the "great principle" therein) regarding the acknowledgement of *Modeh Ani*. Then comes the Torah of Chassidus—the *Yechidah* of Torah—and provides a general explanation, explaining the essential point within *Modeh Ani* that comes from the level of *Yechidah* in man.

The explanation according to the Torah of Chassidus regarding *Modeh Ani*, etc., is as follows: The beginning of the daily order is with *Modeh Ani*, which is recited before *netilas yadayim*, even with impure hands, because all the impurities in the world cannot defile the *Modeh Ani* of a Yid. It is possible that there may be a deficiency in this matter or that matter—but his *Modeh Ani* always remains in its completeness.

This concept is specifically due to the *Yechidah*: Regarding the four levels of *Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah, Chayah*, the concept of a blemish or even impurity, etc., is applicable. However, from the perspective of the *Yechidah* in the soul, which is always unified with His Essence, may He be blessed, no blemish or impurity, *chas v'shalom*, is applicable, and it remains always in its completeness.

(And this is the inner reason why no name from the seven names that may not be erased is mentioned in *Modeh Ani*; since *Modeh Ani* comes from the level of *Yechidah*—the essence of the soul—the acknowledgement of the essence of the soul is to His Essence, may He be blessed, which "is not grasped by any name".)

## 12

This explanation is therefore stated specifically in Chassidus. Since the four parts of Pardes—which correspond to the *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah* (of Torah)—do not *explain* the concept of the *yechidah*. We even find something more than this in the Zohar —even though it is the "Sod" (secret) part of Torah—which counts and details the levels of the soul: "nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, and neshamah of the neshamah." The level of *yechidah* is not counted, as it is included within the "neshamah of the neshamah"—the level of *chayah*.

However, in Chassidus—the *yechidah* of Torah—the concept of the *yechidah* is explained, and at length.

And since the *yechidah* is the essence of the soul from which the four levels of *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah* are drawn, the explanation in Chassidus (which clarifies the essential point of "Modeh Ani" that comes from the level of the *yechidah*) also enlivens and clarifies the aforementioned four explanations in the Pardes of Torah (which explain the details of "Modeh Ani"), as follows:

**12.** The vitality that Chassidus introduces into the "Peshat" (literal) part of "Modeh Ani" (that the gratitude is for "that You have returned my soul within me") is the emphasis on the interpretation of "my soul"—the soul that is *mine*. That is to say, the entire gratitude is for the return of the soul of a Jew. If they were to return a soul to him, but a different one (that of a non-Jew or a servant)—even though he would then also emerge from the category of (one-sixtieth of) death to life—he would not be grateful. For from the perspective of the illumination of the *yechidah*, he feels that "life" means specifically Jewish life.

## 13

[The vitality of *Adam* (for "you are called Adam", and "the spirit of the children of man that ascends upward"), and the physical vitality *in and of itself* [the vitality of the animal (the nation that is like a donkey, or—is called a donkey), and "the spirit of the animal that descends downward"]—in his estimation, these are not life, and he has no reason to be grateful for this.

Even though physical life is also an extremely important reality—especially that of the *medaber* (the speaking species)—all of Yisrael are children of kings. The very concept of "life" for a prince is his connection with his father, the King. If they were to grant him life but sever him from his father the King, and place him in a barn to live the life of an animal, he would not only fail to find pleasure in this vitality, but on the contrary, he would loathe and despise such a life, and so on.

[As is known, the story of the Chossid Rabbi Yekusiel Liepler. When the Alter Rebbe wanted to bless him with long life, he said: "Do not give me 'peasant years'—years that have eyes but do not see, and ears but do not hear; one does not see Elokus and one does not hear Elokus". Seemingly, this is puzzling: when a person is given a gift, especially a great gift, there is no place for him to say that he does not want it unless the gift is even greater. Since long life in itself is a very great thing (and especially since, as mentioned above, the pleasure of vitality includes all the pleasures in the world), how could R' Yekusiel set conditions on the blessing?

But the explanation for this is that R' Yekusiel’s condition was not that they should *add* to the blessing, but rather that the long life should be a reality of "days of life." For it was fundamentally ingrained in him that the entire reality of vitality is the seeing and hearing of Elokus. Therefore, he stipulated "not peasant years," because days and years in which one does not see or hear Elokus were not considered a reality to him at all; on the contrary, he *loathed* such days and years.]

This feeling comes specifically from the revelation of the *yechidah*: regarding the four levels of *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah*, since they are defined by a certain configuration (intellect, emotions, etc.), they possess a matter of their own, and a matter of

13. Just as in the realm of Pshat, regarding the Modeh Ani, Chassidus does not *add* another *interpretation*—beyond the simple interpretation (for even according to Chassidus, the interpretation of Modeh Ani as it is understood simply is an acknowledgment that You have returned my soul to me)—but rather clarifies and illuminates this very interpretation by emphasizing *what* the soul (vitality) of a Jew is and *what* it is that he is acknowledging, so too is it in the realms of Remez, Drush, and Sod regarding Modeh Ani: Chassidus illuminates and clarifies these very interpretations.

The Remez of the return of the soul in the morning to Techiyas HaMeisim (without the explanation of Chassidus) is, on the face of it, a very distant allusion. This is because (in addition to the fact that sleep is only one-sixtieth of death and not actual death) the entire novelty in the return of the soul after sleep is merely in the *connection* of the soul to the body. Yet, the novelty in Techiyas HaMeisim will be in the body and soul *themselves*: before the resurrection, only the Luz bone will remain of the body, and from this it is understood that in the soul as well (since the soul and the body are, in general, in a state of value and connection to one another) there will be a fundamental change. At the time of the resurrection, a complete body will be built from this bone, and similarly, the soul will also pass through several stages until it is "built" and enters his body. Consequently, the allusion of the return of the soul to Techiyas HaMeisim is merely a superficial sign.

Then comes the Torah of Chassidus and explains that even in the return of the soul, the novelty is not only in the connection of the soul to the body, but also in the body and soul *themselves* (which is why they said that every morning a person becomes a *new* creation). For at every single moment, the entire creation is brought into being anew, exactly as it was during the Maaseh Bereishis. (Nevertheless, Techiyas HaMeisim is alluded to specifically in the return of the soul in the morning, because the expression and revelation of the novelty of creation, which is constantly being brought into being from nothingness to existence, occurs specifically every morning.)

The true feeling of this comes specifically from the revelation of the Yechidah. Since the four levels of Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah, and Chayah are themselves situated within a "world" [Nefesh in Asiyah, Ruach in Yetzirah, Neshamah in Beriah, and Chayah in Atzilus], what is felt by them *simply* is the reality of the worlds. And the feeling that there is no reality whatsoever to the worlds, and that their entire existence is constantly being renewed from nothingness and void...

the absolute, is by way of renewal for them. And only the level of *yechidah*, which is above the worlds, feels with simplicity that all the worlds are absolute nothingness and void, and that their entire existence is a complete novelty that is renewed constantly at every single moment.

**14.** The vitality and clarity that Chassidus introduces into the required portion of *Modeh Ani* is as follows:

The prohibition against withholding a deposit to cover debts owed by the depositor (without the explanation of Chassidus) seemingly lacks understanding: Since the depositor owes him money and he has no other way to collect from him, when an opportunity arises to receive what is rightfully his, why should he not take advantage of it? And how is this different from an object stolen from him, where, if he cannot retrieve it from the thief through a *beis din*, he is permitted to tell someone else to purchase the object from the thief in order to save the stolen property through him ?

The teachings of Chassidus come and explain that all the *mitzvos*—even those that have a rationale—are essentially the *ratzon ha'elyon* (the Supreme Will) which is above rationale (for the *ratzon*, even when it comes clothed in a rationale, remains in its essence and simplicity). Therefore, even the *mitzvos* of *mishpatim* must be fulfilled out of the Creator’s command, by way of *kabbalas ol*, just like the *mitzvos* of *chukim* (except that regarding the *mitzvos* that have a rationale, since the *ratzon ha'elyon* within them has also clothed itself in a rationale—which is why they are called "mishpatim" in the *Torah*—their fulfillment must also be from the perspective of the intellectual rationale within them).

## 16

Since the essence of all mitzvos is the will of the Holy One, blessed be He—and He and His will are one—just as it is impossible to say regarding Him, may He be blessed, that He exists for the sake of some other purpose, Heaven forbid, so too His mitzvos are not for the sake of any other purpose. Rather, their entire purpose is the mitzvos themselves. Regarding our subject—the mitzvah of returning a deposit: the goal of the mitzvah is not (merely) for the benefit of the depositor (that his money be returned to him in full), but the act of returning itself is the goal and the purpose.

Therefore, even when the depositor owes him money and does not repay his debt, there is no room to say that he may withhold the deposit, for he is obligated by the mitzvah of returning it.

The true feeling of this comes specifically from the *yechidah*. For the *nefesh*, *ruach*, and *neshamah*, since their connection to the Creator is (as it were) from the perspective of their own definition, their grasp of the Divine Will is also as it is clothed in definition. Only the *yechidah*, which has no definition and no self-seeking whatsoever, and whose connection to *Elokus* is from the perspective of *Elokus* itself—only in it is the true essence of the Divine Will felt as it is in its simplicity, where its purpose is the will itself.

**15.** It is the same regarding the "secret" aspect of *Modeh Ani*:

The interpretation in Kabbalah that "a living and enduring King" refers to the *sefirah* of *Malchus* as it is unified with the *sefirah* of *Yesod*, and that it is from this level that the return of the soul comes—beyond the fact that Chassidus explains and clarifies this with intellectual explanations (as with all matters in Kabbalah, whose explanation in the intellect is specifically through Chassidus, as mentioned at the end of Chapter 1), there is more: through the explanation of Chassidus, the inner content of the interpretation is felt, and the matter lives in an entirely different way.

The explanation of this interpretation according to Chassidus:

The *sefirah* of *Malchus* is the level of *Elokus* that relates to the world, and therefore the constraints of space and time are drawn from it (and relate to it). The *sefirah* of *Yesod* is the level of *Elokus* that is above the constraint of relating to the world; therefore, in *all* the *middos* above the *sefirah* of *Malchus*, the constraints of space and time do not apply (and through the revelation of these *middos*, time and space are nullified from their existence).

## 17

(entirely). The concept of the *yichud* of *Malchus* with *Yesod* is the revelation of *Ohr Ein Sof*—which is above the worlds—within the dimension of *Elokus* that is enclothed within the world.

This is why the return of the *neshamah* comes from the dimension of "King who is living and enduring": The return of the *neshamah* every morning is a matter of a *new* creation (as mentioned in section 13). This *chiddush* of *yesh me'ayin*—even though in practice it comes specifically from the dimension of *Malchus* (for from the perspective of the *middos* that are above the *middah* of *Malchus*, there would be no existence of *yesh* at all)—nevertheless, the power within *Malchus* to create *yesh me'ayin* comes specifically from the *Atzmus Ohr Ein Sof* that is above any categorization of relationship to the worlds. Consequently, it is not within the definitions of "nothing" and "something," and therefore it has the capacity to make that which "is not" into that which "is." This is in contrast to the dimension of light that relates to the worlds, which is, as it were, defined by these parameters.

This recognition comes to a person from the dimension of *Yechidah* within them. There are two aspects to this: (a) The recognition of *Atzmus Ohr Ein Sof* is possible only from this dimension of the *neshamah*, which is unified with His *Atzmus* (unlike the four dimensions of *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah, chayah*, whose relationship is to the *Elokus* that is within the worlds). (b) Furthermore, the true *recognition* of the *chiddush* of *yesh me'ayin*—that one should *recognize* (and not merely understand) that there is no place for the *yesh* on its own, and that its entire existence is newly created from *Elokus*—which compels the conclusion that the *chiddush* comes from *Atzmus Ohr Ein Sof*, comes specifically from the dimension of *Yechidah*, as mentioned in section 13.

**16.** The explanations in *Chassidus* regarding the four parts of *Pardes* within "Modeh Ani" are interconnected:

The person’s recognition that the *chiddush* of *yesh me'ayin* comes from *Atzmus Ohr Ein Sof*—which is above the worlds—is the explanation of the "Sod" (secret) part of "Modeh Ani." This strengthens the recognition that all the worlds are constantly being renewed *from absolute nothingness*—the explanation of the "Remez" (allusion) part of "Modeh Ani." For if the formation of the worlds were through the *Sefirah* of *Malchus* on its own, since the worlds occupy space relative to it, there would not be a renewal of their *entire* existence (but rather, it would be similar to how the *neshamah* enlivens the body, where even without the vitality from the *neshamah*, it still possesses existence). However,

18

Since creation comes from the *Atzmus* of *Ohr Ein Sof*—before which the worlds occupy no space at all—there is no room for their own independent standing; their entire *essential* existence is constantly renewed by the *Ohr Ein Sof*.

Consequently, one arrives at the recognition of unity in the sense of "there is nothing else besides Him," where all worlds "are truly nothing and null before the *Ribono shel Olam*, and are not even called by a name, not even the name 'else' (*od*), which implies something secondary". Because this is so, it is felt by him quite simply that the vitality of his flesh is not an existence at all (not even an existence in a secondary sense), and "existence" is only—connection to *Elokus*, as explained above in the *Chassidus* interpretation of the "peshat" level of *Modeh Ani*.

From this recognition that "there is nothing else besides Him," it is clear to him that in fulfilling the command of the *Ribono shel Olam*, it is impossible to intermingle (even as a secondary factor) any other reason or purpose; their entire fulfillment must be solely because of the Creator's will. This is as explained above in the *Chassidus* interpretation of the "drush" level of *Modeh Ani*.

17. The level of *yechidah*, being essential, does not negate the *nefesh, ruach, neshamah, chayah*; on the contrary, it is the very "essence" of all the individual levels. (This is one of the differences between a "revelation" and an "essence": a "revelation" is a specific matter, and therefore things that are not that revelation—things that are not "it"—are negated by it. An "essence," however, is the selfhood of every single thing.) Therefore, one of the primary emphases in *Chassidus* is the unification of the particulars with the essence. This emphasis, too, will be better understood through the vitality that *Chassidus* infuses into all four parts of *Pardes* within *Modeh Ani*:

Regarding the *Sefiros*—the "sod" level: Even the level of *Malchus* itself is unified with the *Sefirah* of *Yesod*, until they become one thing—"a living and enduring King." Consequently, the matter of unity—that "there is nothing else besides Him"—does not mean that the existence of the worlds does not exist; rather, it means that even the worlds themselves, as they exist in their reality (and are defined by the constraints of time and space), are unified in absolute unity with the *Atzmus* of *Ohr Ein Sof*.

Regarding the *mitzvos*—the "drush" level: Even the *mitzvos* that have a rationale and are called in the Torah of *emes* by the name "mishpatim" (judgments), are unified with, and their essence is, the will that transcends rationale.

Regarding creation—the "remez" level: Even regarding the matters of the world as they appear to our eyes—where creation has already become an independent existence (which is why we do not *feel* the constant renewal within it...

19

At every single moment, the renewal of a person (and the world) that occurs each morning is a renewal of the very essence of their existence, just like the innovation of the act of creation.

Regarding human emotion—the "simple" level: even in a person’s gratitude for their vitality (upon awakening from sleep), they feel that their entire vitality is defined by the fact that they are a Jew.

Based on the above (at the beginning of section 17), we can understand the words of our Rebbes, who stated regarding various specific virtues of Chassidus that "this is the essence of Chassidus" (as mentioned at the beginning of the sicha). For even though these virtues are merely branches of the essence of Chassidus (as mentioned there), it is precisely through them that its (very) essence is expressed. This is because the indication of an "essential" matter is that it "permeates," penetrates, and is present in all details, down to the smallest detail (as mentioned above regarding the difference between a "revelation" and an "essence").

And according to the principle that "their beginning is embedded in their end", the more Chassidus is drawn down into lower levels—to the point of transforming the nature of the animal soul, the "outer" aspect within a person, and lower still, the refinement of one's portion in the world, the "outer" aspect outside of the person—the more its essential nature is expressed.

Therefore, the Rebbe Rayatz emphasized in many of his sichos that every concept one learns in Chassidus must be brought into actual practice, for it is precisely through actual avodah that one grasps the essence of Chassidus.

**19.** Just as the level of "Ein Sof" in Chassidus is *generally* present in all parts of the Torah (as mentioned in section 3), so too, the expression of the Ein Sof by drawing it down to the lowest level—to transform even it and make it a vessel for Elokus—is also present in the Torah as a whole (though the primary focus of this is in Chassidus).

This is what our Sages meant when they said, "I created the yetzer hara, and I created for it the Torah as an antidote". For on the face of it, this is puzzling: Of all the worlds, this world is the lowest, "than which there is no lower". And within this world itself—the yetzer hara is something regarding which the Ribono shel Olam regrets having created. And since all the worlds, even the higher worlds, are considered as nothing in comparison to the Torah (and therefore, when David praised the Torah by saying that all the upper and lower worlds depend on the precision of a single mitzvah of Torah, he was punished for it), then surely, a fortiori, it would seem there is no place to say that the creation of the Torah was in order for it to be an antidote for the yetzer hara?

## 20

Based on the above, we can understand: the very essence of Torah, which is unified with the *Atzmus* of Hashem, is expressed precisely through the fact that it is a "spice" for the *yetzer hara*. This is because the power to "spice" (refine) the *yetzer hara* and transform it into *tov* comes specifically from the *Atzmus* of Hashem.

The explanation for this is as follows: All revelations, even the most sublime, are defined by the parameters of *ohr* and revelation. Therefore, the reality of *ra*—which is the opposite of *ohr*—stands in opposition to them. Consequently, they lack the ability to transform it into *tov* (they can only fight it, or at most, cause it to be nullified entirely). Only the *Atzmus* of Hashem, which is simple with the ultimate simplicity and is removed from all limitations—and to which no opposition can possibly exist—has the ability to change it and transform it into *tov*.

This is also the meaning of what is said regarding the aspect of Torah that is unified with the *Atzmus* of Hashem: "She was *mesachekes* (playing/rejoicing) before Him." The *sechok* (rejoicing/taanug) that is Above is brought about through the clarification and rectification of *choshech* —the transformation of darkness into light. And this is "she was *mesachekes* before Him": specifically through the aspect of transforming darkness into light, the Torah "plays"—meaning, its inwardness is expressed as it is unified with the inwardness of *Ohr Ein Sof*—"before Him."

**20.** These two matters in *Toras HaChassidus* (and similarly in the Torah as a whole)—(a) its penetration into all parts of the *nefesh, ruach, neshamah, chayah, yechidah*, to the point that it acts even within the *nefesh habehamis* and the world, and (b) the fact that the expression of its essence is specifically through its action within the *nefesh habehamis* and the world—have their parallels (as do all matters in *Chassidus*) in the *nigleh* (the revealed dimension of Torah) and in *halacha*. Furthermore, this *halacha* in the *nigleh* is augmented in its explanation by the aforementioned matters in *Chassidus*:

"A person’s four cubits acquire for him in any place" : in an alleyway, or on the sides of the public domain.

## 21

(And regarding *gittin* and *kiddushin* — even in a *reshus harabim*, according to several opinions). This *kinyan* is a *takanas chachamim*, enacted so that "they will not come to quarrel"[118].

Although the enactment is that an *object* placed within a person’s *daled amos* should be acquired by him, nevertheless, in order to facilitate the *kinyan* of the object, the *chachamim* granted him the *daled amos* themselves and made them like his *chatzer*. Through this, they automatically acquire for him the object placed within them by way of *kinyan chatzer* (and therefore, a *katan* who does not have a *chatzer* also does not have *daled amos*). However, this granting of the *daled amos* by the *chachamim* is only for the purpose of acquiring an object placed within them, and not for any other matter.

And although the *kinyan* of *daled amos* is based on the principle of *kinyan chatzer*, it is nonetheless superior to *kinyan chatzer*. For a *chatzer* that is not guarded (even when one is standing close enough to the object that he could lean over and take it) does not acquire until he says, "My field has acquired for me". Whereas *daled amos* (even though they are not guarded) do not require one to say "has acquired, etc.", to the point that they acquire for a person even without his knowledge or consent at all. This requires explanation: Since the entire *kinyan* of *daled amos* is based on the principle of *kinyan chatzer* (and regarding the *daled amos* themselves, they are inferior to a *chatzer*, as mentioned above, that they are not acquired by him except for the purpose of acquiring the object), how is it possible that the *kinyan* of *daled amos* should be more powerful than *kinyan chatzer*?

## 22

The explanation for this is as follows: The level of *yechidah* within a person extends into his *daled amos*. This is why one’s *daled amos* acquires property for a person without his explicit statement or conscious intent, for the level of *yechidah* is not touched by the matters of *da'as* and intellect. However, the extension of the *yechidah* into one’s *daled amos* is for the purpose of acquiring an object—the acquisition of a physical item *outside* of the person. The person’s own powers, in all *four* levels of *nefesh, ru'ach, neshamah,* and *chayah* within him—the *four* amos —are unable to draw down and reveal the level of *yechidah*. It is only when these powers come to clarify some physical object outside of the person—acquiring an object for the person’s domain (in order to clarify and refine it by using it for the sake of Heaven)—that the level of *yechidah* is specifically revealed within them.

**21.** Based on all of the above, it becomes clear why the preparation and the "vessel" for the coming of Moshiach is specifically the spreading of the wellsprings outward (*hafatzas hama'ayanos chutzah*). For:

(In addition to the fact that since the revelation of Moshiach will be in *all* matters of the world, until "all flesh will see," it follows that the preparation and vessel for this is the spreading of the wellsprings *everywhere*—down to the lowest level, the *chutza*—)

The expression of the essence of the wellsprings of Chassidus occurs when they spread "outward" specifically.

## 23

As long as the *maayanos* remain only on the "inside," their true essence is not yet expressed. And since the preparation and the vessel for the coming of Moshiach is the *mahus* of Chassidus, it is therefore necessary to disseminate the *maayanos* specifically "outward"—until they transform the "outside" into *maayanos* themselves. Through this, the essence of the *maayanos* is expressed, and then "the Master comes"—this is King Moshiach.

In the words of the Alter Rebbe: "For then the *gashmius* (meaning that it will remain *gashmius*, but it will be refined) of the body and the world will be refined... (and the light of Hashem will shine for Yisrael 'without any *levush*') and from the abundance of the illumination for Yisrael, the darkness of the nations will also be illuminated, as it is written, etc., 'all flesh together,' etc., 'all the inhabitants of the world,' etc. (And he explains that) this ultimate perfection of the days of Moshiach and the resurrection of the dead... depends on our actions and our *avodah* throughout the entire duration of the *galus*, for the cause of the reward of a *mitzvah* is the *mitzvah* itself."

In the true and complete *geulah*, imminently.

– Notes of one of the listeners –

**A.** It is stated in the Zohar: "And in the six hundredth year of the sixth millennium, the gates of wisdom above will open, and the fountains of wisdom below, etc. And your sign is: 'In the six hundredth year of Noach’s life... all the fountains of the great deep were broken open.'" The simple meaning of the holy Zohar is that at that time, the gates of wisdom in general, and the gates of the wisdom of Torah in particular, would be opened. We indeed saw this at that time regarding wisdom in general. And regarding the wisdom of Torah, we merited in those days the revelation of *pnimiyus haTorah*—the part of Torah that had been hidden and sealed until then.

For initially, this wisdom was revealed and known only to "select individuals, and even then, only in a hidden manner, not in public", as they concealed it from the eyes and study of the majority of the Jewish people. It was only beginning with the Arizal that it was said, "that specifically in these final generations, it is permitted and a mitzvah to reveal this wisdom". However, even then, it had not yet come to a state of revelation and widespread dissemination—in the manner of "they shall be sustained"—until the Baal Shem Tov appeared. Through him and by his hand, this wisdom began to be revealed and disseminated widely among all of Israel, in accordance with the response of Melech HaMoshiach (to the Baal Shem Tov's question, "When will the Master come?"): "When your fountains spread outward". This was especially true after the revelation of Chassidus Chabad by the Alter Rebbe, until it reached the level of "they shall be sustained."

## 26

This means that the teachings of Chassidus were clothed in the garments of understanding and comprehension (nourishment) of the human intellect, and from that time on, it has been spreading in a manner of "increasing and brightening, increasing and brightening."

Seemingly, this is perplexing:
After all, the final generations are the time of the "footsteps of Moshiach, when the Sukkah of Dovid has fallen to the level of the feet and heels," and it is an "orphaned generation," for the darkness within it is doubled and redoubled—so how is it possible that specifically these generations should merit such lofty revelations that previous generations did not merit, not even the generations of the Tannaim and Amoraim, the Geonim and the Rishonim, etc.?

However, we find a similar matter regarding the Torah in general:
Our Sages said, "Everything that a veteran student is destined to innovate in the Torah was already given to Moshe at Sinai." On the face of it, this too is perplexing: Since the innovations of the student were already given to Moshe at Sinai, why were they not revealed in all those generations, until this veteran student came and innovated this matter?

The explanation is:
It is written in the Torah, "He has made everything beautiful in its time." Our Sages said that Adam HaRishon was worthy of having the Torah given through him, etc., but the Holy One, Blessed be He, said, etc., "I will give it to his children"—meaning that every thing that the Holy One, Blessed be He, does at a specific time, that is the "beautiful" time, the time that is appropriate and necessary for this thing. From this it is understood that until the time arrives when this matter in Torah is innovated by the veteran student, the world was not yet in need of this matter, and therefore it was not revealed.

For Torah is from the word *hora'ah* (instruction), whose purpose is to instruct the Children of Israel in the path they should walk and the avodah they should perform to refine and purify the world—the purpose of the descent into this world. And since the world was not yet in need of the clarification provided by this instruction that comes through the matter innovated by the student, the innovation was not *revealed*. And when the time arrives in which the veteran student...

## 27

The very fact that a student is able to innovate in Torah is proof that this is the "appropriate" time for that innovation; this is because the state of the world is such that the instruction derived from the student’s innovation is necessary and essential.

The same applies to our current situation: all the revelations of the future, at the coming of Moshiach, are "dependent upon our deeds and our avodah throughout the duration of the golus". This does not refer only to the avodah of this final golus. Rather, since our Sages have said that "a mitzvah is called only by the name of the one who completes it," it is understood that with the completion of the avodah and the birurim of this golus, the purpose and essence of all the exiles will also be completed and concluded (for the redemption from this golus is a redemption after which there is no further golus). It is understood from this that our avodah, which brings about the end of the fourth golus and the subsequent redemption, also effects an elevation in the three previous redemptions.

The conclusion to be drawn from this is that precisely because these final generations are the "ikvesa d'meshicha" — the time of the clarification and conclusion of the fourth and final golus — a greater responsibility is placed upon them, and their avodah must be more elevated, performed with greater strength and intensity than that of the previous generations and exiles. For our avodah is the final preparation for the complete redemption: to transform the double and redoubled darkness into double and redoubled light.

And since "the Holy One, blessed be He, does not act tyrannically with His creatures", and only demands according to the strengths He has granted them, it is precisely in these generations that the hitherto hidden and inner parts of Torah are revealed (which earlier generations did not merit). Through these revelations, and by means of them, special strengths are bestowed from above for this elevated avodah—for the Torah is "oz v'tushiya" ; it is what provides the strength to accomplish great things in one's avodah.

Furthermore: in addition to the fact that this great avodah, in and of itself, requires special strengths...

## 28

It is well known that the more essential the avodah is, and the closer it brings us to the desired ultimate goal, the more the kelipah and the yetzer hara intensify their opposition to hinder it. Since the avodah of the *final* generations is directly related to the completion and perfection of all the birurim, the opposition of the yetzer hara against it is with greater force and intensity—seeking to prevent the worker from fulfilling his duty. Consequently, for this reason as well, these generations require special strengths, so that through our avodah we can fulfill and complete the will of Hashem, for "Hashem desired to have a dwelling place in the lower worlds".

**2.** Based on the above, it can be said that just as this applies generally to the difference between the final generations and the generations that preceded them, the same is true within the final generations themselves. In every generation that draws closer to the coming of Moshiach, just as the responsibility grows, so too does the revelation of the lofty matters of Torah increase compared to the generations that preceded it. We see that this is indeed the case regarding the revelation of pnimiyus haTorah—from generation to generation it is *revealed* more, and descends and is enclothed more in the garments of understanding, comprehension, and grasp.

In an orderly fashion, with graded explanations and analogies that clarify, and so on.
