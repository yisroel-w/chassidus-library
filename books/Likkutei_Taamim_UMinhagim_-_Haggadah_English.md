# Likkutei Taamim UMinhagim for the Haggadah of Pesach

## Laws of Pesach

**Laws of Pesach.** These were the **first** to be printed from the Shulchan Aruch of Rabbeinu HaZaken (by his sons, Shklov, 5573), and they are also the first (together with the Laws of Tzitzis) to have been written and completed by Rabbeinu HaZaken back when he was "sitting in the seat of wisdom" in Mezritch (Introduction of the Mitteler Rebbe to the Shulchan Aruch, Introduction of the Rabbis, sons of the Gaon the author, of blessed memory). The Laws of Talmud Torah (and the Order of Blessings) were printed by the Alter Rebbe, but as a separate matter and not as part of the Shulchan Aruch.

## Shabbos HaGadol

It is called this because a great miracle occurred on that day (Tosafos, s.v. *Ve’oto Shabbos*, Pesachim 68b; Tur Shulchan Aruch, beginning of 430). In an old manuscript Machzor, it is found that the Shabbos before Shavuos is also called Shabbos HaGadol, as is the Shabbos before Rosh Hashanah (*Kesser Shem Tov*, Vol. 3). It may be said that they applied this name to these Shabbosos (even though the aforementioned reason does not apply to them) because of their similarity to the Shabbos before Pesach, as they also occur before a Yom Tov.

When Shabbos HaGadol falls on Erev Pesach — and only then — we read the Haftorah of *Ve’arvah* (Be’er Heitev, 430:1), because in that passage it is written, "Bring the tithe into the storehouse," and Erev Pesach (of the fourth and seventh years of the Shmittah cycle) is the time for the removal of tithes (*Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, end of 430; see there).

On Shabbos HaGadol, after Minchah, we recite *Avadim Hayinu* until *l’chaper al kol avonoseinu*, because that was the beginning of the Geulah and the miracles (*Shulchan Aruch*, end of 430). This is the custom (contrary to what is written in *Shaar HaKollel*).

## Searching for and Burning Chametz

**The custom to place pieces of chametz, etc., before the search.** According to the strict letter of the law, there is no need to place them at all, for the mitzvah is to search for the chametz, and even if one finds nothing, the mitzvah has been fulfilled properly (*Shibolei HaLeket*, Siman 206, in the name of *Responsa of the Geonim*; *Kol Bo* in the name of *Rav Hai Gaon*). However, the custom to place them has already spread, and "the custom of Israel is Torah" (*Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, end of Siman 432). See *Chok Yaakov* for the reasons behind this custom. One may add another reason: so that during the *bitul* (nullification) of the day, one can say "all leaven and chametz **that I have seen**," and also so that one does not forget the obligation of *biur* (burning) and will not need to burn the utensil used for the search, as discussed at the end of Siman 445.

**Hard chametz.** So that it does not crumble (*Shulchan Aruch*, Siman 432).

**According to Kabbalah, one should place ten pieces.** (*Mishnas Chassidim*, Siman of the Arizal). In the book *Bris HaLevi* by the Maharash Alkabetz, he writes that this is alluded to in the Gemara (*Pesachim* 10a): "He placed ten, etc." (*Shaar HaKollel*).

**The custom of the Rebbe’s house.** Ten pieces of hard chametz are placed in various places in the house, each wrapped in a piece of paper. In *Kesser Shem Tov*, Vol. 3, it is written that this is also the custom of the Sephardim in Eretz Yisrael, Syria, Turkey, and Egypt. The reason is so that it does not crumble, similar to what is stated in *Shulchan Aruch*, Siman 432.

One searches by the light of a wax candle (*Shulchan Aruch*, Siman 433) and using a bird feather. The searcher places the chametz that he finds into a small paper bag. At the conclusion of the search, he places this bag, the feather, and the remnants of the candle—if there are any—into the hollow of a wooden spoon, wraps everything in paper (but not the handle of the spoon, which remains unwrapped), and fastens it with a string that he winds around the paper several times and ties.

**"Blessed... concerning the burning of chametz"** (*Pesachim* 7b). Even though at this moment he is not yet burning it, nevertheless, since the search is not the ultimate purpose of the mitzvah, and immediately after the search he nullifies and declares ownerless all the chametz that remained and was not found, this nullification is considered *biur* (burning/removal). Furthermore, the *biur* that takes place on the fourteenth day is also covered by this blessing (*Tur* and *Beis Yosef*; *Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, Siman 432).

**"Concerning the burning" (al biur).** And not "to burn" (l'vaer). See Rambam, *Hilchos Berachos*, Chapter 11, Law 15, and the commentaries on the Shas there.

**One must search by the light of a candle** because the light of a candle is effective for searching (*Pesachim* 8a).

**One should be careful to search first in the room adjacent to the place where the blessing was heard.** Because walking from house to house is considered an interruption between the blessing and the beginning of the performance of the mitzvah (*Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, Siman 432, Subsection 8).

**One must nullify after the search.** Nullification at this time is an enactment of the Sages of the Gemara, so that one has a set time... and in later generations, they added that one should nullify it during the day as well (ibid., Siman 434, Subsection 13).

**"All leaven" (Kol Chamira).** In the Yerushalmi (*Pesachim*, Chapter 2, Halacha 2), the text of the nullification is in the Holy Tongue (Hebrew), but the Geonim enacted it in Aramaic for the sake of the unlearned (*Darkei Moshe*; *Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, Siman 434, Subsection 8). It is found—with variations—in Rashi (*Pesachim* 6b), Rif (ibid. 3a), Rosh (ibid., Chapter 1, Subsection 9), *Bahag*, *Machzor Vitry*, the *Yotzer* for Shabbos HaGadol, and others. Our Rebbe’s text is precise according to what the *Bach* wrote in Siman 434—cited in *Chok Yaakov*—and so it is in the *Shaar HaShamayim* of the Shelah. Now, in the *Bach* and *Shaar HaShamayim* they wrote: "all leaven and **all** chametz," but our Rebbe copied "all leaven **and** chametz," and so it is in *Chok Yaakov* and *Aruch HaShulchan* (ibid.), in the Arizal’s text, in the Yaavetz’s text, and others. Seemingly, according to the Sages (Moed Katan 26a), "Say 'until they are all'—he said 'on, on'—he interrupted the matter," here too, since even if only one of the two exists, either chametz or leaven, one nullifies it, it should be: "all leaven **and all** chametz." It may be said that the word "all" itself indicates that—in whatever manner it may be—one is nullifying it. See *Kol Yaakov* regarding the hints for the word "and chametz" with a *vav*.

**"And chametz" (v'chamia).** Even though "leaven" (chamira) includes chametz, it is appropriate for one who is meticulous in his deeds to mention it explicitly (*Bach*, *Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, Siman 434, Subsection 9).

**"That I have not seen" (d'la chamitei).** So it is in the *Siddur*, *Kol Yaakov*, and others. And not like the *Bach* and *Chok Yaakov* (Siman 40, Rash Raskov) who wrote "that I have not seen" (*d'la chazitei*)—and the *Aruch HaShulchan* was not precise in copying the language of the *Bach*. See *Kol Yaakov* for the reason according to *sod* (mystical tradition).

**"And that I do not know of,"** because the chametz that one sets aside for tomorrow is not nullified (*Bach*, Siman 434).

**"Like the dust of the earth,"** this excludes gold, which is also called dust, as it is said (Job 28:6), "And it has dust of gold" (*Chok Yaakov*, *Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, Siman 434, Subsection 7).

**Searching for chametz.** Admur HaZaken went to Mezritch for the first time in the year 5524 and stayed there (at that time) until before Pesach 5525. When he returned home—on the 13th of Nissan of that year—he did not eat (he did not fast, for it is forbidden to fast, rather he simply did not eat) because he was occupied with the preparation for the search for chametz, so that all the *kavanos* (meditations) he received in Mezritch regarding the search for chametz would be implemented in the actual search. The search lasted all night, and he only had one room at that time (*Sichos* of the Rebbe Rayatz, Pesach 5698).

At that time, our Rebbe explained the Mishnah (beginning of *Pesachim*): "On the night of the fourteenth, one searches for chametz by the light of a candle"—the 13th (yud-gimmel) has the numerical value of "one" (echad), which is the concept of *Daas*; in this, a search is not applicable (or: is not needed?). The 14th (yud-dalet) is the concept of the *Middos* (the seven *Middos* of the *Nefesh HaBahamis* and *Nefesh HaElokis*); there, one must "search for chametz," and the search is by the light of the candle, "The candle of Hashem is the soul of man." The search is in the holes and crevices, which is why it must be by the light of a candle, for a torch is the level of *Atzilus*, and there "no evil shall dwell with You"; a flame is *Beriah*, and the light of a candle is *Yetzirah*, for *Asiyah* is the category of darkness (ibid. See also *Shelah*, *Maseches Shabbos* 136b; *Klal HaYedios*, entry "Torch"; *Maamar* "Ohr L'Yud-Dalet" 5708).

**On the 14th day at the fifth hour.** See *Shulchan Aruch*, Siman 449, regarding how to calculate these hours.

**"A torch by itself"** (*Tur*, Siman 445; *Maharshal*, *Maharil*, *Magen Avraham*), for we issue a decree lest he burn it after the time when it is forbidden to derive benefit from it (*Beis Yosef*).

**"That I have seen," etc., "that I have seen."** It may be said: *chamitei* (that I have seen) refers to the time of the search. *Chazitei* (that I have seen) refers to ever (in general) (*Bach*, Siman 434).

**"And one should burn ten pieces."** It is repeated again regarding the ten pieces, for both in the search and in the burning there must be ten pieces (see *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar HaChametz*, end of Chapter 4, and *Mishnas Chassidim* there).

**"May it be Your will."** In the *Siddur* of the Arizal, it is "My G-d and the G-d of my fathers" in the singular. Perhaps the reason he changed it to the plural "Our G-d and the G-d of our fathers" is that through this, the prayer is heard (*Berachos* 30a; *Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken*, *Orach Chaim*, Subsection 10, Paragraph 4). Especially since the rest of this entire prayer is also in the plural. See *Magen Avraham* and later authorities, *Orach Chaim*, beginning of Siman 46. It is difficult to understand, for in the prayer "May it be Your will... from the brazen-faced," and at the end of the order of *Kiddush Levanah*, our Rebbe’s version is also in the singular, and so it is in *Siddur* 315 (and in *Siddur* Rashar: "May it be Your will... from the brazen-faced" is in the plural).

And what is written in *Shaar HaKollel* regarding "May it be Your will... from the brazen-faced," that the reason is because it is not in a congregation—this is difficult to understand, for the "May it be Your will" for the burning of chametz is also not in a congregation.

**"Shall be consumed in smoke"** (b'ashan tichleh). And so it is in the prayers of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur—with a *beis*, according to the language of the verse (Psalms 37:20), "They shall be consumed in smoke, they shall be consumed."

**"With a spirit of burning and with a spirit of judgment."** The language of the verse (Isaiah 4:4) is "with a spirit of judgment and with a spirit of burning." The fact that they changed it and placed "burning" before "judgment" is understood: regarding the washing and purifying of the sins of the daughters of Zion in the verse, He will wash them with a "spirit of judgment," which is *rachamim* (mercy), as Rashi explains regarding suffering, that this is like a father disciplining his son (see *Iggeres HaKodesh* by our Rebbe HaZaken, Siman 22). And that which remains without purification, He will burn from the world with a "spirit of burning." But here, regarding the *chitzonim* and *kelipos* that oppress the *Shechinah*, He will burn them with a "spirit of burning," and all the sparks of holiness from which the *chitzonim* draw sustenance (see *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar HaChametz*, Chapter 1) He will wash with a "spirit of judgment" to purify them (*Shaar HaKollel*).

**"In those days and at this time."** And so it is in *Siddur* Rashar. It is difficult to understand, for in all prayers the version is "at this time" (*bazman*), and "and at this time" (*u'bazman*) with the addition of a *vav* is not found in other places. In *Siddur Kol Yaakov* here, it is correctly "at this time" without a *vav*.

**Burning of chametz.** One who eats chametz on Pesach is like one who worships idols (*Zohar*, Vol. 3, 182a). The Arizal wrote that one who is careful even regarding a minute amount of chametz on Pesach is guaranteed that he will not sin all year (*Aruch HaShulchan*, beginning of Siman 457). Perhaps the intention is that no harm will befall him inadvertently, for he is certainly a master of his own choice even then.

## The Order of the Korban Pesach

**"And we will render for bulls" etc.** (with a slight variation in wording: *Shelah*, Tractate *Pesachim*, at the beginning; *Siddur HaAriZal*).

**"The Korban Pesach" etc. "This is the matter" etc.** (the language of *Seder HaYom* is copied there). In his *Siddur*, the Yaavetz raised several difficulties regarding this order and wrote that it should be recited in a different version. In order to resolve why our Rebbe included the language of *Seder HaYom* in all of this, one may answer the difficulties of the Yaavetz as follows:

1) That it does not specifically mention the time of the offering, nor its precedence when Erev Pesach falls on Shabbos—one can say that since the recitation of the *Seder Korban Pesach* is for the sake of "and we will render for bulls our lips," and one is not obligated to precisely time its recitation to the hour when the *Korban Pesach* was actually offered—it is sufficient that it be at a time when the offering of the *Korban Pesach* is valid, just as it is regarding the *Mincha* prayer—they did not want to mention in this version that the *Pesach* was offered at eight and a half hours, etc., because in most cases one would not be able to time its recitation to that moment, and it would be self-contradictory.

2) It does not specify upon whom the obligation falls—one can say that they did not want to specify that it applies only to those who are ritually pure and able to reach Jerusalem at the time of the *Pesach* slaughter, for if so, it would contradict the concept of "and we will render," as mentioned above.

3) It does not detail the difference in skinning the animal on Shabbos—one can say that even if you argue that its skinning on Shabbos is not the same as its skinning on a weekday (see *Nitzotzei Kesav* of the Rambam, *Hilchos Korban Pesach*, Chapter 1, Law 14), the difference is only in the **manner** of skinning, and it does not descend into such details.

4) It writes simply that the fat tail (*alyah*) is offered, without stipulating that this is regarding a sheep, for the fat tail of a goat is not offered—one can say that this is not common, as most of their *Pesachim* were from sheep, similar to the first answer **to a question of this nature** in *Tosafos* (s.v. *Me'alyah*, *Pesachim* 63b). And it still requires further study.

5) The details of roasting the *Pesach*, its de-veining, and its consumption were omitted—one can say this is because none of this pertains to the offering of the *Korban Pesach*, and it is also not at the time of the recitation of the *Seder Korban Pesach*; therefore, it mentioned them only in general terms.

6) There is no mention of the *Chagigah* of the fourteenth—one can say, as the Yaavetz himself hinted, that it was omitted because the *Chagigah* is only optional, and it does not come on Shabbos in large numbers or in a state of impurity (*Pesachim* 69b).

**"And he receives the full vessel first, and afterwards returns the empty one"** because one does not pass over mitzvos (*Pesachim* 64b).

**"They recite the Hallel"**—the Levites (*Tosefta Pesachim*, end of Chapter 4).

**"They blow"**—the Kohanim with trumpets (*Bamidbar* 10:8-10; *Tamid* Chapter 7, Mishnah 3).

**"And they give"**—it appears this is a printing error and it should be *v'nosnam* ("and they give them"), and this is how it appears in several editions.

## The Order of the Haggadah

**The Order of the Haggadah.** My father-in-law, the Rebbe, once said to me: "Yosef Yitzchak, one must think—during the Seder—that Hashem will help a person. Especially when opening the door. Do not ask for material things; ask for spiritual things" (Sichos of Pesach 5702).

**The Order of the Haggadah.** See the Mishnah in *Pesachim* (Chapter 10), the *Mechilta* on *Parshas Bo*, and the Babylonian and Jerusalem Talmud there, etc. A fixed order for the Haggadah is already found in the *Siddur* of Rav Amram Gaon (Siman 263), in the Rambam, the *Machzor Vitry*, and others. The explanation according to *Sod* (the mystical dimension) is in *Pri Etz Chaim* and the *Siddur* of the Arizal.

Every person should be trembling with awe to fulfill the words of the Sages who established the mitzvah of the Seder and the Haggadah. The matter should not be light in his eyes; even if there are several things in the Seder that seem to a person as if they are not strictly required, he should apply his intellect to fulfill them, knowing that there is nothing empty in them (*Maharil*).

There is room to say that a convert is not obligated in Pesach because neither he nor his ancestors were in Egypt, similar to the Sages' statement that a convert does not recite [the declaration of Bikkurim] because he cannot say, "that Hashem swore to our forefathers" (*Bikkurim* 1:4; but see Rambam there). Regarding this, the Torah states (*Bamidbar* 9:14), "And if a stranger dwells among you and performs the Pesach..."—"with you," he is as one of us. Our exodus from Egypt was a universal redemption, including the soul of the convert. Even though he was not there in the revealed sense, he was there in the hidden sense, for the root of holiness is one. When our forefathers were in Egypt, the totality of holiness was submerged in the impurity of Egypt; if, Heaven forbid, the totality of holiness had been submerged, there would be no remedy for the branches. Thus, the salvation of the root affects the branches as well (*Ohr HaChaim*, *Bamidbar* there).

**The name "Haggadah"** – In *Pesachim* (115b). It is called *Haggadah* because of the verse, "And you shall tell (*ve'higadta*) to your son." Others explain that *Haggadah* is a term for thanksgiving and praise to the Holy One, blessed be He, for taking us out of Egypt, as the Jerusalem Talmud translates, "I have declared (*higadti*) today to Hashem your God"—"I have offered praise this day." Rabbi Saadia Gaon translated it similarly in Arabic (*Abudraham*). Some have the version *Aggadah* with an *Aleph* (see *Dikdukei Soferim* to *Pesachim* 115-116; *Tosafos* to *Avodah Zarah* 45a, and many *Rishonim*).

**"He shall arrange on his table..."** – In *Pri Etz Chaim* and the *Siddur* of the Arizal, with a slight variation in wording. See also *Shulchan Aruch HaRav* (Siman 473, subsection 26).

**"He shall arrange on his table a bowl."** In the *Tur* and *Shulchan Aruch* (Siman 473, subsection 4), it is explained that even now, one brings the bowl only after drinking the first cup, and only his table should be set while it is still day (beginning of Siman 472). In *Kol Yaakov*, it is implied that one should arrange the bowl on the table before going to the synagogue and before reciting the *Seder Korban Pesach*. This is also somewhat implied in the *Shelah* (beginning of *Maseches Pesachim*). However, in the *Siddur* of the Rebbe Rashab, it is written: "When he comes home (after *Maariv*), he should rejoice and say... and prepare the bowl with the *matzos* as mentioned above."

We follow the custom of arranging the bowl at night, before *Kiddush*, as is also evident from our Rebbe’s *Siddur*, which writes about the bowl between the *Seder Korban Pesach* and *Kiddush*. This is also stated in the *Pri Megadim* (*Mishbetzos Zahav* 486). As for what is stated in the Mishnah (*Pesachim* 114a), "They poured him the first cup... they brought before him dipped greens," this can be explained like the commentary of Rabbeinu Chananel (cited in *Tosafos* there), that this refers to bringing the table. This was specifically in the days of the Sages of the Talmud, who had small tables in front of each person, and they would not bring the table until after *Kiddush*, which is not the case in later generations. See *Tosafos* ("She'ein") in *Pesachim* (100b).

**The Order of the Haggadah. "He shall arrange on his table."** We inherited the *Kiddush* cup of the Alter Rebbe, containing the remnants of the wine (which had solidified) from the last time he made *Kiddush*. We would place it on the table during the Seder, until it was lost due to certain circumstances. Similarly, the silver bowl that is now on the table belonged to the Alter Rebbe.

Generally, there was no wealth of silver vessels in the home of the Alter Rebbe. In the "deep years" of the Rebbe’s leadership (after he had already served as Nasi for many years), the Chassidim collected a certain sum among themselves; from each family, they took no more than a Polish coin, which was worth about three silver *eshi* (barley grains?), and with this, they bought the Alter Rebbe this bowl and a *menorah*. The Alter Rebbe also had a *hadas* (myrtle) that the Maggid gave him—"a gift from the Rebbe, the Baal Shem Tov—'and his fragrance shall be like the Lebanon'."

The Alter Rebbe also had a silver snuffbox, a gift from a *baal teshuvah* (even though the Alter Rebbe generally did not accept gifts). He said: "There is one limb in a person that is not a *baal taavah* (a slave to desire), and even that they want to stuff with desire!" He separated the lid of the box, which was polished silver, and used it as a mirror to ensure his *tefillin* were placed in their proper position (Sichos of Pesach 5702).

The aforementioned *Kiddush* cup and the wine remnants within it are from the last time the Alter Rebbe made *Kiddush* on Shabbos in the village of Piena (Sicha of 10 Iyar 5691).

**Bowl.** In the Rebbe’s house, they arrange the *matzos* on a cloth, not on a bowl, except for the Admur, who arranges the *matzos* on a silver tray.

**With three matzos.** The people of Kairouan asked Rav Sherira Gaon why we take three *matzos* on the nights of Pesach, no less and no more. He replied that it is a hint from the Torah to the three *seahs* of flour that Avraham told Sarah to use to make cakes, etc., and it is a tradition that it was Pesach time then. Others say it is a remembrance of the three "mountains of the world": Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov (*Maaseh Rokeach* 16, 58).

**"The Yisrael, and above him the Levi..."** See the explanation in *Pri Etz Chaim* and the *Siddur* of the Arizal. It is customary to place a cloth between each *matzah*, and the reason is explained in *Kol Yaakov*. This is also in *Tiferes Yisrael* (end of *Maseches Pesachim*, *Tosafos Chaim*).

**"He shall arrange... the Yisrael, and above him..."** "Our order is Yisrael, Levi, Kohen—*Y-L-K*—which also hints that he is already 'walking' (*yoleich*) in his *avodah*, and not *K-L-Y* (vessels). In general: the *matzos* hint to the *keilim* (vessels), and what is placed upon the *matzos* hints to the *oros* (lights). Therefore, the custom is to take bent *matzos*—a receptacle—for that is the essence of a true vessel" (Sichos of Pesach 5698).

**Note from the compiler:** In the *Siddur* of the Arizal, the order is *Y-L-K*. In *Pri Etz Chaim* (*Shaar Chag HaMatzos*, Chapter 6), it is *K-L-Y*. Based on the above, that the *matzos* are *keilim* and the six species are *oros*, one can also explain what many have struggled to understand: the order that the species must be placed specifically upon the *matzos*, etc.

**The *Zeroa*... the egg.** Because there must be two cooked dishes: one in memory of the *Pesach* offering, and one in memory of the *Chagigah* offering (*Pesachim* 114b). The Jerusalem Talmud states that the custom is to take a *zeroa* (shank bone) and an egg (*beia* in Aramaic), meaning: "The Merciful One *requests* (*ba'a*) to redeem us with an outstretched *arm* (*d'raa*)" (*Kol Bo*, cited in *Beis Yosef*, *Abudraham*). Some write that we take an egg as a remembrance of the mourning for the destruction of the Beis HaMikdash. This is difficult, as it is evident from the Mishnah and the Jerusalem Talmud that even during the time of the Beis HaMikdash, they took two cooked dishes, and back then they did not make a remembrance for mourning. If so, we must say that for hundreds of years they took a different species in memory of the *Chagigah*, and after the destruction, they changed this custom.

**The *Zeroa*.** The custom is to take a part of a **chicken** neck. We are also careful not to eat the *zeroa* afterwards—all this is a distancing so that there is no resemblance to the *Pesach* offering. For this reason, the Rebbe would remove almost all the meat from the bones of the *zeroa*.

**The egg.** Boiled until it is hard. Even when Pesach falls on Shabbos, we take the egg in memory of the *Chagigah* (contrary to what is written in the *Abudraham*), even though the *Chagigah* was not offered then (*Tosafos* "Achar," *Pesachim* 114b).

**"Beneath them, in the middle, the Maror."** Seemingly, the *Maror* should be on the left side, as it is the aspect of *Gevuros*. However, it is because bitterness arouses the drawing down of great mercies. When one pours out his bitter soul and arrives at a great bitterness over how far he is from Elokus, through this he arouses the aspect of great mercies (*Likkutei Torah*, *Shir HaShirim*, discourse "Kol Dodi," subsection 2).

**The Charoses.** A remembrance of the mortar in which our forefathers were enslaved. We put something tart in it (*Pesachim* 116a), a remembrance that the teeth of the Jewish people were set on edge by the harshness of the slavery (*Bach*, Siman 473). It is made from fruits to which the Congregation of Israel is compared (*Teshuvos HaGeonim*, cited in *Tosafos* "Tzarich" there), and afterwards, it is softened with red liquid, a remembrance of the blood of the first plague (Jerusalem Talmud, *Pesachim*, Chapter 10). Any food that one dips into is called *charoses* (*Machzor Vitry*).

A mnemonic for the *charoses*: *charoses* of **stone**, *charoses* of **wood**. In the Ashkenazic language: *eppel, birnen, nissen, ingber, tzimernind*, and in Hebrew: apples, pears, *kidah* (cassia), nuts, and cinnamon (*Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar Chag HaMatzos*, Chapter 6). However, for many years now, our custom has been not to take *kidah* and cinnamon due to the concern of a mixture of *chametz*.

It is called *charoses*—a remembrance of the bricks, which are made of clay (*Mordechai* to *Pesachim*, *Seder Haggadah HaKatzar*—in the name of the Jerusalem Talmud).

**The Karpas.** A *notarikon* (acronym) for *Samech Perech*, meaning 600,000 (*samech* = 60) worked at backbreaking labor (*perech*) (*Abudraham*, *Maharil*). We are accustomed to taking an onion (or a potato). See *Magen Avraham* 473:4 and the later authorities.

**"And beneath the Maror, the Chazeres that we use for the Korech."** Because of the change in terminology—at first it calls it *Maror* and afterwards *Chazeres* (this is also in *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Magen Avraham*, *Kol Yaakov*, *Siddur* of the Rebbe Rashab)—some have the custom not to take the same species for *Maror* and for *Korech*. Even in places where lettuce is found, they do not make everything from lettuce. Their reason is perhaps that they are not expert in the five species mentioned in the Mishnah (*Pesachim* 39a), so they fulfill the eating of *Maror* with a bitter vegetable. But they did not consider that if so, one should eat it without a *bracha*, as written in *Shulchan Aruch HaRav* (Siman 473:31). In truth, there is a mitzvah to seek out *chazeres* (*Pesachim* there), which is lettuce, for the essence of the mitzvah is with it (*Shulchan Aruch* 473:30), and there is no difference between *Maror* and *Korech*. The reason it changed its language is that if it had written, "And beneath the *Maror*, the *Maror* that we use for *Korech*" (similar to what is written in *Shulchan Aruch HaRav* 473:26), people would mistakenly say that for *Korech* it is better to take the species called *Maror* in the Mishnah, whereas in truth, the mitzvah is with *chazeres* (*Shaar HaKollel*). This answer is puzzling, for if so, even now there is room for error, **as for Maror** it is better to take the *Maror* of the Mishnah. To remove all error, it should have said: "Beneath them, in the middle, the **Chazeres**, and beneath... and beneath the *Chazeres*, the **Chazeres** that we use for *Korech*." Perhaps the change in terminology can be explained by the fact that the words "*Maror*" and "*Chazeres*" have several hints according to *Sod*—as written in *Pri Etz Chaim* and *Koheles Yaakov*—and the hints of the word ***Maror*** belong more to the aspect of the *Maror* on the bowl, and the hints of the word ***Chazeres*** to the *Korech*. Therefore, in *Shulchan Aruch HaRav*, which is more according to the *Nigleh* (revealed dimension), he was not particular about changing it, and this requires further study. We are accustomed to taking *chazeres* (lettuce) and *tamcha* (horseradish)—**both**—for both *Maror* and *Korech*.

We are not accustomed to wearing a *kittel* for the Seder. Likewise, we are not particular about which direction one’s seat should face.

## The Order of the Pesach Seder

**The order of the Pesach Seder: Kadesh, etc.** In Machzor Vitry (Section 65), these mnemonics are cited as being part of the "arranged order composed by R' Shlomo bar Yitzchak, of blessed memory" — Rashi (though some say one of the Baalei Tosafos, R' Shmuel of Falaise — mentioned in Or Zarua, Section 256 — composed the mnemonic *Kadesh u'Rechatz*, etc.; see Shadal in the name of a manuscript). In the *Pardes* of Rashi, in the *Hagahos Maimoniyos* (on the order of the Haggadah in the Rambam), and in the *Abudraham*, other versions of the Seder mnemonics are brought. However, in *Pri Etz Chaim* (Shaar Chag HaMatzos, Ch. 17), in *Mishnas Chassidim*, and in the Siddur of the Arizal, the order of the Pesach Seder is explained according to the mnemonic *Kadesh u'Rechatz*, etc. The Alshich, the Chida, and others explain it according to the path of *mussar*.

**U'Rechatz:** This word includes a *vav* — so that all the words in this mnemonic consist of two syllables. In the Siddur of the Rashash and *Kol Yaakov*, it is written as *Rechatz* without a *vav*; perhaps they vocalize *Rechatz* with two *patachs*.

Another explanation for the addition of the *vav* is to emphasize that one must perform *Kadesh* **and then** *Rechatz*, even for one who does not follow this practice during the rest of the year (in accordance with the opinion of the Taz and Chok Yaakov, beginning of Section 473), or that even if one washes before *Kadesh* (in accordance with the opinion of the Bach there), one must wash again (as per the Mahar).

From my father, of blessed memory, I heard an explanation according to *sod* regarding the precision of the *vav* and another change present in these two words: that they alone are stated in the imperative form (**command**) rather than as a narrative. For *Kadesh* and *Rechatz* allude to the three *gimel rishonos* of Chochma (holiness) and the three *dalet rishonos* of Binah (purity, washing), which did not experience separation or breaking, for *Atik* took them for His portion. This is not the case for the thirteen *bechinos* that remained from them. This is why *Kadesh* and *Rechatz* are connected with the *vav* of connection. Furthermore, they alone are stated in the imperative form — a hint that one must sanctify and wash the remaining thirteen *bechinos*, which are alluded to in the thirteen remaining steps: *Karpas*, etc., until *Nirtzah*, from these supernal *bechinos*.

**Rachtzah:** Some have sought to emend this to *Rechatz* (with two *patachs*), but in Machzor Vitry, Abudraham, *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Mishnas Chassidim*, the Siddur of the Arizal, the Siddur of the Shelah, the Siddur of the Yaavetz, etc., it is written as *Rachtzah*. See *Pri Etz Chaim* for the reason for this.

## Kiddush

**Atkinu seudata.** (See the *Siddur* of the Rashash and *Zohar Chadash*, *Parshas Bo* 8a, and there).

**Kadesh.** (*Mishnah, Pesachim* 114a). Our custom is to recite Kiddush standing, both on Shabbos and Yom Tov, and on Pesach. (This is according to *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar Chag HaMatzos*, ch. 14, and *Shaar HaKavanos*, ch. 6; see the *Siddur* of the Arizal. See also *Shulchan Aruch Orach Chayim* 271:10 and the commentaries there).

**Kadesh.** In every word of the *Kadesh Urchatz* mnemonic, the Alter Rebbe includes in his *Siddur* a brief explanation of the term—such as *Urchatz* meaning "washing of the hands without a blessing," and the like—except for the words *Kadesh*, *Hallel*, and *Nirtzah*. The reason for this is that there is no novelty in the meaning of these words.

Although in his *Shulchan Aruch* (beginning of ch. 473) he copied the ruling of the Rema (ibid.) and wrote, "If possible, it is good that the head of the household should not pour the wine himself, but rather someone else should pour for him, so that he may be like a master, meaning in a manner of freedom and nobility, in memory of the Exodus," it can be said that since this is only "if possible," he did not include it in his *Siddur*. (Regarding the explanation in *Shaar HaKavanos* that the law of having someone else pour is specifically for strong wines that require great effort to prepare because one must bring water for mixing—unlike our wines, where everyone pours for themselves—this requires study. For it is obvious that even with our wines, if another person pours for him, it is a matter of wealth, and therefore it is a manner of freedom and nobility in memory of the Exodus. Where do we find a distinction in this? Furthermore, if so, why did the commentators on the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe not mention that with our wines everyone pours for themselves, as they distinguished the law of our wines in ch. 204 and 272?)

The custom of the Rebbe’s household: We pour the cup, and we are not particular that someone else must pour it. One takes it in his right hand, transfers it to his left, and then sets it down—in a downward motion from above to below—onto the palm of his right hand, which is slightly cupped like a receptacle, with the four fingers raised and the thumb laid along the side. We recite Kiddush standing, and the cup is raised more than three *tefachim* above the table—this is the practice throughout the year as well.

**Kadesh.** On every Yom Tov, one is obligated to recite Kiddush by Rabbinic decree. When it falls on Shabbos, it also has a basis in the Torah. On this night, one adds to it, as it is one of the four cups that are a mitzvah by Rabbinic decree on the night of Pesach. Therefore: a) One does not recite Kiddush over bread. b) No one else can fulfill the obligation on his behalf (*Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, 472:21). c) One must exert himself to drink the four cups, even if it harms him or if he must sell his garment (*Nedarim* 49b; *Pesachim* 99b). d) One only recites Kiddush at night, not during the *Tosefet Yom Tov* (*Terumat HaDeshen*, ch. 37), because the Sages established the four cups for the time appropriate for eating matzah (*Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, ibid., sec. 2). e) One should be concerned for the opinion of those who say it is a mitzvah to drink the entire cup, or at least the majority of it (ibid., sec. 19).

**Four cups**—a mitzvah by Rabbinic decree. Even women are obligated in them, for they too were part of that miracle of the Exodus from Egypt (*Tosafot, Pesachim* 108b). We do not recite the blessing "Who has commanded us to drink the four cups," because they are interrupted (by the Haggadah and the meal) (*Kol Bo*, *Rokeach*).

They were established corresponding to the four expressions of redemption: "I will bring out," "I will deliver," "I will redeem," and "I will take" (*Shemos* 6:6-7)—which correspond to the four decrees Pharaoh issued against them (*Shemot Rabbah*, end of ch. 6). They also correspond to the breaking of the four *kelipot* (*Tola'at Yaakov*); to the four cups of Pharaoh (*Bereishis* 40:11-13), which hinted in that dream at the redemption of Israel, as the Sages say in *Bereishis Rabbah* ch. 88; to the four kingdoms; to the four cups of retribution that the Holy One, blessed be He, will give the nations of the world to drink, and corresponding to them, He will give Israel four cups of consolation (*Yerushalmi, Pesachim* ch. 10, halacha 1); and to the four letters of the Name *Havayah* (see *Zohar* Vol. III, 95b).

**When Yom Tov falls on Shabbos,** we say "Yom HaShishi" first (*Pesachim* 106a and *Tosafot* there). We begin with "Yom HaShishi" (and not from "Vayechulu"), because the first letters of "Yom HaShishi Vayechulu HaShamayim" spell the Name *Havayah*. When beginning, one should fix his eyes on the candles, and during the blessing of Kiddush, on the cup (Rema, ch. 271:10). One should not begin from the earlier text, "And G-d saw...," because the Sages said, " 'And it was very good'—this is death" (*Responsa Chatam Sofer*, *Orach Chayim* ch. 10).

**Savrei Maranan.** This is how it should be said, and no more (*Siddur* of the Yaavetz), as these words complete the number of words in the Kiddush. The reason for saying *Savrei Maranan* is found in the *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe, end of ch. 174. The *Abudraham* brings additional reasons.

*Savrei Maranan* is mentioned in *Tikkunei Zohar*, *Tikkun* 24, and one must say *Savrei Maranan*. It is the same in *Tanchuma*, beginning of *Parshas Pekudei*, in the *Likutei Responsa* of the Geonim at the end of *Kol Bo*, and in the *Tur*, ch. 474, in the name of his father (in *Tosafot* to *Berachos* 43a, and elsewhere: *Savrei Mori* in the *Rosh*, *Berachos* there; *Savrei Rabotai*). See *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar Chag HaMatzos*, ch. 15, for an explanation of this phrase specifically according to the secret (Kabbalistic) tradition.

**Yom Chag HaMatzos.** This is how it is called throughout the Torah, and we call it Pesach—because the Holy One, blessed be He, recounts the praise of Israel, that they did not delay and did not say, "How shall we go out into the wilderness?" And we recount the praise of the Holy One, blessed be He, who "passed over" (*Kevod HaLevi*, *Parshas Bo*). Some say that the name *Chag HaPesach* was instituted in memory of the Temple (*Ateret Zekenim*).

**Ve'et Yom Tov Mikra Kodesh Hazeh.** This is the version in *Masechet Soferim* ch. 19, in the *Siddur* of Rav Amram Gaon, in the *Sefer HaTefillah* of the Rambam and at the end of *Hilchos Shabbos*, in the *Abudraham*, the *Siddur* of the Rashash, and others. See *Shulchan Aruch*, beginning of ch. 487, and the commentaries there.

On Shabbos, we say **"Be'ahavah Mikra Kodesh."** Because the *Mikra Kodesh* of Shabbos was accepted by Israel with love, at Marah, but that of Yom Tov was through the Holy One, blessed be He, forcing the mountain over them like a cask at Sinai (*Levush*, ch. 487; *Mateh Moshe*; *Aruch HaShulchan*).

**U'mo'adei.** Even on a weekday, we say *U'mo'adei* with a *vav*, just as we say *VeShabbos* with a *vav*.

**U'mo'adei Kodshecha.** This is a phrase corresponding to the conclusion, placed near the conclusion (*Pesachim* 104a).

**Mekadesh Yisrael VeHaZemanim.** (*Pesachim* 104b). We do not say *VeHaMo'adim* as the verse says, because all the appointed times (*mo'adim*) depend on the times (*zemanim*): Pesach depends on the time of spring, etc. (*Bachya*, end of *Parshas Bo*).

**Shehecheyanu... on Motzaei Shabbos, Yaknehaz.** (*Pesachim* 103a). The text of the *Shehecheyanu* blessing is from the *Mishnah, Berachos* (beginning of ch. 8). We say *Shehecheyanu, Vekiyemanu, VeHigianu* three times—corresponding to "Bless the L-rd, O my soul," "I will bless the L-rd while I live," "I will sing praises unto my G-d while I have my being" (*Rokeach*, ch. 271; see also the *Siddur* of the Arizal, *Birkat HaNehenin*).

We do not recite the blessing "Who performed miracles," because we are destined to say it in the Haggadah (*Machzor Vitry*; *Rabbi Yehuda ben Yakar* on the *Yotzer Elokei HaRuchot*; *Rokeach*). It is for the One who performed all these miracles for us (ch. 273; *Shibolei HaLeket*). Some say it is because we only recite this blessing over a Rabbinic mitzvah (*Maharil*).

**LaZeman Hazeh.** The *lamed* has a *chirik* (vowel), and this is the version in ch. 315. *Rashi*, *Levush*, *Magen Avraham*, beginning of ch. 676.

**Havdalah.** The custom of the Rebbe’s household: In the Havdalah of Motzaei Shabbos that falls on Yom Tov, we do not bring the candles together, and we do not unify their flames, nor do we look at our fingernails. Rather, at the time of the blessing *Borei Me'orei HaEish*, one looks at the candles as they are, each one individually.

**Me'orei HaEish.** Because there are many lights (reddish and greenish) in the fire (*Berachos* 51b, 52b; see also *Tikkunei Zohar*, *Tikkun* 21).

**HaMavdil...** (*Pesachim* 104a and *Tosafot* there).

**Havdalat VeKidashta.** Two separations: between Kohanim and Leviim, and between Leviim and Yisraelim (*Tosafot* there).

**On Pesach, one drinks the cup.** The entire cup (*Bach*, ch. 472) and all at once (*Magen Avraham*, ibid.)—this is the custom of the Rebbe’s household. The same applies to the other three cups.

**Leaning to the left.** Because leaning to the right, or on one's face, or on one's back, is not considered leaning (*Pesachim* 108a, as per the commentary of the Rambam, *Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah*, ch. 7, halacha 6).

**Heseibah (Leaning).** "And G-d caused the people to turn (yisov)..." From here our Sages said: Even the poorest person in Israel should not eat until he leans, for that is what the Holy One, blessed be He, did for them (*Shemot Rabbah* ch. 20:18). What is the meaning of *yisov*? That He made them recline in the manner of kings reclining on their couches (*Bamidbar Rabbah* ch. 1:2).

## Urchatz

**Urchatz** — one performs **netilas yadayim** without a **berachah**. This is because one who is about to eat something that is dipped into a liquid requires **netilas yadayim** beforehand (Pesachim 115a), yet one does not recite a **berachah** because of the principle that in cases of doubt regarding **berachos**, we rule leniently (Shulchan Aruch Admur, Siman 158, Seif 3). Even someone who is not careful about this throughout the year performs **netilas yadayim** here, as it is one of the various changes we make on this night so that the children will ask questions (Chayei Adam, Klal 130, Seif Katan 28).

## Karpas

**Karpas:** One takes [the karpas], etc., in order to pique the curiosity of the children, as this is not done throughout the rest of the year (Pesachim 114a, b).

**Less than a kezayis:** Because this is also sufficient to pique the curiosity of the children, and in this way, according to all opinions, one will not be obligated to recite a beracha achrona (Shulchan Aruch Admur, 473:15 and 16).

**And he shall dip – and afterwards – he shall recite the beracha:** In order to make the beracha as proximate to the eating as possible (Chayei Adam 175:5). The reason that at a meal throughout the year one recites Hamotzi **and afterwards** dips in salt is because one recites the beracha before breaking the bread (so that one recites the beracha over the more significant item).

**In salt water or vinegar:** The Tosafot (Pesachim 114a, s.v. *metabel*) writes "in vinegar or in salt water," but in [Shulchan Aruch] 312 and the Yaavetz, it is written **"in salt water or in vinegar."** However, not in charoses, for since charoses is a mitzvah during the second dipping, [one might ask:] after he has filled his stomach with it [the charoses], shall he bring it for the mitzvah? (Rosh, ibid.).

**Yechaven (He shall intend):** Seemingly, it should be spelled with two vavs, and not with a vav and a yud. And so it is in every instance.

**He shall intend to exempt the maror as well:** Because the recitation of the Haggadah, etc., is not considered an interruption or a diversion of attention (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, 474). And he intends to exempt the maror of the korech as well.

Regarding karpas, it is not specified whether one should eat it while reclining or without reclining (as is written regarding maror). It can be said that its status is like the rest of the meal on this night, where it is optional to recline, and one who reclines is praiseworthy (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, 472:14), and it was only specified where the obligation or exemption is absolute. And the custom is to eat it without reclining.

According to most poskim, karpas does not require reclining (Shibolei Haleket, Matteh Moshe, Birkei Yosef, and others; and not like the opinion of the Beis Dovid).

After **karpas**, I have not seen [people] return the remainder of the karpas to the ke'arah, and consequently, from this point forward, there are only five items on the ke'arah.

## Yachatz

**Yachatz** **He takes, etc.** One breaks the matzah before the Haggadah (Ran in the name of Rav Hai Gaon), because the Haggadah must be recited over matzah that is fit to fulfill the mitzvah, which is *lechem oni*—broken bread (Pesachim 115b and Rashi ad loc.). It must be placed between the two whole matzos (Berachos 39b), because the blessing of *Hamotzi*, which precedes the blessing of *Al Achilas Matzah*, is recited over a whole loaf (Tosafos s.v. *Hakol* ad loc., and this is the primary ruling—Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe 475:6-7). Furthermore, because we do not bypass mitzvos, the whole matzah must be on top.

One breaks the matzah while it is inside the cloth, covered.

**The larger piece is set aside for the Afikoman,** as it is an important mitzvah, serving in place of the Korban Pesach (Maharil, Bach). It is customary to wrap it in a cloth (see Zohar Vol. III, 158b; Tur Shulchan Aruch 473), a remembrance of "their kneading troughs being bound up in their garments" (see Rokeach, Beis Yosef), and to place it between the pillows (HaManhig) so that one does not come to eat it during the meal (Yaavetz).

The Rebbe (Rashab) n.e.—and it is understood that his son, the Rebbe Rayatz, did the same—would break the Afikoman into five pieces. Once, it happened that it broke into six pieces, and he set one aside. He explained that the middle matzah corresponds to Yitzchak. There must be a breaking into five pieces—the sweetening of the five *gevuros* of Yitzchak in the *Afiku-man* (bring out the food), the influence of sustenance, the *chesed* of Avraham (Sichos 5688).

**Afikoman** (Pesachim 119b): The matzah eaten at the end, in remembrance of the Korban Pesach, is called Afikoman (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe 477:3). The meaning of Afikoman is: "Bring out and bring in" food and delicacies to the table (ibid. 477:2; see also Sefer HaPardes by Rashi, Hilchos Pesach). In the *Tishbi* and *Musaf HeAruch*: Afikoman is a Greek term for things eaten after the meal.

Some have the custom that the children "snatch" the Afikoman (and one redeems it from them in exchange for a gift). It is said that this is **alluded to** in the words of our Sages: "We snatch matzah on the nights of Pesach for the sake of the children, so they should not fall asleep" (Pesachim 109a; Chok Yaakov 472:2). The Rebbe Rayatz related how, once, the eldest daughter of the Rebbe Maharash "snatched" his Afikoman, and this was **in her childhood.** It appears this was an exceptional case, and the custom of snatching the Afikoman is not practiced in the Rebbe’s house. It is worth noting the words of our Sages: "After a thief, steal, and you will taste the flavor" (Berachos 5b).

**The smaller piece is placed between the two matzos,** because it is over this that we recite the Haggadah, as mentioned above.

## Maggid

**And one lifts the Ke'arah** (Rosh, end of Pesachim; Tur Shulchan Aruch. However, what is written in Pesachim 115b refers to the removal of the Ke'arah that we perform **after** reciting *Ha Lachma Anya*, and not as written in *Shaar HaKavanos*). In the house of the Rebbe, they do not lift the Ke'arah, but merely uncover a portion of the Matzos.

**Ha** - with a *tzere* (see *Minhagim*, citing the Arizal for the reason), and it is found written with this vocalization (Bereishis 47:23; Yechezkel 16:43; Daniel 2:43), but there it means **"Behold,"** and not **"This."**

**Ha Lachma** - This is the version in the *Siddur* of Rav Amram Gaon, the *Yotzer* for Shabbos HaGadol by R. Yosef Tov Elem (cited in *Tosafos*, s.v. *Lama*, Pesachim 115b), *Machzor Vitry*, *Shaarei Halacha* of the Riyah, Rambam at the end of *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*, *Abudraham*, and others. And even though this is not the exact bread itself but rather a representation of the bread, it is the language of the verse (Shemos 16:32), "So that they may see the bread that I fed you" (*Magen Avraham* 473:24, see there).

They instituted this passage in Aramaic so that even the unlearned (*amei ha'aretz*) would understand, for it was established at a time when everyone spoke Aramaic. However, **"Next year we will be free men"** is in Lashon HaKodesh so that the Babylonians would not understand and consider them rebels against the kingdom (*Kol Bo*; see *Shelah* for an explanation according to *sod*). One should not raise a difficulty from the rest of the Haggadah, which is in Lashon HaKodesh, especially *Mah Nishtanah*, which is the children's question and is also in Lashon HaKodesh—for this part of the Haggadah already existed during the time of the Beis HaMikdash, as brought in the Mishnah (Pesachim 116a). Note that in *Siman* 263, the entire paragraph is in Aramaic (though the beginning of the paragraph, "Ha Lachma - of Egypt," is not there), and in the Rambam, only **"Next year"** (the first one) is in Lashon HaKodesh, and the rest is Aramaic.

In *Machzor Vitry*, it adds at the end of the *Ha Lachma* paragraph, and likewise at the end of *Mah Nishtanah*: "and translate it into the vernacular." In *Sefer HaManhig*, he mentions this as a custom that had already been accepted. He writes: "And the reason they were accustomed to translate the first two passages into the vernacular is for the sake of clarity and to publicize the miracle." And in *Kol Bo*: "Some translate *Ha Lachma* and *Mah Nishtanah* into the vernacular so that the women and children will understand; some also translate *Af Avadim*. It is a beautiful custom, for what is the benefit of understanding the question if they do not know the answer?"

To resolve our custom, where we do not translate into the vernacular except for *Mah Nishtanah*—see below—it can be said simply that we are particular that at the very least, the one reciting the passage should understand what is being said by him. And as for *Mah Nishtanah*, the children are the ones asking.

**Kol Dichfin Yeisei V'Yeisach** - Meaning, let him come and perform the Seder of Pesach, that is, the Mitzvah-Matzah, the four cups, etc., similar to what Chazal said (Taanis 20b): "When R. Huna would eat a meal, he would open his doors and say, 'Whoever is in need, let him come and eat'" (*Abudraham*).

**Next year, etc. - HaBa'ah** - My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, said: the first [instance] is accented on the penultimate syllable (*mil'eil*), the second on the ultimate syllable (*milra*) (*Sichos 1931*). See Rashi on Bereishis 29:6; *Zohar Chadash* 265a; Rambam, *Hilchos Teshuvah*, end of Chapter 8.

**They remove the Ke'arah** (Pesachim 115b) so that the child will be astonished and ask: "We have not yet eaten, and you are already removing the table?" And through this, he will come to ask the other questions (ibid., *Tosafos*, s.v. *Kedei*).

**And they pour him the second cup, and here the son asks** (Mishnah, Pesachim 116a). Here, at the pouring of the second cup, the son asks, "Why is this night different now, that we pour the second cup before eating?" (Rashi and Rashbam, ibid.). Through this, he will also ask the other questions; therefore, we pour the cup before reciting the Haggadah, even though one does not need to hold it in his hand until reaching *Lefichach* (*Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 473:40).

**And they pour him** - The word **"him"** (*lo*) is a printing error; in the first Kapust *Siddur*, which the Alter Rebbe proofread, it is absent, and he likewise omitted it in his *Shulchan Aruch* (*Shaar HaKollel*; see above, paragraph *Kadesh*).

**The son asks** - The fact that it is specifically the child who asks awakens Above the concept of, "For Israel is a youth, and I love him," a verse that speaks of the time of the Exodus (*Sichos, Chol HaMoed Pesach 1944*).

**The son asks, "Mah Nishtanah"** - In older versions, there was also the reading: **"The son asks, 'What...'"** (*Sichos, Chol HaMoed Pesach 1943*, section 5).

In *Zohar Chadash, Tikkunim* at the end (112d): "On the night of watching... when the night of the redemption of her [the Shechinah] arrives, the *Mah* (the Name) shines, which is the explicit Name. At that time, the son asks his father, 'Mah (what) is this night different...'" See there.

Our custom, that the one asking precedes his recitation of *Mah Nishtanah* with: "Tatty, I will ask you four questions" — and even if he has no father, he says this — and continues: "Mah Nishtanah... What is different about the night of Pesach from all other nights of the entire year? The first question is, 'On all nights...' all other nights of the entire year... two times, one time Karpas in salt water, the second time Maror in Charoses. The second question is, 'On all nights,' etc."

After the recitation of *Mah Nishtanah* as above by the youngest among those present, the Rebbe Rayatz was accustomed to repeat—in a whisper—*Mah Nishtanah* (with the aforementioned introduction) and its translation.

**Mah Nishtanah, etc.** (Mishnah, Pesachim 116a).

**Anu** - The *Aleph* has a *kamatz*.

**Matbilin, Matzah, Maror, Mesubin** - This is the order in the Mishnah found in the Yerushalmi, Rif, and Rosh (though the last two questions are missing there; the *Bach's* gloss on the Rif is difficult). It is in *Siman* 273 (in manuscript), *Siman* 263, Rambam, Tur, *Abudraham* (since they prioritize the explanation of the question of **Matbilin** over the explanation of **"other vegetables"**), *Zevach Pesach* by R. Yitzchak Abarbanel, *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Minhagim* of the Arizal, *Magen Avraham*, and others. This is also how it appears in the first printed Haggadah (Soncino, 1485). This corresponds to their order on this night: dipping (at least the first, which is only to astonish the children), Matzah, Maror; and the question of **Mesubin** is not included in the above, because it was added long after the first questions, i.e., when they ceased reclining while eating throughout the year. Therefore, it was not mentioned in the Mishnah and Gemara (Gra. His interpretation is difficult, for in Rambam, *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah* 8:2, he brings the question of *Mesubin* and also the question of "all of it roasted," which shows they were asking about *Mesubin* even during the time of the Beis HaMikdash). Therefore, it also comes at the end of all the questions according to all opinions. See *Pri Etz Chaim* that the aforementioned order corresponds to the order of the worlds from below to above: *Asiyah, Yetzirah, Beriah, Atzilus*.

One should not raise a difficulty against the Rambam's version: why was the question of *Mesubin* not mentioned in the Shas? It is not a difficulty, for **it must be** that according to the Rambam, the question of *Mesubin* was taught (whether in the Mishnah or the Gemara)—for he would not innovate on his own a version of what they used to say—something that is not brought **anywhere!**

**Furthermore, it can be said** regarding the above, and as a premise—that there are many variations in the manuscripts of the Shas—not only in the order, but also in the count of the questions [there are some where the question of **Maror** was omitted, or **roasted meat** (even during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed)].

The reason can be said to be **that according to the law**, it is not essential to say specifically all the questions, and not even *lechatchilah* (Pesachim 115b: "You have exempted them from saying..."). Therefore, there were variations in custom from the outset, or there was no fixed custom at all, until after some time (during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed, as is evident from the Rambam) they unified the customs and instituted that all five questions be said everywhere, "so that all of Israel would perform one act," similar to the explanation regarding the *Takkanas Rabban Gamliel* concerning the *Teki'as Shofar* (*Rav Hai Gaon* in *Beis Yosef*, Orach Chaim 590).

**Two times** - Even though we dip three times, the dipping of the Maror and the *Chazeret* for the *Korech* are considered as one (Taz 475).

**This night, Maror** - He does not say "all of it Maror" because we eat other vegetables during the first dipping (*Tosafos*, s.v. *HaLailah*, Pesachim, ibid.).

"On all nights we eat roasted, seared, or cooked meat; on this night, all of it is roasted" (Mishnah, ibid.). This is how he would ask during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed.

He does not ask about the obligation to drink four cups, because it is entirely Rabbinic and has no element of Torah law, unlike *Haseibah* (reclining), which has an obligation with Matzah, and *Matbilin*, as the second dipping is in Maror (Maharal).

**And they return the Ke'arah and uncover a portion of the bread**, for the Haggadah must be said over the Matzos, the "bread of poverty" over which we answer many things (Pesachim 115b).

**Avadim Hayinu** - For it begins with "disgrace." What is the "disgrace"? Rav said: "Initially, our ancestors were idolaters," and Shmuel said: "We were slaves" (Pesachim 116a). And nowadays we do both (Rif and Rosh; see there the explanation of R. Chananel and *Abudraham*). Some have the reading **Rav** (*Siman* 273) or **Rava** (*Hagahos Maimoniyos*, *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah* 7:4; see *Dikdukei Sofrim* to Pesachim). He says "We were slaves," and if so, our custom is well-founded, for the *halacha* follows Rav in matters of prohibition and Rava [in matters of civil law]. (And what is written in *Sefer Imrei Shefer* by the Netziv—the paragraph "Initially, our ancestors were idolaters"—is puzzling, as it contradicts what is written in the Rif, R. Chananel, *Abudraham*, and Rosh mentioned above. At the very least, he should have mentioned them). Therefore, the compiler of the Haggadah placed **"We were slaves"** first, even though the matter recounted in the paragraph "Initially, our ancestors were idolaters" occurred to our ancestors before the event of "We were slaves" (*Abudraham*).

**We were slaves, etc., our G-d, etc., and with an outstretched arm** - Founded on the verse (Devarim 6:21), with the addition of the words **"our G-d"** and **"with an outstretched arm,"** which are not in the verse. See *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Minhagim*, and the *Minhagim* of the Arizal for the explanation according to *sod*. In the *Siddur* of our Rebbe, it is explained according to *Chassidus*. The connection of **"with an outstretched arm"** to **"our G-d"** is also understood according to Chazal, that **"Elokim"** indicates the attribute of *Gevurah* (Bereshis Rabbah, end of Ch. 12), and **"arm"** means the left hand (Berachos 6a, Nazir 3b).

**To Pharaoh** - A hard king. **In Egypt** - From where a slave could not escape (Yalkut Shimoni, Remez 269).

**From there** - In the verse, it says **"from Egypt."** The reason for the change requires study.

**Behold, we, etc., were enslaved to Pharaoh** - Naturally, this does not mean the Pharaoh of the days of Moshe, but rather that all the kings of Egypt were called by the general name: Pharaoh (Rashi, Tehillim 34:1). And even though in the days of the Mishnah and the arrangement of the Haggadah this custom had already ceased (as per the difficulty of the *Abudraham*—and therefore he omitted the word **"to Pharaoh"** in the text of the Haggadah), it can be said—to resolve our version—that it ceased because at the Exodus, Egypt descended from its greatness. Afterward, it had ups and downs and was also conquered by others. But **"had the Holy One, Blessed be He, not taken our ancestors out of Egypt,"** it would have remained in its strength (note Yalkut Shimoni, Remez 230: "When Israel left Egypt, the kingdom of Egypt ceased... Pharaoh ruled from one end of the world to the other... for the sake of the honor of Israel") and all the protocols and customs of the monarchy would have been preserved. Therefore, it must be so, to resolve what they used to say—even according to the version of the *Abudraham*—"we were enslaved **in Egypt**," even at a time when the kingdom of Egypt had ceased.

**And even if we were all wise, etc.** And proof of this: **"It happened that Rabbi Eliezer, etc."**

**We are all understanding, we are all knowledgeable**—and we do not say **"we are all elders."** This is also in *Shibolei HaLeket*, brought in *Tanya Rabbati*. In *Bris HaLevi* by R. Shlomo Alkabetz, although he resolves both versions, nevertheless, in his Haggadah, he omitted **"we are all elders"** (*Shaar HaKavanos*). Likewise, these words are not in the *Sefer HaMitzvos* of the Rambam, Mitzvah 157 (unlike the Haggadah in his *Chibur*), in *Zevach Pesach* by R. Yitzchak Abarbanel, *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Minhagim*, *Siman Kol Yaakov*, and the Gra.

**It is a mitzvah upon us to tell of the Exodus from Egypt** (*Mechilta*, end of *Bo*; *Zohar Chadash* 41a—but it is not necessarily certain there that it refers specifically to the night of Pesach; Rambam, *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*, beginning of Ch. 7; *Sefer Mitzvos Gadol*, Positive Mitzvah 41; *Sefer HaChinuch*, Mitzvah 21; our Rebbe at the beginning of *Hilchos Pesach*, and others).

And even women are obligated in this mitzvah (by Torah law—according to the *Chinuch* and R. Yehuda in *Tosafos*, s.v. *Sha'af*, Megillah 4a; see *Tosafos*, s.v. *Mi*, Sukkah 38a—or by Rabbinic law. See *Birkei Yosef* 473:15 and *Zevach Pesach* at length; this is also the implication of our Rebbe's opinion in his *Shulchan Aruch*, 472:26. And also in *Sefer Beis Dovid* 164, that they are exempt—and this is the opinion of the *Minchas Chinuch*—he retracted this in his book *Lechem HaPanim*. See *Sefer Maggid Devarav* 42).

And we do not recite a blessing over this mitzvah, and several reasons have been given for this: because one has already fulfilled it by reciting **"a remembrance of the Exodus from Egypt"** in *Kiddush*; because one is exempted by the recitation of *Shema*, over which we recite a blessing before and after; because it has no fixed measure and one fulfills it even with mere speech; because it is entirely a blessing and praise, and especially since they instituted the blessing *Asher Ga'alnu* within it, and one does not institute a blessing over a blessing; because one performs the mitzvah with an interruption—eating, etc.—and one does not recite a blessing in such a case, as stated regarding the four cups (Rif, *Shibolei HaLeket*, Rashba, *Ma'amar Nesim*, *Zevach Pesach*), and other reasons.

And even though it is a mitzvah from the Torah to mention the Exodus from Egypt every day, by day and by night (Berachos 12b; Rambam, *Hilchos Krias Shema* 1:3; *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe, Orach Chaim 67:1, and others), one must distinguish between them, and also by Torah law: a) In the daily mention, one fulfills it even with a remembrance in the heart and mere contemplation (see Megillah 18a; and in *Shaagas Aryeh*, end of 13, that it can be said this is the opinion of the *Sifra*, and even according to our Shas, which discusses it there, and the gates of explanation are not locked, and it can be said that only by Rabbinic law is one obligated to do so with speech. This is not the place to elaborate), unlike the night of Pesach, where it is said, **"And you shall tell your son."** This is specifically with speech (see *Minchas Chinuch* there). b) In the daily mention, a mere remembrance is sufficient, as per the language of the Mishnah. And in the Rambam: it is a mitzvah **to mention** the Exodus from Egypt, unlike Pesach, where it must be a "telling" (*Ma'amar Nesim*, *Zevach Pesach*, *Siman Mahari"d*, *Malbim* on Shemos 13:8, and others. But this is not the case in the Rif and Rashba, as written in *Ma'amar Nesim* and *Minchas Chinuch* there). c) Some say that on Pesach, it is added that it must be in the form of an answer to a son or another person (*Minchas Chinuch*, *Zevach Pesach*, and this is somewhat implied in *Sefer HaChinuch*. See also *Responsa of the Rosh*, Rule 24:2. But in the Rif, Rashba, Rambam, *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe, and others, this is not mentioned. And perhaps it can be said that it depends on the two versions: in the *Mechilta* brought in the *Sefer HaMitzvos* of the Rambam, Mitzvah 157: "And you shall tell your son—even if he does not ask you... I have only [the obligation] at a time when he has a son*—between him and another, from where? The verse says...". But in the *Mechilta d'Rashbi* it says: **"Between him and himself,** between him and another, from where? The verse says..."). d) The telling of the Exodus on Pesach is counted in the tally of the mitzvos, as mentioned above (note that the *Rassag*, *Halachos Gedolos*, and R. Shlomo ibn Gabirol did not count it in their tally of positive mitzvos. See on this in the commentary on the *Sefer HaMitzvos* of the *Rassag* by R. Y. Perla, Positive Mitzvah 33), unlike the daily mention, and this has practical consequences (see *Minchas Chinuch* there, *Tzlach* on Berachos there, *Aruch HaShulchan*, Orach Chaim, end of 67, and others). e) The daily mention is not for the days of Mashiach according to Ben Zoma (Berachos 12b), and according to all, the *halacha* follows him (see also the Mishnah there). f) On the night of Pesach, the mitzvah is for the duration of the entire night (or until midnight—according to R. Avraham ibn Ezra), even if one already told it at the beginning of the night, unlike the daily mention—for if one mentioned it once, we do not find a mitzvah (even Rabbinic) to mention it again. This still requires study.

It is likely that this is an omission in the printing of the Rambam's book and the *Mechilta d'Rashbi*).

**It is a mitzvah upon us to tell of the Exodus from Egypt** - The Rebbe Rayatz recounted: When I was a child, my father asked me why we do not recite a blessing over the telling of the Exodus from Egypt? I did not know. He told me that he, too, was asked this by his father (the Rebbe Maharash) and did not know. His father told him that he, too, was asked this by his father (the Rebbe Tzemach Tzedek) and did not know. His father told him that he, too, was asked this by his father, his grandfather (the Alter Rebbe) and did not know. His uncle (the Mitteler Rebbe), who was present at that time, replied according to the opinion of the Rif, that one has already fulfilled it with the blessing of *Kiddush* in which the Exodus is mentioned. There is also the answer of the Rashba, that a mere remembrance is sufficient. The Alter Rebbe raised a difficulty against this, for on the night of Pesach, there must be a Haggadah and a telling. He answered that it is similar to why we do not recite a blessing over *Birkas HaMazon* ("to bless *Birkas HaMazon*") (*Sichos, Chol HaMoed Pesach 1937*).

**And all who increase** - Even Kohanim and Leviim who were not enslaved, and converts. Proof of this: the incident with R. Eliezer, R. Yehoshua (Levi), R. Akiva ben Yosef (a Kohen), R. Akiva (son of converts), and R. Tarfon (a Kohen) (*Simchas HaRegel*).

**It happened that Rabbi Eliezer, etc.** - This paragraph already exists in the *Seder Haggadah* of the *Rassag* and is mentioned in *Tosafos*, s.v. *Dechashiv* (Kesuvos 105a) and s.v. *Amar* (Avodah Zarah 45a). In *Tosafos*, he notes its place as **"the Haggadah of Pesach."** As of now, I have not found it in any Midrash. See *Tosefta*, end of Ch. 10 of Pesachim, an incident similar to this with Rabban Gamliel in Lod, where they occupied themselves with the laws of Pesach all night, etc.

**R. Eliezer, R. Yehoshua, R. Elazar ben Azaryah, R. Akiva, and R. Tarfon** - He places R. Elazar ben Azaryah before R. Akiva because he was from a distinguished family and was a Nasi (*Tosafos*, Kesuvos, ibid.). It can be said that the reason he places R. Akiva before R. Tarfon (as per the difficulty of *Tosafos* in Avodah Zarah, ibid.) is because this occurred in Bnei Brak, where R. Akiva was the *mara d'atra* (Sanhedrin 32b). Even so, he does not place him before R. Yehoshua, and certainly not before R. Eliezer, for R. Yehoshua was R. Akiva's teacher, unlike R. Tarfon, who, even though he was his teacher, later (this can be said; with this, the Shas in Kesuvos 4b is well understood) became his colleague (see *Seder HaDoros*, entry for R. Tarfon and references there).
After some time, I found an explanation for the order according to Chazal (Berachos 46b and *Tosefta* there): the order of reclining—the great one in the middle, the second to him above him, the third to him below him. Based on this, they reclined here: the Nasi in the middle, R. Eliezer and R. Yehoshua—the teachers of R. Akiva—above the Nasi, and R. Akiva and R. Tarfon below him. And he mentions them in the Haggadah in the order they were reclining (*Me'ir Einayim* on the Haggadah).

**In Bnei Brak** - The name of a city (Yehoshua 19:45). It can be said that he mentions the name of the city because through this, the order in which he lists them is understood, as mentioned above. In the aforementioned *Tosefta*, it mentions that it was in Lod. It can be said—to teach us that even though it was in Lod, and thus there was no Korban Pesach then, nevertheless, they occupied themselves with the laws of Pesach all night.

**All that night** - Even R. Eliezer and R. Elazar ben Azaryah, even though they hold (Mechilta, end of *Bo*, and Pesachim 120b) that the eating of the Pesach is only until midnight. See *Imrei Shefer*, which discusses this at length. In *Mechilta*, end of *Bo*: "R. Eliezer says... they must occupy themselves with the laws of Pesach until midnight."

**R. Elazar ben Azaryah said** (Berachos 12b and elsewhere). Some have the reading (Yerushalmi, Berachos 1:6; *Shibolei HaLeket HaShalem*, *Siman* 218; Haggadah in the Rambam, *Machzor Roma*): **"R. Elazar ben Azaryah said to them,"** and if so, it is a continuation of the previous paragraph. And even according to our version, it can be explained that this was at the gathering in Bnei Brak—and it is understood simply why they arranged this Mishnah in the Haggadah, even though it speaks of the chapter of *Tzitzis*, which we say every day.

**Like a man of seventy years** - And not literally an elder, for gray hair came upon him on the day he was appointed Nasi, and he was eighteen years old (Berachos 28a), and according to the Yerushalmi (Berachos 4:5), sixteen years old. And according to the version of the *Abudraham*—thirteen years old. (But this expression is also found in *Mechilta* to Shemos 13:2, and in Yalkut Shimoni there, "like a man of eighty years." Both are regarding R. Yehoshua).

**Like a man of seventy years** - "That I labored with the men of wisdom, **I did not merit** to know the hint for the obligation of saying, etc." (*Peirush HaMishnayos* there). Or **"I merited"**—I prevailed (*R. Ovadiah of Bartenura*).

**So that you may remember** (Devarim 16:3).

**To bring in the nights** - In the margin of the Munich manuscript of the Shas, the version is "all to bring in the nights." And in no other place have I found so far, "all the days of your life **to bring in** the nights."

**And the Sages say** - They disagree with Ben Zoma and hold that one does not mention the Exodus from Egypt at night (see the Mishnah there; *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Shaar Chag HaMatzos*, Ch. 6). Some explain that the Sages **add** to Ben Zoma, and according to all, one mentions the Exodus from Egypt at night (*Zevach Pesach*, *Shelah*, *Tzlach*, and others). Seemingly, even according to this explanation, they are in disagreement, for according to Ben Zoma, there are **two** obligations: one by day and one by night, like *Krias Shema*. But according to the Sages, it is one obligation whose time is by day and by night. This is not the place to elaborate.

**Blessed be the Omnipresent, etc.** (This is the version in *Siman* of the *Shelah*, Yaavetz, R. S. R. Hirsch). Now he begins to expound on the verses, and says "Blessed be the Omnipresent, etc.," like a person who expounds and says "In the Name of Hashem," and afterward begins to expound (*Abudraham*). Here there are four blessings corresponding to the four verses written regarding the mitzvah of the Haggadah, which is a positive mitzvah and requires a blessing, except that one is exempted by *Krias Shema*, over which we recite a blessing before and after, and because of its preciousness, they hinted at it through these four blessings (*Shibolei HaLeket*).

**The Omnipresent** (*HaMakom*) - Why do they use a nickname for the Holy One, Blessed be He, and call Him *Makom* (Place)? Because He is the place of the world, but His world is not His place (Bereshis Rabbah 68:9).

**Corresponding to four, etc.** (According to the Yerushalmi, Pesachim 10:4; *Mechilta*, end of *Bo*). This is how it is in the Haggadah of the Rambam, *Zevach Pesach*, *Siman* of the *Shelah*, Yaavetz, R. S. R. Hirsch, and others. In the Yerushalmi, it says **"fool"** instead of **"simpleton"** (*tam*), and several other changes.

**Wise, wicked, simple, and one who does not know how to ask** - The order of the verses is not written this way, and he mentions them according to the way of their wisdom: the wise, and after him the wicked, for he too is wise, and through the malice of his heart he acts wickedly; and after him the simple, who has a little wisdom to ask (*Abudraham*).

**The wise, what does he say? etc. The wicked, what does he say? etc.** - A hint: what a person is, and his essence, he reveals through his speech (*Sichos of the Rebbe Rayatz, Chol HaMoed Pesach*).

**What is the testimony... that Hashem our G-d commanded you** (Devarim 6:20) - "You," meaning you who left Egypt, for the matter was addressed to you. But, perforce, he does not exclude himself from the collective, since he says **"our G-d"** (*Machzor Vitry*).
Note that in the Yerushalmi, *Mechilta*, and Rambam, the version here is **"us"** instead of **"you."** This is also how it is in the first printed Haggadah.

**And you shall also say to him, etc.** - Besides what is written in the passage ("We were slaves"—which was already brought in the Haggadah above), say to him also the other laws of Pesach, until the last matter in it, which is "one does not conclude, etc." (*Zevach Pesach*).

**One does not conclude** (Pesachim 119b) - One does not conclude the meal, after having eaten the Pesach, with a dessert (*R. Ovadiah of Bartenura*).
Note that in the Yerushalmi, this answer is for the simple son. In *Simchas HaRegel*, he brings a version that this is the answer for the wicked son.

**What is the service?** (Shemos 12:26).

**And you shall also say to him** - Besides the answer in the passage there—blunt his teeth, etc. (*Zevach Pesach*).

**Because of this** (Shemos 13:8). It is written in the passage of the one who does not know how to ask, but in it, only the words **"because of this"** are expounded. But **"for me and not for him"** is expounded regarding the wicked, as if to say: if it is not relevant to the one who does not know how to ask, apply it to the wicked (*Zevach Pesach*; *Abudraham*).

**For me and not for him** - In the *Mechilta*, it is in the second person: **"and not for you,"** had you been there, etc. Apparently, out of respect for those reclining, the compiler of the Haggadah made this change.

**What is this? - Slaves** (Shemos 13:14).

**You shall open for him** - The father's pain is great, and upon seeing that there is no answer in his son's mouth, his strength weakened like a woman's, similar to Chazal's way of speaking (based on "And if you do so," *Simchas HaRegel*).

**Open for him** - From the language of the verse (Mishlei 31:8), "Open your mouth for the mute."

**And you shall tell your son** (Shemos 13:8).
**I might think from the first of the month** (This is the text in all versions. However, in the Mechilta - Shemos, ibid. - it reads: **I hear** from the first of the month). This is not part of the telling to the son, but rather, since the verse mentions it, the Sages brought it up in the context of the laws of Haggadah (Abudraham). Others say that this, too, is part of the telling to the one who does not know how to ask (Zevach Pesach, Maaseh Nissim).
**From the first of the month.** Just as Moshe Rabbeinu also told the Bnei Yisrael from the first of the month (ibid.).
**While it is still day.** For that is the time for the service of the Pesach offering mentioned in the verse preceding this (ibid.).
**For the sake of this... Matzah and Maror.** One cannot say that it speaks only of the Pesach offering, and if so, it is while it is still day; for if that were the case, the words **"for the sake of this"** or the words **"on that day"** would be superfluous (and this resolves the difficulty in Shaar HaShamayim).
**Matzah and Maror are placed.** In the time of the Pesach offering, they would say: "Because the Pesach, Matzah, and Maror are placed before you" (Shibolei HaLeket. See Rashi on the Torah). However, it must be that even one of them is sufficient to obligate one in the recounting of Yitziyas Mitzrayim, for the Haggadah in our time is a Torah obligation even when there is no Pesach offering, and Maror is only a Rabbinic obligation (Pesachim 120a; see there 116b).
**Placed before you.** Meaning, at the time when the eating of Matzah and Maror is a mitzvah, regarding which the verse says **"for the sake of this"**—that I may fulfill these mitzvos of His, Hashem did for me. And not when he placed them while it was still day (based on Terumas HaDeshen, siman 137; see Shulchan Aruch Admur, 473:3).
**Before you.** In the Mechilta and in Midrash Sechel Tov, the version is **"before you on your table,"** and this is also found in Maamar HaChametz by the Rashbetz. It can be said that according to this version, we know from the extra wording that there are two conditions to be obligated in the recounting of Yitziyas Mitzrayim: at a time when one is obligated in Maror or Matzah, and when he has Maror or Matzah with him. This is not the case according to our version.
Now, in the commentary on Sefer HaMitzvos by the Ralbag (ibid.), he discusses this at length and concludes that according to many Rishonim, if one does not have Matzah, he is exempt from the recounting of Yitziyas Mitzrayim. This requires study, for according to many opinions, the reason we do not recite a blessing over the Haggadah is because one has already fulfilled the obligation by reciting **"a remembrance of Yitziyas Mitzrayim"** in the text of Kiddush, as mentioned above. And the Kiddush is at a time when Matzah and Maror are not placed before him, for they would not bring the table until *after* he had made Kiddush, as stated above. Furthermore, for those who provide a different reason for why the Haggadah does not require a blessing, this difficulty is not the cause of their disagreement. It is forced to say here that "whatever is fit for mixing, the mixing does not delay it." I will not elaborate further.
**Initially our ancestors were idolaters.** Mentioned in Pesachim 116a and in the Yerushalmi there.
**Our ancestors.** In the days of Terach and before him (Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah, ch. 7, halacha 4).
**And now** — some write **achshav** with a yud, but it is not necessary (Tishbi) — the drawing near was in the days of our forefather Avraham, but since the action continues even now, and therefore we are also obligated to give thanks for it, he says **"and now,"** and not **"and afterwards."** (Note the Zohar Chadash, beginning of Parshas Yisro).
**And Yehoshua said... Thus says Hashem.** (Yehoshua 24:2-4). Did all of Yisrael not know this, and Yehoshua all the more so? Rather... (Zohar Chadash 98b. The matter of the "good" according to the Zohar's interpretation is explained in Matok MiDvash there and in the Arizal's Haggadah).
**And I gave - the letter hei is missing - his seed.** How many quarrels I had with him until I gave him Yitzchak (Yerushalmi Pesachim, ch. 10, halacha 5).
**Blessed is He who keeps.** This passage is brought in the Siddur of Rav Saadia Gaon, etc.; however, for the time being, I have not found it in any Midrash.
**His promise.** At different times: to our forefather Avraham regarding the exile of Mitzrayim, and to the prophets regarding those who arise in every generation, and "it is this that has stood for our ancestors," etc.
**To Yisrael.** This can be interpreted: 1) He keeps for Yisrael that which He promised to Avraham and the prophets. 2) **"To Yisrael"** is like **"concerning Yisrael."**
**He calculated.** We do not have the version **"He is calculating,"** because this calculation has already passed (Abudraham; this is also the case in the Shelah, Yaavetz, and the Arizal's Haggadah). However, in Shibolei HaLeket, siman 263, and the Rambam and Zevach Pesach, the version is **"He is calculating."**
**To do.** This connects to what follows (Shibolei HaLeket, Zevach Pesach). Some explain it as connecting to what came before (Maharal, Kol Yaakov).
**"In the covenant" between the pieces.** This is the version in Machzor Vitry, Zevach Pesach, the Arizal's Haggadah, Shelah, and Yaavetz (unlike Shibolei HaLeket, siman 263, and Rambam, where the version is **"to our forefather Avraham between the pieces"**). This is the language of the Seder Olam (brought in Yalkut Shimoni, remez 77). And it is more fitting, because the promise "know for certain" was said before he passed between the pieces.
**And He said to Avram** (Bereishis 15:13-14).
**And afterwards they shall go out.** Rabbi Yissa Zeira of the Chaveraya asked Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: "This that the Holy One, blessed be He, reminds Yisrael in every place, 'I took you out of the land of Mitzrayim'—what is the greatness here? The condition was fulfilled, 'and afterwards they shall go out'." He said to him: "Come and see, my son, the Holy One, blessed be He, did not make a condition with Avraham except that He would take Yisrael out of the exile of Mitzrayim, and not immediately from the servitude of the 'other' power. Yisrael in Mitzrayim were dwelling under the 49 forces of impurity, and the Holy One, blessed be He, took them out, and furthermore, He brought them into the 49 gates of understanding that correspond to them. And because of this, you will find in the Torah 50 times [the mention of] Yitziyas Mitzrayim. And this is what we count from the Yom Tov of Pesach, days and weeks, for every day He takes us out of the force of impurity and brings us into the force of purity" (Zohar Chadash, beginning of Parshas Yisro, in abbreviated form. See also Tikkunei Zohar 432).
In any case, regarding the 50 times Yitziyas Mitzrayim is mentioned in the Torah—see Pardes Rimonim by the Ramak, Shaar HaShearim, at the beginning.
**One must lift - the Arizal.** So it is written in the Shelah and Yaavetz—and this is the custom—even though in Machzor Vitry, the Arizal's Haggadah, and Shulchan Aruch Admur 473:4, they wrote: **"He shall take (hold) the cup in his hand."**
The custom of the Rebbe's house: They cover the bread and only afterwards lift the cup, which corresponds to what is said at the end of this passage: "He shall set the cup down and uncover the bread." Similarly, in the passage "Therefore," he precedes the covering of the bread to the lifting of the cup. The reason may be that since the covering of the bread is so that it should not "see" its shame, as follows, it is proper that it should not "see" the lifting of the cup either. Especially since no reason is found to delay the covering of the bread until later. A remembrance of this is the order of Kiddush on Yom Tov, where the bread is covered during the lifting. And what is written here: **"to lift... and to cover,"** is because here he comes to inform us of the **innovation** written by the Arizal, and as follows, and the innovation is **the lifting of the cup** (and **automatically,** through this, one must cover the bread), and therefore he mentioned it first. And the words **"and to cover the bread"** are a parenthetical statement.
**To lift the cup.** In honor of "I will lift the cup of salvations" (based on what is written in the Shelah and Yaavetz there). In Shaar HaKavanos, it is explained that the connection of this passage to the cup is because one only recites song over wine (Berachos 35a). And this is puzzling, because this passage is not a song and praise but a recounting of events, and therefore only **some** have the custom to hold the cup, unlike when he reaches "Therefore... to thank," where **everyone** has the custom (as per the precision of the language in Shulchan Aruch Admur). Rather, the reason is **according to Kabbalah.** And as the language of the Rokeach, brought in the Shelah at the beginning of his Maseches Pesachim.
**And to cover the bread.** So that it should not see its shame (Agur; Shulchan Aruch Admur there). Even though the Tur in siman 271 explains the matter of the shame of the bread when making Kiddush over wine, because the bread is prior to wine for a blessing, and here there is no place for a blessing—nevertheless, even here it is a shame to the bread when one recites this part of the Haggadah over wine at a time when the entirety of the Haggadah should be recited specifically over the Matzah, which is why it is called "bread of poverty," and not over the wine.
**So wrote the Arizal.** That which our Rebbe copied this, even though it is not his custom in the Siddur to note the sources, it can be said that it is according to the above, to indicate that it is not only according to Kabbalah, for this is not a song like the passage "Therefore."
**And it**—the promise—**is that which has stood.** See above, the passage "His promise."
**One... to destroy us.** In siman 263, Rambam, Shibolei HaLeket, and Zevach Pesach, they omitted the word **"to destroy us"** (the first one). The reason is because **"the one"** refers to Pharaoh, and his intention was not to eradicate everything (Zevach Pesach). It can be said that the reason for our version (and this is the case in the Arizal's Haggadah, Shelah, and Yaavetz) is because even though initially he only decreed against the males, afterwards he said "I will draw my sword, my hand shall dispossess them," for he sought to eradicate everything, as the Maharal wrote, and as is indicated in Targum Yonasan and Targum Yerushalmi on the Torah there.
**Go and learn...** Based on what is written in the Mishnah (Pesachim 116a), and he expounds from "An Aramean sought to destroy my father" until he finishes the entire passage; he is expounding it now as it is in the Sifrei (Abudraham).
**Go and learn.** This expression, even though there is no "going" in the place—is also found in Kiddushin 37a, Sanhedrin 86a, Chullin 63a, and in many places in the Midrashim. And in Zevach Pesach, he explains: "Go" from the subject of Mitzrayim that you are currently occupied with, and "learn" from the stories of Lavan and Yaakov.
**And learn**—that the Holy One, blessed be He, saves us from the hand of those who rise against us—**that Lavan sought to eradicate everything**—had the Holy One, blessed be He, not saved them (Machzor Vitry, Shibolei HaLeket, Abudraham).
**An Aramean sought to destroy my father** (Devarim 26:5), and as it is translated: "Lavan the Aramean sought to destroy my father." And in the Sifrei (ibid.): "This teaches that Yaakov did not go down to Aram except to be destroyed."
**And he went down to Mitzrayim...** and even then the Holy One, blessed be He, saved us.
**Against his will,** as the Sages said (Shabbos 89b): "Yaakov our father was worthy of going down to Mitzrayim in iron chains—**according to the decree**—for the decree was 'your seed shall be a stranger'."
And in this passage, he comes to tell how the descent happened, but he does not learn this from the verse (and therefore he does not say: **"This teaches** that he went down against his will"), for on the contrary: **"And he went down"**—**and not "he was brought down"**—implies with good will. And in the Sifrei and in the Rambam's Haggadah, this exposition is not present. The reason may be that in practice, Yaakov did go down with his good will.
**And he sojourned there - Datzach Adash Beachav** (Sifrei ibid. with slight variations).
**And they said...** (Bereishis 47:4).
**For the famine is heavy**—but when the famine passes they will return to Kenaan, so they did not go down to settle permanently (Shibolei HaLeket, Machzor Vitry).
**With a few people, as it is said**—and it is explained in the verse in detail. And likewise below, there are times when this is the only intention of the compiler of the Haggadah when he says **"as it is said."** And with this, several of the commentators' difficulties are resolved.

**Seventy** (Devarim 10:22).
**And now your name, etc.** It is difficult to understand why the conclusion of this verse is cited (it is not in the Sifri), and furthermore, he should have cited from Bereishis (46:27): "All the souls of the house of Yaakov who came to Mitzrayim were seventy." (See *Ma’aneh Lashon*. Its resolution requires further study). It can be said that the verse "Seventy souls, etc." is primarily intended to **emphasize** how few they were when they descended to Mitzrayim, and the conclusion of the verse, "And now your name, etc.," points to this intent. Therefore, the author of the Haggadah specifically brought this verse, including its conclusion, "And now your name, etc."

**And became there a nation, learned.** In *Machzor Vitry*, *Abudarham*, *Shelah*, and *Yaavetz*, the reading here is "a **great** nation," but in *Siddur R. Saadia Gaon*, *Rambam*, *Maharal*, and *Siddur HaRashar*, the word "great" is omitted. The reason may be: this citation is expounded upon the word "nation" alone. Furthermore, the Maggid expounds upon the word "great" elsewhere, as the verse says, "And the children of Yisrael, etc." (Zevach Pesach). Since the word "great" is not expounded here—according to these authorities—they omitted it.

**Distinguished** – Marked, in that they did not change their names, their language, their religion, or their attire (Zevach Pesach).

**And the children of Yisrael** (Shemos 1:7).

**And numerous, as it is written, "I passed by you, etc., myriads, etc."** (Yechezkel 16:6-7).

**I passed by you.** The arrangers of the Haggadah established this verse in the middle of the Haggadah of the Pesach night (*Shaar HaKavanos*, Inyanei Pesach, end of section 4; *Pri Etz Chaim*, Shaar Chag HaMatzos, ch. 1; *Machzor Vitry*; *Siddur HaAriZal*—it is surprising that the Yaavetz writes that he and his father, the Chacham Tzvi, used to recite this verse, yet it is not found in any book). There are several versions regarding this: 1) **I passed by you** comes after **"And the land was filled with them,"** before the exposition of **"And numerous, as it is written"** (*Machzor Vitry*; *Siddur HaRashar*). 2) After **"naked and bare"** (*Kol Yaakov*, Yaavetz). 3) Our Rebbe’s version (and this is also how it appears in Sephardic Haggadahs in *Chafetz Chaim* and *Maggid*), and in *Simchas HaRegel* it is written that this is the version of the AriZal.
The reason for our Rebbe’s arrangement can be explained according to the *peshat*: a) "I passed by you, etc." has no connection to the exposition of "great, mighty." Furthermore, we do not find that the author of the Haggadah brings verses from **different** places in a single exposition. b) He arranged the verses as they are written in the text. The narrative "I passed by you" explains the matter of **"and numerous,"** as it concludes, "Live by your blood," and as it is written (Shemos 1:12), "And as they would afflict him, so he would multiply."

**And they did evil to us** (Devarim 26:6) – The continuation of the exposition in the parsha of *Arami Oved Avi*. And similarly later in the verses: "And we cried out," and "And He took us out."

**And they did evil** – Seemingly, what does this add to "and they afflicted us"? Therefore, the explanation is that **"they did evil"** means that the entire intent of the Mitzriyim (and not only that which they appointed taskmasters over them) was to find counsel on how to do evil to Yisrael. For this, he brings proof **"as it is written, 'Come, let us deal wisely.'"**

**Come, let us deal wisely** (Shemos 1:10).

**And they set over him** (ibid. 1:11).

**And they worked, etc., and they embittered, etc.** (Shemos 1:13-14). I have not yet found the verse "and they embittered" in any other Haggadah version, except in *Siddur Tefillah L'Moshe* by the Ramak, where it is implied that he also includes it in the Haggadah. It is impossible to clarify this through the variants of the Sifri, because the printers, starting from the first Venice edition (1560), omitted the entire Haggadah from the Sifri (and likewise from the *Yalkut Shimoni*). In the Sifri printed with the *Malbim* commentary, the Haggadah was also printed, **and in it is the verse "and they embittered,"** but I do not know who was instructed to do this. However, our Rebbe’s version is necessary, for the intent of the author of the Haggadah in his expositions is to explain and detail the matters more fully according to what is written elsewhere. When he comes to the narrative of how they imposed hard labor upon Yisrael, its explanation is explicit in the verse "and they embittered"—what this labor was and its manner—and the expression **"hard labor"** is also brought there. **Certainly,** the author of the Haggadah included it and would not have been satisfied with the verse "and they worked," which contains only a **general** notification that the labor was "with rigor" (*b'farech*)—especially since R. Elazar explains "with rigor" (*b'farech*) as "with a soft mouth" (*b'peh rach*) (Sotah 11b), and according to this, this verse does not speak of hard labor at all (and the words of the *Imrei Shefer* on this passage are difficult to understand, and seemingly he contradicts himself). As for why this verse was omitted in other versions, it can be said: the early scribes of the manuscripts of the Haggadah (and the Sifri) recorded in shorthand "And the Mitzriyim worked, etc., with rigor." Their intent was the **second** word "with rigor" at the end of the verse **"and they embittered."** A copyist who had not served his apprenticeship properly came along, and when he copied up to the **first** "with rigor," he stopped. He did not notice that the following verse also ends with this word.

**And we cried out** (Devarim 26:7).

**And it was in those days** (Shemos 2:23).

**And Hashem heard** (ibid. 2:24).

**Our affliction: this is the separation from marital relations.** "Affliction" (*onyeinu*) is from the word *inuy* (suffering), and separation from marital relations is called *inuy* (Bereishis 31:50, Yoma 74b).

**Separation from marital relations.** For Pharaoh also intended this in his decree, as the Sages relate (*Shemos Rabbah* 1:12; see also *Sefer HaLikkutim* on the verse "And as they would afflict him").

**As it is written, "And He saw"** (Shemos 2:25). Many commentators have questioned that there is no proof from this verse, and they have resolved it in various ways and allusions. Seemingly, the intent of the expounder is understood simply, for the proof **"as it is written"** relates to the word **"and He saw,"** and not to the exposition **"this is the separation from marital relations,"** which comes only as a parenthetical remark. It is as if he said: **"And He saw our affliction, as it is written, 'And Elokim saw, etc.,'"** except that, in passing, he explains what **"our affliction"** is. Similarly, this is also the case later with what is written: **"And our pressure: this is the crowding, as it is written, etc."** and **"And with great awe: this is the revelation of the Shechinah, as it is written, etc."**—that the proof is not for the definition of the words "pressure" and "great awe," but for the matter itself (and through this, the difficulties of the commentators there are removed)—and the definition of the words comes as a parenthetical remark. One should not object that if so, he should have brought only the word **"and He saw"** in this passage and made a separate passage for **"our affliction: this is the separation from marital relations,"** for it can be said that the word **"and He saw"** by itself is a truncated statement.

**Our toil: these are the children.** For they are the strength and vigor of a person, as it is written (Bereishis 49:3), "My strength and the beginning of my vigor" (*Machzor Vitry*). And the Sages say (Shabbos 32b), "What are the works of a person's hands? You must say, his sons and daughters" (*Shevet HaLevi*).

**As it is written, "Every son"** (Shemos 1:22) – Proof for the matter (and not for the definition), that they decreed against the children.

**Our pressure: this is the crowding.** The word *lachatz* (pressure) is used for physical crushing, and for coercion and oppression in doing something, and this is what is called in the words of the Sages "crowding" (*dochak*). He says that here it is like the second definition, that it was not enough that they enslaved them, but they also did not give them any rest in their labor, as it is written (Shemos 5:13), "And the taskmasters were pressing" (Zevach Pesach).

**And also I have seen** (Shemos 3:9) – See the paragraph above, **"As it is written, 'And He saw.'"**

**And Hashem took us out** (Devarim 26:8).

**An angel, a seraph, a messenger.** Regarding the distinction between these levels, see: *Ramban* and *Bachya* (Shemos 12:12), *Zevach Pesach*, *Maharal*, *Alshich*, *Siddur Kol Yaakov*, *Yaavetz*, *Gra*. In *Likkutei Torah*, *d.h. Lehavin Mah Sheb'Haggadah* 63: Angel, seraph, messenger—Asiyah, Yetzirah, Beriah.

**And not through a messenger.** In *Siddur R. Saadia Gaon*, there is an addition here: **"Not through a plague, but, etc."** And this requires study from the Zohar (Vol. I, 117b).

**In His glory and in Himself** (so it is in *Siddur HaAriZal*, *Abudarham*, *Zevach Pesach*, *Siddur Shelah*, *Yaavetz*) – **Malchus** of Ein Sof and the **Essence** of the Ein Sof that is before the Tzimtzum (writings of the Rebbe Rashab).

**And I will pass through** (Shemos 12:12).

**And I will pass through the land of Mitzrayim, I, and not, etc., and not another.** In *Rambam* it is not present, and likewise in *Shevet HaLevi* it is written that his teacher did not say it, but it is found, according to our version, in the Sifri, and likewise in *Siddur HaAriZal*, *Zevach Pesach*, *Maharal*, *Alshich*, *Shelah*, *Yaavetz*.

**I, and not an angel** – Brought and explained in the Zohar (Vol. I, 117a).

**I am He, and not another** – (So it is in all the above, except for the Sifri, where it is not there) – What else is it coming to exclude? See: *Maharal*, *Alshich*, *Yaavetz*, *Likkutei Torah* there.

**This is the plague, etc., this is the sword, etc., this is the blood.** The commentators asked why he singled out and detailed these three plagues, and in this specific order. In my humble opinion, it can be said that he is explaining the greatness of what He did: He killed their wealth (their livestock). Greater than this—He killed their firstborns (sword). And even greater than this—He also executed judgments against their gods, for the Nile, which they worshipped, was struck, as the Sages relate (*Shemos Rabbah* 9:9).

**Behold, the hand of Hashem** (Shemos 9:3).

**As it is written, "And his sword"** (Divrei Hayamim I 21:16) – We have found the expression of a "drawn" sword in the plague of the sword (*Shevet HaLevi*, *Zevach Pesach*), and it is the sword with which He killed the firstborns (*Zevach Pesach*, *Simchas HaRegel*).

**And with great awe** – (A term for appearance; and so in *Targum Onkelos* and *Targum Yonasan* and *Chazona Raba*) – **this is the revelation of the Shechinah** – at night, at the time of the plague of the firstborn and the redemption of Yisrael (*Shevet HaLevi*, *Abudarham*).

**As it is written, "Or has a god tried"** (Devarim 4:34) – It is evident that there was great awe. And the words **"this is the revelation of the Shechinah"** are a parenthetical remark, as above. And the difficulty of the commentators is removed.

**And the staff** (Shemos 4:17).

**And with wonders: this is the blood.** The first plague of the ten plagues, which he lists shortly after—so most commentators explain. And regarding these two explanations, it requires study, for if so, the author of the Haggadah should have clarified his intent.

**And I will show wonders - smoke** (Yoel 3:3).

**And the wrath "sheva"** (with a *heh*, not an *aleph*).

**"The rejoicing" (for that)** (the word **"for that"** is also circled) – So it is in several editions. In my humble opinion, this is how it should be.

And see *Menachos* 96a—R. Yehudah says so that you will not err, etc.).

**"How many levels," etc., "how much more so."** This is not in the Rambam, but it is present in the aforementioned passage in the paragraph **"Rabbi Yehuda HaGalili."** I have not found it, as of now, in any Midrash, but in the Zevach Pesach it is implied that this is the conclusion of the statement of Rabbi Akiva!

**"These," etc., "Dayenu."** One should not pause while reciting the fourteen "Dayenu"s.
In the *Machzor Vitry* it is written: **"Dayenu"** counts as one, and fifteen "Dayenu"s, etc. (and similarly in the *Siddur HaRashar*). Apparently, because the *yud* is emphasized, it is considered as two. Now, according to our version, there are only **fourteen**, and one could say—though it is forced—that it refers to the levels in which "Dayenu" is said. I later found that in the *Shibolei HaLeket* the version is: "If He had brought us into Eretz Yisrael and not built for us the Beis HaMikdash (meaning Shiloh, Nov, and Givon), Dayenu. If He had built for us the Beis HaMikdash and not built for us the Beis HaBechirah (the Beis HaMikdash in Yerushalayim), Dayenu." This is also how it appears in the first printing of the Haggadah for Pesach from the Soncino Machzor, 5246. If so—according to this version—there are fifteen "Dayenu"s. And considering that the author of the *Siddur Shaar HaShamayim* lived in Italy—the place of the author of the *Shibolei HaLeket* and the Soncino printing—it is highly probable that he also held by their version, which is why he writes that there are fifteen "Dayenu"s. But in the *Siddur HaRashar*, it requires further study.

**"If He had done to their gods and not killed their firstborn."** Seemingly, this requires study, as this is not in chronological order, as it is written: "And I will strike every firstborn... and against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments" (Shemos 12:12), and "And Hashem struck every firstborn... and every firstborn of cattle" (ibid. 12:29. And in the Mechilta and Tanchuma it says that the cattle were struck—because they were their idols).
Likewise, after this: "Killed their firstborn, gave us their wealth, split for us the sea"—this is also not in order.
And likewise, "Fed us the manna"—in Alush—"gave us the Shabbos"—in Marah, which was before it.
One could say that the aforementioned verses mean: "I will strike every firstborn," and this causes "and against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments." And similarly regarding the second verse. But the time of the execution of the judgments and the killing of the firstborn of the cattle was prior to the killing of their firstborn. This is also understood from the Mechilta (Shemos 12:29): "And every firstborn of cattle—so that they would not say, 'Our deity brought the punishment upon us.'" This is also implied by our Sages (Sukkah 29a): "When the sun is eclipsed, it is a bad omen for the nations... and there is no nation that is struck whose gods are not struck with it, as it is said: 'And against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments'" (see there in the Chiddushei Aggados). This is also explicitly in the Zohar (Vol. II, 18a and there; in Shemos Rabbah 21:5 it speaks about the **Prince** of the nation).
Likewise, regarding the giving of the wealth—the intention is to what they borrowed from Egypt, vessels of silver and vessels of gold, etc., and this was decided to be the property of Bnei Yisrael when it became known that they were not returning to Egypt.
And regarding the manna and Shabbos—it can be said that this is according to the one who says that in Alush the Shabbos was given (Yerushalmi Beitzah 2:5, Devarim Rabbah 3:1. And see Mechilta to Shemos 15:25). Or according to the commentators (Rivav to Shemos 16:1) that even though it was given in Marah, Moshe forgot to warn them until they came to Alush.

**"And had not split for us the sea."** And then the Ribono shel Olam would not have commanded them to return and camp by the sea and wait for Pharaoh, and He would not have strengthened the heart of Pharaoh to pursue them, for all this was done for the sake of the miracle of the splitting of the sea, as it is written at the beginning of Parshas Beshalach (and thus the difficulties of the commentators are resolved)—**Dayenu.**

**"And had not brought us through it 'on dry land'"**—but rather through a little water or mud, Dayenu (Zevach Pesach, Abudraham, Maharal). This requires study, as it should have said **"on dry land"** instead of **"on dry ground"**: 1) Like the language of the verse. 2) "Dry land" indicates that it was completely dry, whereas "dry ground" does not. As it is written (Bereishis 8:13-14): "In the first... the face of the ground was dried," and "In the second month, on the twenty-seventh day of the month, the earth was dry."

**"And had not supplied our needs in the desert"**—meaning that we would have bought our needs from the merchants of the nations of the world, according to our Sages in Yoma (75b)—**Dayenu.**

**"If He had brought us before Mount Sinai"**—for this it is said: "Hashem our G-d showed us His glory and His greatness" (Devarim 5:21). And in the version of Mussaf for Rosh Hashanah: "You were revealed in the cloud of Your glory upon Mount Sinai"—**"and had not given," etc., Dayenu.**

**It can also be said:** **"If He had brought us before Mount Sinai"**—and we would have also heard the Ten Commandments—**"and had not given us the Torah"**—afterward, meaning the Tablets, the Torah, and the Mitzvos—**Dayenu.**

**Rabban Gamliel—"And we say before Him Halleluyah"** (Mishnah Pesachim 116a-b. And according to the Rambam there is a different version there, and therefore it is also added in the Haggadah).

**"Anyone who has not said"**—who has not explained their reasons (Rashbam).
**"Has not fulfilled his obligation"**—properly, but he has fulfilled it according to the Torah (Ran, Pesachim there. And such is the opinion of the Rif, Rashba, Abudraham, and others—who wrote that by mere speech one fulfills the positive commandment of telling the story of the exodus from Egypt. See above, the paragraph **"It is a mitzvah upon us to tell"**).

**"That our fathers were eating, etc., that we are eating, etc."** All of this is not in the Mishnah and is an addition by the compiler of the Haggadah (and this was already questioned in the *Simchas HaRegel* regarding the Rashbatz who wrote otherwise).

**"And you shall say, 'It is a Pesach sacrifice'"** (Shemos 12:27).
**"He takes the broken piece in his hand, etc., he takes the maror in his hand."** In Pesachim 116b it says **"to lift."** But in the *Siddur Rashi* and *Machzor Vitry* it is written: When he reaches "This matzah, this maror," he **places** his hand upon them (and perhaps they had a different version in the Gemara). And the custom of the Rebbe's house is to hold the matzos, the second and the third (by means of the cloth that is upon them), and **to place** his hands upon the maror and the *korech*—until **"on account of the second."**

**"This matzah, etc., on account of the fact that there was not enough time, etc."** In the Mishnah (there) it is in brief: **"On account of the fact that our fathers were redeemed from Egypt."** And the author of the Haggadah explains and clarifies: On account of the fact that they were redeemed, and on account of the **manner** of the redemption, which was with such haste that there was not enough time, etc.—for only in this can it be understood why the redemption of our fathers obligates us to eat matzah.
It can be said that the two things—the essence of the redemption and its manner—are one, according to what is written in the seforim (*Siddur HaRashar* and *Kav VeNaki* here; *Tzeror HaMor*—cited in the Shelah, paragraph "We were slaves"—*Simchas HaRegel* there) that if Yisrael had stayed one more moment in Egypt, they would never have been redeemed—and according to this, it is also understood why it emphasizes in the Mechilta (Shemos 12:41): "Since the time arrived, the verse did not delay them **for the blink of an eye.**"

**"On account of the fact that there was not enough time."** Even though they were commanded regarding the matzos before this (Shemos 12)—the Ribono shel Olam commanded them to make the matzah as a remembrance of the wonder that He was destined to perform for them. And likewise, they were commanded regarding the Pesach—see "And Hashem will pass over," as mentioned above—before the act of the miracle (Zevach Pesach, Shibolei HaLeket, Abudraham; Likkutei Torah, discourse beginning "To understand what is written in the Haggadah").

**"King of the kings of kings."** Kings—those below in this world. Kings of kings—the supernal ministers in the heavenly host, as it is written (Daniel 10:13), "And the minister of the kingdom of Persia." And the Ribono shel Olam is called King over the kings of kings (Likkutei Torah there).

**"Matzah cakes, for it was not leavened, for they were driven out of Egypt and could not tarry."** For if they could have tarried, they would have baked chametz, for the Pesach of Egypt was only for a night and a day (Ran, Shibolei HaLeket, Likkutei Torah there).

**"And they baked the dough"** (Shemos 12:39).
**"And they embittered"** (Shemos 1:14).
**"In every generation and generation."** And in every generation and generation—every day and day, and as if he went out **today** from Egypt (Tanya, beginning of Chapter 47. See there).

**"And you shall tell your son on that day, etc., 'Because of this Hashem did for me'"** (Shemos 13:8)—it follows that one must say **"for me"** even **"on that day,"** meaning even many generations after the exodus from Egypt (Maharsha, Pesachim there).

**"Not our fathers."** An addition by the compiler of the Haggadah, and the matter is doubled to strengthen it. And it is not in the Mishnah.

**"And us He brought out"** (Devarim 6:23). In Pesachim (116b): One must say "And us He brought out," etc.

**"He covers, etc., and lifts, etc., He redeemed us."** (Tur and Shulchan Aruch). This requires study, as he should have said—like the language of the *Hagahos Maimoniyos*—until he blesses *Borei Pri HaGafen* and drinks. Perhaps the reason is that for the blessing of *Borei Pri HaGafen* one only needs to **hold** the cup, not **lift** it.

**"He covers the bread, etc."** In *Torah Or* (page 188b) he copied the gloss of the Tzemach Tzedek on this: "And he shall hold it in his hand until he concludes 'He redeemed Yisrael.'" And the reason is explained in the Tur in the name of the Midrash. From this it is proven that the words **"and he holds it, etc., He redeemed us"** were not in his Siddur. And likewise, they are not in the *Siddur Kol Yaakov*, Shelah, Yaavetz.
The custom of the Rebbe's house (**despite** what is written in the glosses of the Admur the Nasi in *Torah Or*, page 233) is to lift the cup and hold it until he finishes **"And we say before Him Halleluyah"**—this custom is brought in the *Hagahos Maimoniyos*—and he places it on the table and lifts it again for the blessing "Who redeemed us" (and so wrote the *Aruch HaShulchan*).

**"He covers the bread"**—so that it is not seen during the drinking—**"and lifts the cup"**—for one only says song over wine (see above, the paragraph **"One must lift"**).

**"Therefore—because He brought us out and redeemed us—we are obligated to thank, etc."**—nine praises (this is according to our version in the Mishnah in the Bavli. *Machzor Vitry*. Maharal. *Matamim* in the name of the Rokeach. Tiferes Yisrael, Pesachim there, *Siddur HaRashar*)—**"before Him Halleluyah"**—the tenth praise, corresponding to the ten expressions of praise that David said in Tehillim, and the greatest of them all is "Halleluyah," which includes the Name and the praise at once (Pesachim 117a. *Machzor Vitry*).

**"And we say before Him Halleluyah"** (so it is in the Mishnah. Rif. Rambam. Shibolei HaLeket. Abudraham—his own opinion. Maharal. *Siddur Yaavetz*).

**"Halleluyah, praise—to the fountains of water"** (Tehillim 113-114). One should say Hallel just as Yisrael said when they went out of Egypt, as our Sages say—Pesachim 117a (*Machzor Vitry*). Up to where does one say it? Beis Hillel says until "the flint into a fountain of water" (Pesachim 116b).

One does not make a blessing over this Hallel. Rabbi Tzemach Gaon, of blessed memory, explained that it is because one pauses in it for the meal, and Rav Hai Gaon, of blessed memory, explained that it is only like reading from the Torah (*Or Zarua*, Vol. I, beginning of section 43).
And on this night we read it even while sitting, even though the mitzvah of reading Hallel is while standing, because all the actions of this night are in the manner of freedom (Shulchan Aruch Admur, 473, sections 47-48).
The chapters "Halleluyah" and "When Yisrael went out" speak of the exodus from Egypt, the splitting of the sea, and the giving of the Torah (Pesachim 118a), which occurred in the past, and we pause in them and established after them the blessing of the redemption and the eating of the matzah, for all this is a remembrance of the exodus from Egypt. And the chapters that follow them, which speak of the future to come (Pesachim there), come after the meal, because they are a separate matter (Zevach Pesach).

**"Blessed, etc., Who redeemed us"** (Mishnah Pesachim 116b, according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva and the version of the Rif, Rosh, etc. See *Dikdukei Sofrim* there)—this blessing is in place of the blessing "Who performed miracles" (*Rashi* in *Sefer HaOrah*. Abudraham and see above, the paragraph **"Shehecheyanu"**).
**"Redeemed us"**—as mentioned above, that if the Ribono shel Olam had not brought our fathers out of Egypt, we would be enslaved. And thus we **feel** the redemption. And therefore he places **"redeemed us"** before **"to redeem our fathers,"** for that is only what our fathers told us. This is not the case in the paragraph "Therefore," where he says: "Who performed **for our fathers**—and afterward—**and for us the miracles**"—for the fathers saw and were close to the miracles (Chasam Sofer).

**"For the appointed times"**—Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur—**"and for the festivals"**—Pesach, Shavuos, and Sukkos (Shibolei HaLeket. Abudraham).

**"From the sacrifices"**—the Chagigah (which is brought before the Pesach so that it may be eaten while satisfied. Pesachim 70a) and afterward—**"and from the Pesach sacrifices."**

**"On the conclusion of Shabbos"**—(when the Chagigah is not brought on Erev Pesach that falls on Shabbos. Pesachim 69b) one eats there—**"from the Pesach sacrifices and from the sacrifices—the Chagigah that will come on the following day"** (Shulchan Aruch Admur 473, section 40).

**"The wall of Your altar."** The language of the verse (Vayikra 1:15). It requires study why he changed here specifically to bring this language. And in the *Siddur Yaavetz* he explains it according to the secret. And see *Zohar Chadash* (228b).

**"A new song"**—masculine form (Mechilta. Cited in *Tosafos* to "And we say," Pesachim 116b).

**"And he takes the cup in his hand."** These words are a printing error (*Shaar HaKavanos*).

**"And he blesses."** One makes the first blessing over each cup of the four cups—but one does not make the final blessing except after the last one (Rav Sherira and Rav Hai Gaon). The reason: Each of the four cups is considered a mitzvah and a way of freedom in itself and is not exempt by the blessing of the second. But regarding the final blessing—even a distraction does not necessitate that this blessing be **immediately** after the eating and drinking, and one can wait until one decides to eat or drink and bless after them and exempt this one as well (Shulchan Aruch Admur, end of section 474 and sources cited there).

## Rachzah

**Rachzah.** **And afterwards.** It requires study as to why sometimes the words **"and afterwards"** are added, and sometimes they are not.

## Motzi Matzah

*Motzi* and then *Matzah*, because between a frequent and an infrequent occurrence, the frequent takes precedence (see *Berachos* 51b). It is the standard procedure to bless Hashem, who brings forth bread from the earth, and then to bless over the *Matzah*, from which a *mitzvah* is performed (*Shulchan Aruch* 273).

We do not recite *Shehecheyanu* over the *mitzvah* of *Matzah*, because the *Shehecheyanu* recited in *Kiddush*—"who has granted us life... to eat *Matzah* and *Maror* on this night"—is sufficient. Alternatively, it was already recited during *Kiddush* (*Abudraham*; *Birkei Yosef* 473). The second reason is difficult to understand, for if so, one would need to have in mind during the *Shehecheyanu* of *Kiddush* that it also covers the *Matzah*, similar to the *Shehecheyanu* of the *Megillah* reading, where one must have in mind the *Mishloach Manos* and the festive meal, as written by the *Shelah* and the *Magen Avraham* (beginning of 692).

**Matzah.** Even in our time, this is a Torah obligation (*Pesachim* 120a), for both men and women (ibid. 43b). The Torah introduced a specific requirement on the nights of *Pesach*—even though one is obligated to eat bread on other *Yomim Tovim* as well—that one does not fulfill this obligation with *Matzah Ashirah* (enriched *Matzah*) (*Ran*, *Perek HaYashan*). Some say that the obligation to eat bread on *Yom Tov* is only Rabbinic (*Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 529:6; see *Rambam*, *Hilchos Yom Tov* 6:16. This is difficult to reconcile with what our Rebbe wrote in 242:1 in brackets. The opinion of the *Ri* in *Rosh*, *Berachos* (ibid. 23) implies that it is a Torah obligation—see there in *Mishnah Berurah*—due to the requirement of *simcha* (joy). However, there is no *simcha* on the first night of *Yom Tov* according to Torah law—see *Sha’agas Aryeh* 68 at length—and if so, even according to his view, it would only be Rabbinic at that time, except for the first night of *Chag HaSukkos*). It is only on the first night of *Chag HaSukkos* that we derive this from the *Matzah* of *Chag HaPesach*. (What is written in *Responsa R’ Akiva Eiger* 1 that it applies only to *Pesach* night, and there in the *Hashmatos* he writes simply that bread is not relevant to *simcha*—this is difficult to understand.) This is not the place to elaborate.

**Matzah.** A *Mekubaldiker vort* (a received teaching):
*Matzah Shemurah* is called by two essential and internal names: *Michla d’Meheimanusa* (food of faith) and *Michla d’Asusa* (food of healing); it provides strength in *Da’as*.
This is the internal, essential point—the *vort*—that our master, the Maggid of Mezeritch, received from his holy master, the Baal Shem Tov, in the name of his master, the Baal Chai, and transmitted it to his esteemed disciple who received the internal dimension, our Rebbe the Alter Rebbe, and it has come down through *yichud* and *birur* from Rebbe to Rebbe.
Each one of our holy Rebbes, our ancestors, in his generation, repeated, transmitted, and explained this "point-word" (*Sicha*, 11 Nissan 5709; *Sefer HaMaamarim 5709*).

**Matzah.** The Alter Rebbe said: *Michla d’Meheimanusa*—the first night. *Michla d’Asusa*—the second night. The Mitteler Rebbe explained the difference: When healing leads to faith, it means the person was ill and subsequently healed, and he thanks Hashem for his recovery; healing that comes through faith means that from the outset, he does not come to illness at all. He added: This is the same in both the physical and spiritual realms, for among the Jewish people, the physical is not separate from the spiritual; from the spiritual, it is drawn into the physical, and the physical is transformed into the spiritual (*Sichos, Chag HaPesach 5742*; see *Zohar Chadash* 183b, 41a; *Likutei Torah, Tzav* 13d).

**The broken piece between the two whole ones.** Because, as on every *Yom Tov*, one is obligated to break bread over *Lechem Mishneh* (*Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim* 529), and our Sages taught that on *Pesach*, one adds to them another broken *Matzah* because of the "bread of poverty" (*Rosh*; *Mordechai*; *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 475:3).

**He shall hold them in his hand and recite the blessing,** because for anything one blesses over to eat, one must hold it in his right hand while reciting the blessing (*Shulchan Aruch* 206:4). Here, some say the blessing of *HaMotzi* is on the whole *Matzah* and the blessing of *Al Achilas Matzah* is on the broken one (*Rashi, Rashbam, Ri, and Rosh* in *Pesachim* 116a, and this is the primary view), while others say the opposite (*Sefer Mitzvos Gadol; Raavan; Tosafos* and other *Geonim* as written in *Hagahos Maimoniyos*, end of *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*). To fulfill both opinions, he holds both in his hand (*Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 475:5; see *Pri Megadim, Mishbetzos Zahav* 2).

**And he shall not break from them.** So that the blessing is recited over a whole item.

**He shall set down the third Matzah to be removed from his hand and recite the blessing** (*Maharil*). Even though he intends for this blessing to also exempt the eating of the third *Matzah*, as he concludes. One could say this is to show that, nevertheless, the primary connection of this blessing is to the first and second *Matzah*, even according to the view of Hillel, as mentioned above in the paragraph **Korech**. It seems more likely to me that this is so he does not mistakenly break from the third *Matzah* as well, just as we find that he hides the *Afikoman* for this reason (see above, paragraph **The larger part**).

**And he shall intend to also exempt the eating of the Korech.** Therefore, one is careful in the interim not to engage in conversation that is not related to the meal (*R’ Yechiel, brother of the Baal HaTurim; Ibn Yarchi*).

**To exempt, etc., the eating of the Afikoman**—(*Shelah*)—and this is an extra stringency (*Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 475:18). Therefore, in this case, one is not careful about interrupting with conversation—*Minhagei Beis HaRav*.

**"Al" Achilas Matzah, etc., Maror**—and not **"Le'echol"** (to eat) *Matzah*, etc., *Maror* (*Pardes* of Rashi; *Ri Gias* in the name of *Rav Gaon*; *Rambam*, etc.) because he is also exempting others who are listening (*Hagahos Maimoniyos, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah* 8:12; see *Migdal Oz*, 11:15 of *Hilchos Berachos*).

**And then he shall break a *kezayis* from each one**—since the eating of *Matzah* must be a *kezayis*, and some say this is from the first *Matzah* and some say it is from the second, as mentioned above.

**And he shall eat them.** He does not dip them in salt because of the affection for the *mitzvah*, so that there is no mixture of any other taste at all (*Maharil*; *Shulchan Aruch* of the Alter Rebbe 475:10)—and this is the custom of *Beis HaRav* (this is difficult to understand, as the *Shelah* wrote that according to the *Arizal*, one should dip in salt; note the *Likutei Torah*, explanation of the discourse *Lo Tashbit* 6).

**Together.** Meaning, he should place them into his mouth at the same time. The reason is so there will be no interruption between the blessing of *Achilas Matzah* and the eating of the *kezayis* that pertains to it, which is from the first or second *Matzah* as mentioned above (*Tur Shulchan Aruch* ibid.).

## Maror

**Maror.** In our current time, when there is no Korban Pesach, the obligation is only Rabbinic (Pesachim 120a). Women are also obligated in this, as they are in the other mitzvos of this night (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 472:25).

**And he shall dip** — and afterwards — **and he shall recite the blessing.** This is to ensure the blessing is proximate to the performance of the mitzvah, as explained above in the section **And he shall dip.**

**And he shall dip in Charoses.** Before dipping, one should soften the Charoses with the wine that is in the vessel (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 473:32-34). One should not submerge the entire kezayis in the Charoses, because nowadays we do not find the *kapa* (a type of worm) in the Maror as they did in the days of the Gemara. We only dip in Charoses as a mitzvah in memory of the mortar (Pri Chadash; Shulchan Aruch HaRav 475:11). It is preferable not to submerge the whole thing, so as not to nullify the taste of bitterness; this is a possible explanation.

**And he shall shake off the Charoses** (based on what is written in Pesachim 115b).

**And he shall recite the blessing.** It requires study why [the Rebbe] did not transcribe what is written in his Shulchan Aruch (475:18) — and such is our custom — that one must intend for this blessing to also exempt the Maror in the Korech sandwich.

**This blessing.** On the face of it, this is redundant phrasing. Perhaps it hints, in passing, that there could have been room for another blessing: the blessing of *al mitzvas* over the Charoses (see Rambam’s Commentary on the Mishnah, Pesachim 10:3, and Lechem Mishneh on Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 7:11), even though one is not obligated to recite a *berachah me’ein ha’sheva* over it. The reason we do not recite a blessing over its mitzvah is that it is secondary to the Maror and is exempted by its blessing (Abudraham). It must be that its mitzvah is secondary to the mitzvah of Maror. This can be explained based on what the Maharal writes: "Charoses is a mitzvah, because it is not mentioned regarding Maror except that 'they embittered their lives with hard labor,' and there is bitterness for a person in hard labor, but it is not absolute bitterness. Therefore, we make a remembrance of the mortar, and there is nothing harder than the work of mortar, as our Sages said in Shemos Rabbah (1:27)."

**Blessed are You, etc., Maror.** The language of the verse is *merorim* (bitter herbs) (and see Pesachim 39a). This is also the text of the blessing found in the Rambam (Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 8:6, 8:2; see also Yerushalmi Pesachim 9:3, 10:5 — though the singular form is used in Rambam there, 8:4, 8:8, 8:12. See also the variations in the text of his Haggadah: "On this night, Maror, etc., these bitter herbs that we eat." This requires study).

## Korech

**Korech** (Pesachim 115a). According to Hillel the Elder: At the time when the Korban Pesach existed, one does not fulfill his obligation unless he wraps the Pesach, matzah, and maror together and eats them as one. At the present time, when there is no Korban Pesach, one does not fulfill the obligation of matzah—which is a Torah requirement—unless he eats it **separately.** And he does not fulfill the obligation of maror—which the Sages instituted **as a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash**—unless he wraps it with matzah and eats them together (just as was done during the time of the Beis HaMikdash). Consequently, according to Hillel, there is a **second** obligation of matzah today, by Rabbinic decree. Since the Halacha was not decided definitively either like Hillel or like his colleagues, one must fulfill the requirements of both. (See Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, 475:16-18, and the commentaries and the sefer Shevach Pesach for an extensive discussion).

**He shall dip, etc., and recline:** Since we perform this as a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash in accordance with Hillel, and he would dip and recline (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, 475:19-20).

**He shall dip into the charoses:** Only the maror (chazeres), not the matzah. Those who are meticulous about avoiding "soaked" (gebrochts) matzah do not dip it; rather, they place a small amount of dry charoses on the maror and shake it off afterward. This is the custom of the Rebbe’s household.

**And he shall say "This":** Seemingly, the word **"This"** is superfluous. Perhaps it emphasizes that one must say specifically this version: omitting the words **"a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash in accordance with Hillel"** and adding the word **"Pesach."**

**"Thus did Hillel, etc., when the Beis HaMikdash was, etc., Pesach, etc.":** This is the version in Sefer Kol Yaakov. It is also found in Chayei Adam, Aruch HaShulchan, and Mishnah Berurah (based on the addition of the Taz), although they omit the word **"was"** (the first one), and it is likewise absent from the Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, 475:18. See below regarding this.

One might explain the precision of this version: 
a) We do not say **"a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash in accordance with Hillel,"** just as we do not say it when eating maror [earlier in the Seder] as **"a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash."** The Pri Megadim (475, Mishbetzos Zahav, sub-section 7) explains the recitation as a notification that it is only a **remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash**, but that **at the present time** one does not fulfill the obligation through the wrapping. However, since we say this after having already eaten matzah and maror and having fulfilled the obligation, the intent of the Pri Megadim must be that the notification serves to warn one not to err in the coming year by thinking that the Halacha is like Hillel, and mistakenly believing that Hillel holds that even now one fulfills the obligation through the wrapping, and forgetting that last year he ate the matzah beforehand, and remembering only that he said "a remembrance of the Beis HaMikdash." This is a very forced explanation. Especially since in the Korech section there is a slight redundancy, as one immediately says: "Thus did Hillel do at the time the Beis HaMikdash was, etc." Furthermore, one should not interrupt between the blessing and the eating with anything unnecessary, as mentioned above (and the Mishnah Berurah 475:1, in the Biur Halacha, wishes to push off the entire recitation until after one has eaten—for this very reason). This is especially true according to the Alter Rebbe, that for Hillel, one does not fulfill the obligation of maror except through the wrapping, as mentioned above.

Furthermore, through this recitation, one might come to err that the wrapping is *only* a remembrance. In truth, only now does one fulfill the obligation of maror according to Hillel, and one must fulfill it according to his view as well, as mentioned above (unlike the Pri Megadim, who follows his own view that at the present time, even according to Hillel, one fulfills the obligation of maror when eating it separately. If so, it is indeed only a remembrance).

b) **"When the Beis HaMikdash was standing":** This is the version in Sefer Kol Yaakov, the Resher, and Simchas HaRegel. But for our Rebbe, it requires study, since in the recitation of the *Ketores* passage, his version is: "You are He, etc., at the time when the Beis HaMikdash is standing." And so it says above: "The Pesach that they would, etc., when the Beis HaMikdash is standing."

However, in Sefer Kol Yaakov and the Resher, in all the above instances, it says: **"was"** standing. In Sefer Yaavetz and the Shelah, the word **"was"** is absent in all three instances.

c) **"He would wrap the Pesach, matzah, and maror":** According to the commentators (except for the Rambam in Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 8:6, though see the Lechem Mishnah there), this is what Hillel did. If one omits **"Pesach,"** he would be speaking untruths in his narrative. Regarding the question raised in Chayei Adam—that if so, how can one say "Thus did Hillel do," when there is no Pesach in our hands?—it can be said that the word **"Thus"** carries over to the **following** part, and it is the **beginning** of the subject: "Thus did Hillel do, as he will relate." And he did not need to conclude "and my wrapping is a remembrance of this" (even though it is not exactly the same), because it is self-evident, and as mentioned above, one should minimize speech between the blessing and the eating. Alternatively, one can say **"Thus"**—just as I am wrapping the maror and not eating it separately now, so too did Hillel do, to wrap the maror. Especially since, according to the Alter Rebbe, the primary purpose of the Korech is for the obligation of eating maror.

**"Hillel did at the time the Beis HaMikdash was standing":** All the days of Hillel were while the Beis HaMikdash existed—if so, seemingly the words **"at the time, etc."** are superfluous. They are not in the Gemara. Perhaps it can be said that it is to emphasize that specifically because the Beis HaMikdash was standing, he did so; whereas at the present time, one does not fulfill the obligation of matzah through the wrapping.

**"With matzos"** (Bamidbar 9:11): This is written regarding the *Pesach Sheni*. It does not cite what is written regarding the *Pesach Rishon* (Shemos 12:8), "And they shall eat the meat, etc., and with matzos they shall eat it," because, on the contrary, from the extra word **"they shall eat it"** in that verse, we learn that one eats the Pesach even if he does not have matzah and maror (Mechilta there). Also, the literal meaning of this verse is as the Ramban writes there: that "matzos" follows from above, "And they shall eat the meat and matzos," and then it commands "with maror they shall eat it" regarding the meat. If so, there is no proof that one must wrap it with matzah as well, were it not for the fact that the verse appears in *Pesach Sheni*, and from it we learn for *Pesach Rishon* as well (see Rashaash there). However, the Rashbam implies that he has the verse of *Pesach Rishon* in his version of the Gemara, as written in the glosses of the Rav of Ransburg.

## Shulchan Orech

Our custom for **Shulchan Orech** is to eat without reclining. On both nights, we begin by eating the egg (from the Ke'arah), dipping it in salt water—this is a remembrance of the mourning for the destruction of the Beis Hamikdash, for when it stood, we would offer and eat the Korban Pesach (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, end of 476).

We are extremely careful regarding *matzah sheruyah* (see the Responsa of the Alter Rebbe, 6; and see Shaarei Teshuvah, end of 460). Therefore, the matzos on the table are kept covered, lest water fall upon them. Before pouring water, or liquids containing water, into a cup or bowl, we check to ensure there are no matzah crumbs in the vessel. For the middle and final washings of the hands (*mayim emtza'im* and *mayim acharonim*), one should not pass his hands over his lips. (This applies throughout all seven days of Pesach, whereas on the last day of Pesach, one is **mehader** to soak the matzah). From the morning of the 14th of Nissan until after the *Korech* of the second Seder, we do not eat any of the ingredients that go into the *charoses* or *maror*.

I later found: Some have the custom not to eat *chazeres* (lettuce) before Pesach, and this was the custom of the Rashba (Rabbeinu Yeruchem, Nesiv 5, part 4).

It should be noted that some say that when the Beis Hamikdash stood, they would eat the entire meal (reciting *Hamotzi* over dough products, etc.) before the Seder, and only afterwards would the Seder—the eating of the *mitzvah* matzah, *maror*, and the Korban Pesach—take place (Mordechai, end of Pesachim; he disagrees on this point with the Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah, ch. 8).

**"He eats and drinks as much as he needs."** In *Pri Etz Chaim*: **"And he eats as much as he needs."** It is the same in the Tur and *Machzor Vitry*. In *Siddur Yaavetz*: **"They eat and drink as much as they need."** It can be said that this teaches us two things: 1) That one should eat as much as he needs so that he will eat the *Afikoman* while satiated, as it is a remembrance of the Korban Pesach, which was eaten while satiated. 2) That one should not eat more than he needs, so that the eating of the *Afikoman* will not be considered *achilah gasah* (forced or gluttonous eating).

## Tzafun

**Tzafun, etc., a kezayis.** In the Shulchan Aruch (477:3) it is written: "Ideally, it is good to eat two kezaysim: one in memory of the Korban Pesach, and one in memory of the matzah that was eaten with the Korban Pesach." This is the custom of the Rebbe’s household.
- Meaning: There are two opinions—that the kezayis of the Afikoman is in memory of the Korban Pesach, or that it is in memory of the matzah eaten (with the Korban Pesach) when one is already satiated. To fulfill both, it is good to eat two kezaysim. [It may be said that if this is difficult for him, and he eats only one kezayis, he should stipulate that it is in memory of the opinion that is halachically accepted.] It was not ordained to have a memory for the second opinion regarding the maror eaten when satiated, because at a time when there is no Korban Pesach, the obligation of maror is only rabbinic (see Bach, 477; Divrei Peirush there).
- One should not state the reason for this lack of ordinance, because the entire essence of maror—according to the Torah—is merely an accessory to the Korban Pesach (Pesachim 90a) and is not a separate mitzvah (Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 2:1)—for there is an obligation for maror even if one is not obligated in a Korban Pesach (see Pesachim 91b; Kiddushin 37b; Tosefta, Pesachim, end of chapter 2; Tzafnas Paneach on the Rambam there).

**And one should be careful not to drink after the Afikoman** (Rif—so written in Chayei Adam and Maggid Mishneh in the Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, 478. However, this requires study, as written in the Pri Megadim, Eshel Avraham, 478:1). So that the taste of the Afikoman is not nullified (this is implied by the Beis Yosef and Rema, 481:1).
Seemingly, this requires study: 1) He should have warned not to **eat** after the Afikoman, especially since eating is more severe than drinking, as written in the Tur and Shulchan Aruch and commentaries, 478 and 481. 2) He should have said, "**It is forbidden to drink**," or "**One should not drink** after the Afikoman," as all the poskim write. What is the meaning of "**And one should be careful not to drink**"? 3) This warning should have been written after the conclusion of the laws of eating the Afikoman, since its time is after that.
It may be said: The meaning of "and one should be careful not to drink" is that one should strive not to be thirsty. With this, the above is understood: 1) There was no need to warn against eating, because in any case, the Afikoman must be eaten **when one is already satiated**. 2) He did not come to teach us the law of the prohibition of eating or drinking after the Afikoman, for the place for such laws is in the Shulchan Aruch, not in the Siddur. He only teaches us the conduct of the Seder: that one should drink in a way that he will not be thirsty afterward. 3) It is understood that the time for this caution is immediately **before** eating the Afikoman, and therefore he wrote it **adjacent to and before** the word "and he shall eat." Alternatively: If one does not strive not to be thirsty and develops a great desire to drink, he would be permitted to rely on the opinion of some poskim and drink other beverages (481:1, in the Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe), and therefore we warn about this, which is not the case with eating.

**Not to drink**—Since he stated it generally, it implies: 1) That one should be careful regarding all drinking, even water (R’ Shmuel mi-Evreux in the Mordechai, end of chapter Arvei Pesachim). 2) That one must also be careful on the second night (Shaar HaTziyun). **And this is our custom.**

**And one should be careful not to drink after the Afikoman.** According to the letter of the law, there is no prohibition, etc., and one who eats or drinks (on the second night) is not liable for anything, except that he is found to be **removing himself from the category of the mehadrin (those who perform the mitzvah in the most beautiful manner) and transgressing the words of the Sages who said, "A person should never remove himself from the community"** (Admur in Shulchan Aruch 481, end of section 1—from Shaar HaTziyun, etc.). **A clear ruling** as to how much every person is obligated in *hiddur mitzvah* (beautifying the mitzvah), even when it is not according to the letter of the law and he is not liable for anything!

**And he shall eat while reclining** (Rosh, Arvei Pesachim, siman 26; and Tur and Shulchan Aruch 477:1. And not like the implication of the Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 7:5 and 8:10. See Hagahos Maimoniyos there and Maggid Mishneh to the Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, 477:2).

**Before chatzos (midnight)** (Tosafos, s.v. "Le'atuyei," Megillah 21a), like the Korban Pesach, which was only eaten until chatzos.
In his Shulchan Aruch, he wrote that ideally, it is good to finish the Hallel also before chatzos (Ran, end of Arvei Pesachim; end of chapter 2 of Megillah). However, we are not particular about this in the Rebbe’s household.

## Barech

**Mayim Acharonim.** The Alter Rebbe was asked by his son (the Mitteler Rebbe): What will be the function of Mayim Acharonim in the time to come, when the verse "I will remove the spirit of impurity from the land" is fulfilled? He replied: Its function then will relate to those who engaged in worldly matters with purity (Sichos of Pesach 1942).

**Barech.** All instances of "מעיני" in Bircas Hamazon are recited with a cholam. One holds the cup in his hand, raised, until the end of "Boneh Vrachamav Yerushalayim, Amen." He then places it down, and picks it up again for the blessing of Borei Pri Hagafen. Regarding "Yaaleh Veyavo": The one reciting the blessing raises his voice slightly when saying "Zochreinu, etc." in it (the *beis* is dageshed) "letovah, etc." in it (the *beis* is rafeh) "livrachah, etc." "lechayim tovim" — and all those seated answer "Amen" after "letovah," "livrachah," and "tovim." The text: "**Melech** chanun verachum atah." One does not answer "Amen" after "**yachserenu.**" Everyone says "Avi Mori Baal Habayis Hazeh" and "Immi Morasi Baal Habayis Hazeh," even a guest or someone who does not have his father or mother — this is the custom throughout the entire year as well.

**Kos Shel Eliyahu** (18th [of Nissan]. Shulchan Aruch of the Rebbe, end of section 480). This cup is poured after Bircas Hamazon (except at certain times — Sicha of Pesach 1943). The Rebbe Rayatz was meticulous about pouring this cup himself, and from generation to generation, the Admurim were meticulous about this.

The reason for this cup is that Eliyahu is the one who testifies that the Jewish people observe the mitzvah of milah (according to what is written in Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer, ch. 29), which is a prerequisite for Pesach, as it is written: "And no uncircumcised person shall eat of it" (Rabbi Moshe Hagiz in Sefer Birchas Eliyahu). This can be further clarified by what is written (Shemos Rabbah 19:5) that on this night, the Jewish people circumcised themselves [and although one only circumcises during the day — as has been questioned in many books — it can be explained simply that the law that a delayed milah must be performed during the day was only established after the Giving of the Torah (Tosafos, s.v. "Mar," Yevamos 72b)].

However, it is difficult, because according to this reason, this cup should be poured at the beginning of the Seder, or at the very least before eating the Afikoman, which is a remembrance of the Korban Pesach.

**Mozgin Kos, etc., Shefoch, etc.** It is difficult to understand why this part of the Haggadah is not included in the mnemonic "Kadesh, etc.," because the section **Barech** has already concluded, and **Hallel** is written after this. In the mnemonic "Kadesh U'rechatz," the mnemonic **Hallel** is written before "Shefoch."

**Mozgin Kos Daled.** Before reciting "Shefoch Chamascha" (Section 273; Ran, beginning of ch. Arvei Pesachim; Bachya, Parshas Va'era; Abudraham). This is because the four cups correspond to the four cups of retribution that Hashem will cause the nations of the world to drink — Yerushalmi, Pesachim 10:1 — and we say: With this cup, we are completing the mitzvah; therefore, fulfill what You have promised us and "pour out Your wrath, etc." (ibid).

It is puzzling why they did not explain that this is based on the words of Rabbi Levi in the Yerushalmi there, that the four cups correspond to the four kingdoms. If so, the fourth cup is a hint to the exile and the final redemption, and therefore it is specifically then that we say "Shefoch" (this resolves the difficulty of the Rashbetz regarding the first reason: why do we say it specifically at the fourth cup and not the others?).

**And we open the door.** The custom of the Rebbe’s house: (When it falls on a weekday, they take a candelabra of lit candles and) go to open all the doors between the room where the Seder is being held and the public domain (or the courtyard), and they say "Shefoch" — the shluchim say it by the door. It happened that the Rebbe himself, accompanied by one of the members of his household, went to open the door, and then he too said "Shefoch Chamascha, etc." by the door — upon the return of the shluchim, they begin "Lo Lanu."

"Shefoch Chamascha" is recited while seated; there is no need to stand.

**And we open the door.** In order to remember that it is a "night of watching," and we do not fear anything, and in the merit of this faith, Mashiach will come and Hashem will pour out His wrath upon the nations (Shulchan Aruch of the Rebbe, ibid).

**We open the door** so that Eliyahu may enter, etc., because "they have devoured Yaakov, etc.," and Yaakov took a pledge from Eliyahu, the sign of the letter *vav*, that he will come and herald the redemption of his children (Sefer Maharid).

**Shefoch, etc., Ki Achlu, etc.** (Tehillim 79:6-7). — **Shfoch, etc.** (ibid. 69:25) — **Tirdof, etc.** (Eichah 3:66) — This is the version in the Siddur of the Arizal, the Shelah, and the Yaavetz (in section 273 and the Siddur; there are only the two verses "Shefoch, etc." and "Ki Achlu, etc." In the Machzor Vitry, he adds several verses to our version).

**Shefoch, etc.** — and even though we are not worthy — **Lo Lanu Hashem Lo Lanu Ki Im Lishimcha, etc.** (Siddur).

## Hallel Nirtzah

**Nirtzah.** The meaning of *Nirtzah* is: if one has performed the Seder in this order, he is accepted (*nirtzah*) by Heaven (Shelah). It is noteworthy that the sign *Nirtzah* should logically be placed at the end of the Seder, not here. The Rashar and Shelah both placed it at the end.

**Hallel.** Our Rebbe the Alter Rebbe explains: *Hallel* is a term for praise, and also a term related to "in His light" (*beheilo*), as in "in His light I walked" (Torah Or 30a). Every time the Sages ordained the recitation of Hallel, it is a time of the revelation of the truth of Hashem from above to below—which is that what is below is called "nothing" (*ayin*), and what is above is called the category of "something" (*yesh*) (Likutei Torah, *Drush* "B'yom HaShemini Atzeres" - the third).

**"Not to us," etc., "I love," etc., "Praise," etc., "Give thanks," etc.** (Tehillim 115–118) — Mishnah, Pesachim 117b: "The fourth [cup], he completes the Hallel over it."

**"Give thanks to Hashem," etc.** The leader (or the *shaliach tzibbur* on other days of the year) reads aloud "Give thanks," etc., and all those gathered respond after him: "Give thanks," etc., "Let Israel say," etc. The leader then reads again: "Let the house of Aaron say," etc., and they respond after him: "Give thanks," etc., "Let those who fear Hashem say," etc. This continues for "Let them say," and "Ana Hashem" (four times). After the verses of "Let them say," etc., they recite: "Give thanks," etc., as is customary.

**"All Your works shall praise You," etc., "Give thanks to Hashem," etc., "The soul of all living," etc., "And thus," "May Your name be blessed," etc., "You are the Living One of the worlds."** Mishnah and Gemara, ibid. The various opinions regarding the interpretation of the Gemara and the text of the Haggadah are collected in the *Pri Megadim*, *Eshel Avraham*, beginning of section 480. Our Rebbe’s version is as stated by the Rosh and the Tur (based on the commentary of the Taz, end of section 486), and so wrote the *Levush* in section 480, the *Chok Yaakov* and the *Beis HaLevi* in section 480, the *Yaavetz*, and the *Chayei Adam*.

**"All Your works shall praise You" (*Yehalelucha*).** Mentioned in the Gemara, ibid.

**"Hashem our G-d, all Your works."** Not: "**upon** all Your works" (as per the correction of the Tzemach Tzedek on his Siddur). This is also the version in section 273, 269, Rambam, Laws of Megillah and Chanukah, chapter 3, law 10, the Abudraham, the *Yaavetz*, and others. This follows the verse (Tehillim 145:10): "All Your works shall thank You, Hashem" (Abudraham).

**"For His kindness is eternal" (*Ki le'olam chasdo*).** One must pause between the words (*le'olam* and *chasdo*) (Yerushalmi, Berachos, chapter 2, law 4) so that it is not heard as "le'olam" (as one word), and also to separate the *samech* of *chasdo* (Rashi in the Rif, ibid.).

**"Give thanks to Hashem," etc.** (Tehillim 136) — This is called the *Hallel HaGadol*.
— (The standard Hallel is called the *Hallel HaMitzri* — Berachos 56a.)
— Why? Because it is written therein, "Who gives food to all flesh," and this is a great matter (Pesachim 118a; see Berachos 4b). It contains 26 verses of thanksgiving to the Holy One, blessed be He, corresponding to the numerical value of the Ineffable Name, etc. It is possible that this is the reason it is called in the words of our Sages the *Hallel HaGadol*, based on "For Hashem is great and exceedingly praised." Our Sages provided another reason, as we find in the chapter *Arvei Pesachim*, etc. (Abudraham). See also regarding this in section 335 (Shacharis for Shabbos). It concludes there that it is found in the words of the Kabbalists that this is the *Hallel HaGadol* of the ministering angels.

From "Give thanks" until "And He brought Israel out," one should concentrate on the *Yud* of the Name *Havayah*, and from the verse "And He brought Israel out" until "Who leads," on the *Hei*, etc. (Pri Etz Chaim, Gate of Shabbos, chapter 19). It appears this meditation is intended for everyone, as our Rebbe included the letters *Yud*, *Hei*, etc., in his Siddur opposite these verses (in the Shabbos prayers), even though he generally did not include meditations in his Siddur, as this is something that is equal for every soul.

**"The soul of all living" (*Nishmas kol chai*)** (mentioned in Pesachim, ibid.). Some say that Shimon ben Shetach composed it and hinted at his name in reverse: *Shochen ad* (Dwelling forever), *Mi yidmeh* (Who can be compared), *Ad henah* (Until here), *Ve'ilu pinu* (And if our mouths), *Nishmas* (The soul) (see the commentary of the *Kol Bo*). See also Zohar, Vol. II (138a) and the Abudraham.

**Those who are accustomed to recite *piyutim*.** In the house of the Rebbe, it is not the custom to recite them.

**Those who are accustomed to recite *piyutim* should not interrupt with them between [the Hallel and the blessing].** Because they are merely a custom (Rema, Bach, and Taz, section 480; the Arizal, *Yaavetz*). The ordinance of the Sages was to complete the Hallel and the *Birchas HaShir* over the fourth cup (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, section 480, paragraph 3).

**"But immediately thereafter he shall recite the blessing."** Seemingly, this is redundant. One can say that it emphasizes the reason: why one should not interrupt, because one must recite the blessing **immediately**, as that was the ordinance of the Sages to complete, etc., as mentioned above, and the *piyutim* are not part of the ordinance.

**"And thereafter he shall say 'Next year'..."** Only once (the Arizal, *Yaavetz*—not as written in section 335, which says three times).

The custom of the Rebbe’s house: After saying "Next year in Jerusalem," the Rebbe would return and pour the wine from the cup of Elijah back into the bottle, and all those gathered would sing the *nigun* at that time for the verse: "You are my G-d and I will thank You"—one of the ten *nigunim* of the Alter Rebbe.

The Rebbe—the Alter Rebbe—did not include the words "Chasal Siddur Pesach" in the Siddur after the Haggadah, because in Chabad, Pesach does not end; rather, it is drawn out continuously.

In truth, the illumination of all the festivals shines every day, but the festival of Pesach is drawn out continuously (Sichos of the festival of Pesach, 5703, from the Rebbe Rayatz).
