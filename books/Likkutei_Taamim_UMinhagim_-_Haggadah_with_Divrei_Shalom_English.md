# Likkutei Taamim UMinhagim – Haggadah with Divrei Shalom

## Introduction

B"H

With praise and thanks to Hashem, this Haggadah of Pesach, with *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim* by the Rebbe, is hereby published.

Added to this is the *Divrei Shalom* commentary, to assist the reader in understanding the concepts discussed in the aforementioned work.

Over the years, many notes and explanations have been written on sections of *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim* in various books and compilations.

In the commentary before you, I have endeavored to explain the matters in an easy and accessible manner. Where others have discussed these topics, their works are referenced.

In addition to all this, I have been assisted by the book *Shaarei Shalom* by Rabbi Shalom Shpalter, which contains notes and explanations on *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim*.

*

The work *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim* was first printed in the Haggadah of Pesach, Kehot, 5706.

In the second edition (Kehot, 5715), 12 more sections were added to the end.

In the third edition (Kehot, 5717), 16 more sections were added to the end, bringing the total to 28 sections.

In the fourth edition (Kehot, 5723), 13 more sections were added to the end, bringing the total to 41 sections (some of which were printed at the beginning, after the title page).

In the seventh edition (Kehot, 5733), *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim* was placed in the margins of the Haggadah, with each section appearing in the margin of the page to which it pertains. Several sections were also added in this edition, as has been the case in all subsequent editions.

In the Kehot 5746 edition, 2 more sections were added, printed in the margin as an "addition after the fact." Supplements based on *Igros Kodesh* and other sources were also included.

In the Kehot 5747 edition, a "deciphering of the references and citations" was added to the end, providing quotes for some of the sections referenced in *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim*.

*

It appears that the Rebbe exerted himself in this work to ensure it would be "a small amount containing much," both in style and in the format of the printing. Therefore:

(a) It is sometimes written with extreme brevity, requiring brief explanation.

(b) Citations to books are generally given in acronyms, without specifying the location within the book.

(c) Even when the same interpretation or halacha is brought in several books, he is sometimes content to cite only one of them.

(d) When there are several topics in the same paragraph, he often combined them into the same section, separating them with a period or a period and a dash (". –"). This dash indicates that it is essentially a new section; however, to avoid leaving blank space on the page, the two topics were placed in the same section (this resolves many sections containing two topics that, at first glance, do not appear to be related).

In the edition before you, we have left the text of *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim* as it was printed in previous editions, without change, except for expanding the acronyms to assist the reader.

In the margins, we have provided: (a) A brief explanation to assist the reader. (b) A breakdown of the citations and quotes of the passages mentioned in those books. (c) References and quotes from additional works where the same halacha or interpretation is explained.

*

We do not possess the original handwritten manuscripts from which this work was printed.

However, in the winter of 5777, two pages of a handwritten list of references were published (*Tachyeinu*, issue 7, p. 58). These were notes the Rebbe wrote for himself regarding what was found in various books on the Haggadah, in order to check the original source to see if it contained a *chiddush*, and to copy it into his work *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim*. He wrote on it: "**This is to be clarified** in its place."

Only a small portion of the content of these notes was included in *Likutei Taamim UMinhagim*.

For the sake of completeness, this list is reproduced as an addition, with notes and references in the margins.

Several lines had a line drawn through them for deletion and were not copied here.

Haggadah of Pesach
Laws, Customs, Sources, and Reasons

## Laws of Pesach

**Laws of Pesach.** These were the **first** of the Alter Rebbe’s Shulchan Aruch to be printed (by his sons, Shklov, 5573 [1](#_ftn1)). They are also the first (together with Hilchos Tzitzis) to have been written and completed by the Alter Rebbe while he was still "sitting in the seat of wisdom" in Mezritch (Introduction of the Mitteler Rebbe to the Shulchan Aruch [2](#_ftn2), Introduction of the Rabbis, sons of the illustrious author, of blessed memory [3](#_ftn3)). Hilchos Talmud Torah [4](#_ftn4) (and Seder Birchas HaNehenin [5](#_ftn5)) were printed by the Alter Rebbe, but as independent works, rather than as part of the Shulchan Aruch.

## Shabbos HaGadol

It is called this because a great miracle occurred on that day (Tosafos, s.v. *Ve’oto Shabbos*, Pesachim 68b [1](#_ftn6); Tur Shulchan Aruch, end of Siman 430 [2](#_ftn7)). In an old manuscript Machzor, it is found that the Shabbos before Shavuos is also called Shabbos HaGadol, as is the Shabbos before Rosh Hashanah (Kesser Shem Tov, Vol. 3 [3](#_ftn8)). It may be said that these Shabbosos were given this name (even though the aforementioned reason does not apply to them) because of their similarity to the Shabbos before Pesach, as they too occur before a Yom Tov.

When Shabbos HaGadol falls on Erev Pesach—and only then—we read the Haftorah of *Ve’arva* (Be’er Heitev, 430:1), because in that passage it is written, "Bring the tithe into the storehouse," and Erev Pesach (of the fourth and seventh years of the Shmittah cycle) is the time for the removal of tithes (Shulchan Aruch HaRav, end of Siman 430; see there [4](#_ftn9)).

On Shabbos HaGadol, after Mincha, we recite *Avadim Hayinu* until *l’chaper al kol avonoseinu*, because that was when the beginning of the Geulah and the miracles took place (Shulchan Aruch, end of Siman 430 [5](#_ftn10)). This is the custom (contrary to what is written in Shaar HaKollel [6](#_ftn11)).

## Bedikas and Biur Chametz

**The custom to place pieces of chametz, etc., before the bedikah.** According to the strict letter of the law, there is no need to place them at all, for the mitzvah is to search for chametz, and even if one finds none, one has fulfilled the mitzvah as required (Sefer Shibbolei HaLeket, siman 206, in the name of the Responsa of the Geonim [1]; Kol Bo in the name of Rav Hai Gaon [2]). However, the custom to place them has already spread, and "the custom of Israel is Torah" (Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, end of siman 432 [3]). See the Chok Yaakov [4] for the reasons for this custom. One may add another reason: so that during the bitul of the day, one will be able to say "all chamira and chamia **d'chamitei**" [5]. Also, so that one does not forget the obligation of biur, and so that one will not need to burn the vessel used for the search, as written at the end of siman 445 [6].

**Hard chametz.** So that it does not crumble (Shulchan Aruch, siman 432 [7]).

**According to Kabbalah, one should place ten pieces.** (Mishnas Chassidim [8], Siddur HaAriZal [9]). In the sefer Bris HaLevi [10] by R. Shlomo Alkabetz, he writes that this is alluded to in the Gemara (Pesachim 10a): "He placed ten, etc." (Shaar HaKollel [11]).

**The custom of the Rebbe’s household.** Ten pieces of hard chametz are placed in various places in the house, each wrapped in a piece of paper. In Kesser Shem Tov, Vol. 3 [12], it is written that this is also the custom of the Sephardim in the Holy Land, Syria, Turkey, and Egypt. The reason is so that it does not crumble, as mentioned in Shulchan Aruch, siman 432.

One searches by the light of a wax candle (Shulchan Aruch, siman 433 [13]) and using a bird feather. The searcher places the chametz he finds into a small paper bag. At the conclusion of the search, he places this bag, the feather, and the remains of the candle—if any remain—into the hollow of a wooden spoon [14], wraps everything in paper (but not the handle of the spoon, which remains unwrapped), and fastens it with a string that he winds around the paper several times and ties [15].

**"Baruch... al biur chametz"** (Pesachim 7b [16]). Even though at this moment he is not yet burning it, nevertheless, since the search is not the ultimate purpose of the mitzvah, and immediately after the search he nullifies and declares ownerless all the chametz that remained and was not found, this bitul is called "biur." Furthermore, the biur that takes place on the fourteenth day is also covered by this blessing (Tur [17] and Beis Yosef [18]; Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, siman 432 [19]).

**"Al biur."** And not "leva'er." See Rambam, Hilchos Berachos, ch. 11, halacha 15 [20], and the commentaries on the Shas there.

**"And one must search by the light of a candle"** because the light of a candle is superior for searching (Pesachim 8a [21]).

**One should be careful to search first in the room adjacent to the place where the blessing was heard.** Because walking from house to house is considered an interruption between the blessing and the beginning of the performance of the mitzvah (Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, siman 432, sec. 8 [22]).

**One must perform bitul after the search.** Bitul at this time is an enactment of the Sages of the Gemara [23], so that one has a fixed time... and in later generations, they added that it should also be nullified during the day (ibid., siman 434, sec. 13 [24]).

**"Kol chamira."** In the Yerushalmi (Pesachim, ch. 2, halacha 2 [25]), the text of the bitul is in the Holy Tongue, but the Geonim enacted it in Aramaic for the sake of the unlearned (Darkei Moshe [26]; Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, siman 434, sec. 8 [27]). It is found—with variations—in Rashi (Pesachim 6b [28]), Rif (ibid. 3a [29]), Rosh (ibid., ch. 1, sec. 9 [30]), Bahag [31], Machzor Vitry [32], Yotzer for Shabbos HaGadol [33], and others. The text of our Rebbe is precise according to what the Bach wrote in siman 434 [35]—cited in the Chok Yaakov [36]—and it is likewise in the Siddur Shaar HaShamayim of the Shelah [37]. Now, in the Bach and Shaar HaShamayim, they wrote: "kol chamira v'**kol** chamia," but our Rebbe copied it as "kol chamira **v'chamia**," and this is also how it appears in the Chok Yaakov, the Barei Heitev [38] (ibid.), the Siddur HaAriZal [39], the Siddur Yaavetz [40], and others. Seemingly, according to our Sages (Moed Katan 26a), "He said to him: 'Wait until they are all [finished]'; he said to him: 'Upon [the word] "al" the matter was concluded,'" so too here, since even if only one of the two exists—chametz or leaven—one must nullify, one should say: "kol chamira **v'kol** chamia." It may be said that the word "kol" itself indicates that in whatever manner it may be, one nullifies [41]. See in Siddur Kol Yaakov [42] for the allusions of the word **v'chamia** with a vav.

**"V'chamia."** Although "chamira" includes chametz as well, it is appropriate for anyone who is meticulous in his deeds to mention it explicitly (Bach [43], Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, siman 434, sec. 9).

**"D'la chamitei."** This is how it appears in the Siddur Shelah, Siddur Kol Yaakov, and others. And not like the Bach, Chok Yaakov, and Siddur of R. Sh. Rashkov [44], who wrote **"d'la chazeitei"** (and the Barei Heitev was not precise in copying the language of the Bach). See in Siddur Kol Yaakov for the reason based on sod [45].

**"V'dla yada'na lei."** Because the chametz that one sets aside for tomorrow is not nullified [46] (Bach, siman 434).

**"K'afra d'ara"** [47]. This excludes gold, which is also called "dust," as it is said (Iyov 28:6), "And it has dust of gold" (Chok Yaakov, Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, siman 434, sec. 7).

**Bedikas Chametz.** Admur HaZaken went to Mezritch for the first time in the year 5524 [48] and stayed there (on that occasion) until before the festival of Pesach 5525. When he returned home—on the 13th of Nissan of that year—he did not eat (he did not fast, for fasting is forbidden [49], rather he simply did not eat) because he was occupied with the preparation for bedikas chametz, so that all the kavanos he received in Mezritch regarding bedikas chametz would be implemented in the actual search. The search lasted all night, and he only had one room at that time (Sichos of the Rebbe Rayatz, Pesach 5698 [50]).

At that time, our Rebbe explained the Mishnah (beginning of Pesachim): "On the night of the fourteenth, one searches for chametz by the light of a candle"—13 is gematria "echad" (one), which is the matter of Daas, in which searching is not applicable (or: is it necessary?). 14 is the matter of the Middos (the seven middos of the Nefesh HaElokis and the Nefesh HaBahamis); there, one must search for chametz, and the search is by the light of the candle, "The candle of Hashem is the soul of man" [51]. The search is in holes and crevices, which is why it must be by the light of a candle, for a torch is the level of Atzilus, and there "no evil shall dwell with You" [52]; a torch is in Beriah, and the light of a candle is in Yetzirah, for Asiyah is the level of darkness (ibid. See also Shelah, Maseches Shabbos, 136b [53]. Koheles Yaakov, entry "Avukah" [54]. Maamar "Or L'14" 5700 [55]).

**On the fourteenth day at the fifth hour.** See Shulchan Aruch, siman 449, for how these hours are calculated [56].

**"A separate torch"** (Tur, siman 445 [57]. Maharshal [58], Maharil [59], Magen Avraham [60]), for we decree lest he burn it after the time when it is forbidden to derive benefit from it (Beis Yosef [61]).

**"D'chazeitei... d'chamitei."** It is possible to say: **"Chamitei"** at the time of the search. **"Chazeitei"**—ever [62] (Bach, siman 434).

**And one should burn ten pieces.** He repeated again regarding the ten pieces, because both in the search and in the burning, there must be ten pieces (see Pri Etz Chaim, Shaar Chag HaMatzos, end of ch. 4 [63] and Mishnas Chassidim there [64]).

**"Yehi Ratzon."** In the Siddur HaAriZal [65]. And there it is "D'Elokai v'Elokei Avosai" in the singular. Perhaps the reason he changed it to the plural "Elokeinu v'Elokei Avoseinu" is so that the prayer will be heard (Berachos 30a [66]. Shulchan Aruch Admur HaZaken, Orach Chaim, sec. 10, halacha 4 [67]). Especially since the rest of this prayer is also in the plural [68]. See Magen Avraham and Acharonim, Orach Chaim, beginning of siman 46 [69]. It requires study, for in the prayer "Yehi Ratzon... me'azei panim," and at the end of the order of Kiddush Levanah, our Rebbe’s text is also in the singular [70], and so it is in the Siddur Shelah (and in the Siddur of R. Shabsi Rashkover: "Yehi Ratzon... me'azei panim" is in the plural).

And what is written in Shaar HaKollel [71] regarding "Yehi Ratzon... me'azei panim," that the reason is because it is not in a tzibbur—this requires study, for the "Yehi Ratzon" of biur chametz is also not in a tzibbur.

**"B'eshan tichleh."** And so it is in the prayers of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur—with a beis, and like the language of the verse (Tehillim 37:20), "They are consumed; **in smoke** they are consumed."

**"B'ruach bi'er u'v'ruach mishpat."** The language of the verse (Yeshayahu 4:4) is "b'ruach mishpat u'v'ruach bi'er." That they changed it and placed "bi'er" before "mishpat" is understood: regarding the washing and refining of the iniquities of the daughters of Tzion in the verse, He will wash them with a "spirit of judgment," which is mercy, as Rashi explains regarding suffering, that this is like a father disciplining his son (see in this regard Igeres HaKodesh by our Rebbe HaZaken, Epistle 22). And that which remains without refinement, He will consume from the world with a "spirit of destruction" (bi'er). But here, regarding the chitzonim and kelipos that oppress the Shechinah, He will consume with a "spirit of destruction," and all the sparks of holiness that the chitzonim draw from (see Pri Etz Chaim, Shaar Chag HaMatzos, ch. 1) He will wash with a "spirit of judgment" to refine them (Shaar HaKollel [72]).

**"Bayamim haheim u'vazman hazeh"** [73]. And so it is in the Siddur of R. Sh. Rashkover. It requires study, for in all prayers the text is "bazman," and "u'vazman" with an additional vav is not found elsewhere [74]. In the Siddur Kol Yaakov [75] here, it is correctly "bazman" without a vav.

**Biur Chametz.** One who eats chametz on Pesach is as one who worships idols (Zohar, Vol. 3, 182a). The AriZal wrote that one who is careful even regarding a minute amount of chametz on Pesach is guaranteed that he will not sin the entire year (Barei Heitev, beginning of siman 447 [76]). Perhaps the intention is that no evil will befall him inadvertently, for he is certainly a master of his own choice even then.

## The Order of the Korban Pesach

**"And we will render for bullocks..."** (with a slight variation in wording: see *Shelah*, Maseches Pesachim at the beginning; *Siddur HaAriZal*[1](#_ftn88)).

**"Korban Pesach, etc., this is the matter, etc."** (the text of *Seder HaYom* is copied there[2](#_ftn89)). The *Yaavetz* raised several difficulties regarding this order and wrote that it should be recited in a different version. In order to resolve why our Rebbe brought the language of *Seder HaYom* in all of this, one may answer the *Yaavetz’s* difficulties as follows:

1] That it did not specifically mention the time of the offering and its precedence when Erev Pesach falls on Shabbos—one may say that since the recitation of the *Seder Korban Pesach* is for the sake of "And we will render for bullocks the words of our lips," one is not obligated to precisely time its recitation to the hour when the *Korban Pesach* was actually offered. It is sufficient that it be at a time when the offering of the *Korban Pesach* is valid. Just as is the case regarding the *Mincha* prayer, they did not wish to mention in this text that the *Pesach* was offered at eight-and-a-half hours, etc., because in most cases one would not be able to time its recitation to that moment, and it would be self-contradictory.

2] It did not specify upon whom the obligation falls—one may say that they did not wish to specify that it applies only to those who are ritually pure and able to reach Jerusalem at the time of the *Pesach* slaughter, for if so, it would contradict the concept of "And we will render," as mentioned above.

3] It did not specify the difference in skinning it on Shabbos—one may say that even if you were to say that its skinning on Shabbos is not the same as its skinning on a weekday (see the commentaries on Rambam, *Hilchos Korban Pesach* 1:14), the difference is only in the **manner** of skinning, and it does not descend into such details.

4] It wrote simply that the fat tail (*alyah*) is offered, without stipulating that this is regarding a sheep, for the fat tail of a goat is not offered—one may say that this is not common, as most of their *Pesachim* were from sheep, similar to the first answer to a **similar question** in *Tosafos* (s.v. *Me'alyah*, *Pesachim* 63b[3](#_ftn90)). And it still requires further study.

5] The details of roasting the *Pesach*, removing its sinews, and eating it were omitted—one may say this is because none of this pertains to the offering of the *Korban Pesach*, nor does it occur at the time of the recitation of the *Seder Korban Pesach*, and therefore it was only mentioned in general terms.

6] Nothing was mentioned regarding the *Chagigah* of the fourteenth—one may say, as the *Yaavetz* himself hinted, that it was omitted because the *Chagigah* is only optional, and it does not come on Shabbos in a large group or in a state of impurity (*Pesachim* 69b[4](#_ftn91)).

**"And he receives the full vessel first, and afterwards returns the empty one"**—because one does not pass over *mitzvos* (*Pesachim* 64b[5](#_ftn92)).

**"And they give"**[6](#_ftn93)—apparently this is a typographical error and should be "and they place them" (*v'nosnam*), and this is how it appears in several printings.

**"They recite the Hallel"**—the Levites (*Tosefta Pesachim*, end of chapter 4[7](#_ftn94)).

**"They blow"**—the Kohanim with trumpets (*Bamidbar* 10:8-10[8](#_ftn95); *Tamid* ch. 7, mishna 3[9](#_ftn96)).

## Kiddush

**Atkinu Seudata.** (Siddur of R. Shabtai Rashkover[1](#_ftn185). See Zohar Chadash, Bereishis 8a[2](#_ftn186) and there).

**Kadesh.** (Mishnah, Pesachim 114a[3](#_ftn187)). We have the custom to recite Kiddush standing, whether on Shabbos and Yom Tov or on Pesach (this is stated in Pri Etz Chaim, Shaar 18, Ch. 14[4](#_ftn188) and Shaar 21, Ch. 6[5](#_ftn189), Siddur HaArizal[6](#_ftn190), and Mishnas Chassidim[7](#_ftn191). See Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim, Siman 271, Se'if 10[8](#_ftn192) and commentaries).

**Kadesh.** For all the words of the *Kadesh Urchatz* mnemonic, the Alter Rebbe included in his Siddur, in each respective place, the explanation of the word—such as **Urchatz**: "He washes his hands and does not recite a blessing," and the like—except for the words **Kadesh**, **Hallel**, and **Nirtzah**. It can be said that the reason is that there is no novelty in the explanation of these words[9](#_ftn193).

Although in his Shulchan Aruch (beginning of Siman 473) he copied what is written in the Rema (ibid.) and wrote, "If possible, it is good that the householder should not pour the wine himself, but rather another should pour for him, so that he may have it like a servant—meaning, in a way of freedom and nobility, in memory of the Exodus from Egypt," it can be said that since this is only **"if possible,"** he did not copy it into his Siddur. (Regarding the explanation in *Shaar HaKollel* that the law of another pouring for him applies specifically to strong wines, where there is great exertion involved because one must bring water for the mixing[10](#_ftn194), whereas with our wines each person pours for himself—this requires study. For it is obvious that even with our wines, if another pours for him, it is a matter of wealth, and if so, it is a way of freedom and nobility in memory of the Exodus from Egypt; where do we have the authority to make such a distinction? Furthermore, if so, why is it that the commentaries on the Shulchan Aruch and the Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe did not mention that with our wines each person pours for himself, as they distinguished the law of our wines in Siman 204[11](#_ftn195), 272[12](#_ftn196), and 632[13](#_ftn197)?)

The custom of the Rebbe’s house: We pour the cup[14](#_ftn198), and we are not particular that another should pour it[15](#_ftn199). One takes it in his right hand, transfers it to his left[16](#_ftn200), and sets it down—in a downward motion from top to bottom—onto the palm of his right hand, which is slightly cupped like a receptacle, with the four fingers raised and the thumb laid along the side[17](#_ftn201). He recites Kiddush standing[18](#_ftn202), and the cup is raised more than three *tefachim* above the table[19](#_ftn203)—and all of this applies also during the rest of the year[20](#_ftn204).

**Kadesh.** On every Yom Tov, one is obligated in Kiddush by Rabbinic decree, and when it falls on Shabbos, it also has a basis from the Torah[21](#_ftn205). On this night, one adds to them, as it is one of the four cups which are a mitzvah by Rabbinic decree on the night of Pesach. Therefore: 1) One does not recite Kiddush over bread[22](#_ftn206). 2) No one else can fulfill his obligation for him (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, Siman 472, Se'if 21[23](#_ftn207)). 3) One must exert himself to drink the four cups, even if it harms him or he must sell his garment (Nedarim 49b[24](#_ftn208); Pesachim 99b[25](#_ftn209)). 4) One recites Kiddush only at night itself and not during the *Tosefet Yom Tov* (Terumat HaDeshen, Siman 37[26](#_ftn210)), because the Sages instituted the four cups for the time appropriate for eating matzah (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, ibid., Se'if 2[27](#_ftn211)). 5) One should be concerned for the opinion of those who say that it is a mitzvah to drink the entire cup, or at least the majority of it (ibid., Se'if 19[28](#_ftn212)).

**Four Cups**—a mitzvah by Rabbinic decree[29](#_ftn213). Women are also obligated in them, for they too were part of that miracle[30](#_ftn214) of the Exodus from Egypt (Tosafot, s.v. *Hayu*, Pesachim 108b[31](#_ftn215)). We do not recite over them the blessing "Blessed... who has commanded us to drink four cups," because they are interrupted [by other activities] (Kol Bo[32](#_ftn216), Rokeach[33](#_ftn217)).

They were instituted corresponding to the four expressions of redemption: "I will bring out," "I will deliver," "I will redeem," and "I will take"[34](#_ftn218) (Shemos 6:6-7)—which correspond to the four decrees that Pharaoh decreed upon them (Shemos Rabbah, end of Ch. 6[35](#_ftn219)). They correspond to the breaking of the four *kelipos* (Tola'at Yaakov[36](#_ftn220)); to the four cups of Pharaoh (Bereishis 40:11-13), in which the redemption of Israel was hinted at in that dream, as our Sages taught in Bereishis Rabbah, Ch. 88[37](#_ftn221); to the four kingdoms; to the four cups of retribution that Hashem gives the nations of the world to drink[38](#_ftn222), and corresponding to them, He gives Israel four cups of consolation to drink (Yerushalmi, Pesachim 10:1[39](#_ftn223)); and to the four letters of the name *Havayah* (Siddur Shelah[40](#_ftn224). See there from Zohar, Vol. 3, 95b[41](#_ftn225)).

**When Yom Tov falls on Shabbos,** we say "Yom HaShishi" first (Pesachim 106a and Tosafot, s.v. *Zochrehu*[42](#_ftn226)). We begin with "Yom HaShishi" (and not from "Vayechulu"[43](#_ftn227)), because the initial letters of *Yom HaShishi Vayechulu HaShamayim* spell the name *Havayah*. When beginning, one should fix his eyes on the candles, and at the time of the Kiddush blessing, on the cup (Rema, Siman 271, Se'if 10[44](#_ftn228)). One should not begin from the earlier text, "And G-d saw, etc.," because our Sages taught: "'Very good'—this is death" (Shut Chasam Sofer, Orach Chaim, Siman 10[45](#_ftn229)).

**Savrei Maranan.** This is how it must be said, and no more (Siddur Yaavetz[46](#_ftn230)), because they complete the number of words in the Kiddush[47](#_ftn231). The reason for saying *Savrei Maranan*—Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, end of Siman 174[48](#_ftn232). The Abudraham brings other reasons as well[49](#_ftn233).

**Savrei Maranan** is in *Tikkunei Zohar*, Tikkun 24, and one must say *Savrei Maranan*. It is similar in Tanchuma, beginning of Parshas Pekudei[50](#_ftn234), in *Likutei Shut HaGeonim* at the end of *Kol Bo*[51](#_ftn235), and in the Tur, Siman 174, in the name of his father[52](#_ftn236). (In Tosafot, s.v. *Ho'il*—Berachos 43a—and elsewhere: *Savrei Morai*. In the Rosh, Berachos ibid.[53](#_ftn237)—*Savrei Rabosai*). See Pri Etz Chaim, Shaar 18, Ch. 16[54](#_ftn238) for an explanation of this expression specifically according to the secret [Kabbalistic] path.

**Yom Chag HaMatzos.** This is how it is called throughout the Torah, and we call it Pesach—because the Ribono shel Olam recounts the praise of Israel, that they did not delay and did not say, "How shall we go out into the desert?" And we recount the praise of the Ribono shel Olam, who "passed over" [pesach], etc. (Kedushat Levi, Parshas Bo[55](#_ftn239)). Some say that they established the name *Chag HaPesach* in memory of the Temple (Ateret Zekenim[56](#_ftn240)).

**Ve'es Yom Tov Mikra Kodesh HaZeh.** This is found in Masechet Soferim, Ch. 19[57](#_ftn241), in the Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon[58](#_ftn242), in the *Seder HaTefillah* of the Rambam[59](#_ftn243) and at the end of *Hilchos Shabbos*[60](#_ftn244), the Abudraham[61](#_ftn245), the Siddur of R. Shabtai Rashkover[62](#_ftn246), and others. See Shulchan Aruch, beginning of Siman 487[63](#_ftn247) and commentaries.

On Shabbos: **Be'ahavah Mikra Kodesh.** Because the *Mikra Kodesh* of Shabbos, Israel accepted with love, at Marah; but that of Yom Tov was by way of Him forcing the mountain over them like a cask at Sinai (Levush, Siman 487[64](#_ftn248), Matteh Moshe[65](#_ftn249), Eliyahu Rabbah[66](#_ftn250)).

**Umo'adei.** Even on a weekday, we say *Umo'adei* with a *vav*, just as we say *VeShabbos* with a *vav*[67](#_ftn251).

**Umo'adei Kodshecha.** [This is said] near the conclusion, as a transition to the conclusion (Pesachim 104a[68](#_ftn252)).

**Mekadesh Yisrael VeHazmanim** (Pesachim 117b[69](#_ftn253)). We do not say *VeHaMo'adim* as the verse says, because all the *mo'adim* depend on the *zemanim*: Pesach on the season of spring, etc. (Bachya, end of Parshas Bo[70](#_ftn254)).

**Shehecheyanu, etc.** On Motzaei Shabbos, we say *Yaknehaz* (Pesachim 103a[71](#_ftn255)). The text of the *Shehecheyanu* blessing is in Mishnah, Berachos (beginning of Ch. 2[72](#_ftn256)). Three times *Shehecheyanu, Vekiyemanu, VeHigianu*—corresponding to "My soul shall praise Hashem; I will praise Hashem while I live; I will sing to my G-d while I exist" (Rokeach, 371[73](#_ftn257). See also Siddur HaArizal, *Birkat HaNehenin*[74](#_ftn258)).

We do not recite the blessing *She'asah Nissim* [for the miracles], because one is destined to say it in the Haggadah (Machzor Vitry[76](#_ftn260); R. Yehuda Tov Elem in *Yotzer Elokei HaRuchot*[77](#_ftn261); Rokeach[78](#_ftn262))—"to Him who performed for us all these miracles" (Siddur R. Amram Gaon[79](#_ftn263), Shibbolei HaLeket[80](#_ftn264)). Some say it is because we only recite this blessing for a Rabbinic mitzvah (Maharil[81](#_ftn265)).

**LaZeman HaZeh.** The *lamed* has a *chirik*. This is also in the Siddur Shelah[82](#_ftn266), the Maharshal, Matteh Moshe[83](#_ftn267), and Magen Avraham, end of Siman 676[84](#_ftn268).

**Havdalah.** The custom of the Rebbe’s house: In the Havdalah of Motzaei Shabbos that falls on Yom Tov, we do not bring the candles close together and do not unite their flames[85](#_ftn269), and likewise, we do not look at our fingernails[86](#_ftn270). Rather, at the time of the blessing *Borei Me'orei HaEish*, one looks at the candles as they are, each one by itself.

**Me'orei HaEish.** Because there are many lights (reddish and greenish) in the fire (Berachos 51b, 52b[87](#_ftn271). See also *Tikkunei Zohar*, Tikkun 21[88](#_ftn272)).

**HaMavdil, etc.** (Pesachim 104a and Tosafot, s.v. *Ba'i* there[89](#_ftn273)).

**Havdalat VeKidasht.** Two separations: between Kohanim and Leviim, and Leviim and Yisraelim (Tosafot, ibid.[90](#_ftn274)).

**On Pesach, one drinks the cup.** The entire cup (Bach, Siman 472[92](#_ftn276)) and all at once (Magen Avraham, ibid.[93](#_ftn277))—this is the custom of the Rebbe’s house. And likewise for the other three cups.

**Leaning to the left.** Because leaning to the right, on one's face, or on one's back is not considered leaning (Pesachim 108a and as explained by the Rambam, *Hilchos Chametz UMatzah*, Ch. 7, Halacha 5[94](#_ftn278)).

**Haseibah.** "And G-d caused the people to turn [va-yaseiv]..." From here our Sages said[95](#_ftn279): Even the poorest person in Israel should not eat until he leans, for that is what the Ribono shel Olam did for them (Shemos Rabbah, Ch. 20, 18). What is *va-yaseiv*? That He made them recline in the manner of kings[96](#_ftn280) reclining on their couches (Bamidbar Rabbah, Ch. 1, 2).

## Urchatz

**Urchatz**

**He washes his hands without a blessing.** This is because one who is about to eat something that has been dipped in a liquid requires the washing of hands beforehand (Pesachim 115a), yet without a blessing, as we are lenient in cases of doubt regarding blessings (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, siman 158, se'if 3). Furthermore, even one who is not careful about this throughout the year washes his hands [on this night], as it is one of the various changes we make on this night so that the children will ask [questions] (Chok Yaakov, siman 473, se'if katan 28).

## Karpas

**Karpas** **is taken, etc.** — [This is done] in order to pique the curiosity of the children, as this is not done at any other time of the year (Pesachim 114a, b).

**Less than a kezayis** — Because this, too, is sufficient to pique the curiosity of the children, and in this way, according to all opinions, one will not be obligated to recite the blessing of *Borei Nefashos Rabos* afterward (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, siman 473, se'if 15 and 18).

**And he shall dip** — and afterward — **he shall recite the blessing.** This is to ensure the blessing is as proximate to the eating as possible (Chok Yaakov, siman 475, sub-paragraph 8). The reason that during a meal throughout the year one recites *HaMotzi* **and afterward** dips in salt is because one recites the blessing before breaking the bread (so that one recites the blessing over the more significant item).

**In salt water or vinegar** — The Tosafot (s.v. *Metabel*, Pesachim 114a) writes [to use] vinegar or salt water. However, in the Siddur of the Shelah and the Yaavetz, it is written: **in salt water or in vinegar.** But not in charoses, for since charoses is a mitzvah for the second dipping, [the concern is:] after he has filled his stomach with it, shall he then bring it for the mitzvah? (Rosh, ibid.).

**Yechaven (He shall intend)** — Seemingly, this should be spelled with two vavs, not with a vav and a yud. And so it is in many places.

**He shall intend to exempt the maror as well.** Because the recitation of the Haggadah, etc., is not considered an interruption or a diversion of attention (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, siman 474). And he intends to exempt the maror of the *korech* as well.

Regarding Karpas, it is not specified whether one should eat it while reclining or without reclining (as is written regarding maror). It may be said that its law is like the rest of the meal on this night, where one has the option not to recline, though one who reclines is praiseworthy (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, siman 472, se'if 14); it is only specified where the obligation or exemption is absolute. And the custom is to eat it without reclining.

According to the majority of halachic authorities, Karpas does not require reclining (Shibolei HaLeket, Matteh Moshe, Birkei Yosef, and others. This is contrary to the opinion of the Beis David).

After **Karpas**, I have not seen the custom to return the remainder of the Karpas to the Ke'arah. Consequently, from this point forward, there are only five items on the Ke'arah.

## Yachatz

**Yachatz**

**He takes, etc.** One breaks the matzah before the Haggadah (Ran in the name of Rav Hai Gaon), because the Haggadah must be recited over matzah that is fit to fulfill one’s obligation—namely, "bread of poverty"—which is a broken piece (Pesachim 115b and ad loc). It must be placed between the two whole matzos (Berachos 39b). This is because the blessing of Hamotzi, which precedes the blessing of Al Achilas Matzah, is recited over a whole loaf (Tosafos, s.v. *HaKol* ad loc ; and this is the primary ruling—Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, sec. 475, para. 6 and para. 7). Furthermore, because we do not pass over mitzvos, the whole matzah must be on top.

One breaks the matzah while it is inside the cloth, covered.

**The larger piece is set aside for the Afikoman,** as it is an important mitzvah, serving for us in place of the Korban Pesach (Maharil, Bach). It is customary to wrap it in a cloth (see Zohar Vol. III, 158b ; Tur Shulchan Aruch, sec. 473) as a remembrance of "their kneading troughs being bound up in their garments" (see Rokeach, Beis Yosef), and to place it between the pillows (HaManhig) so that one does not come to eat it during the meal (Siddur Yaavetz).

My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe (the Rashab), of blessed memory—(and it is understood that his son, my revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, did the same)—would break the Afikoman into five pieces. Once, it happened that it broke into six pieces, and he set one aside. He explained that the middle matzah corresponds to Yitzchak. There must be a breaking, and into five pieces—the sweetening of the five Gevuros of Yitzchak through the Afikoman, the bestowal of sustenance, the Chesed of Avraham (Sichos 5698).

**Afikoman** (Pesachim 119b): The matzah eaten last as a remembrance of the Korban Pesach is called Afikoman (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, sec. 477, para. 3). The meaning of Afikoman is: "Bring out and bring in" food and delicacies to the table (ibid., para. 2 ; and so it is in Sefer HaPardes by Rashi, Hilchos Pesach). In the Tishbi and the Musaf HeAruch : Afikoman is a Greek term for things eaten after the meal.

Some have the custom that the children "snatch" the Afikoman (and one redeems it from them in exchange for a gift). They say that this is **alluded to** in the words of our Sages: "One snatches matzah on the nights of Pesach for the sake of the children, so that they do not fall asleep" (Pesachim 109a ; Chok Yaakov, sec. 472, sub-para. 2). My revered father-in-law, the Rebbe, related how once the eldest daughter of the Rebbe Maharash "snatched" his Afikoman, and this was in her childhood. Apparently, this was an exceptional case, and it is not the custom to snatch the Afikoman in the Rebbe’s house. It is worth noting the words of our Sages: "After a thief, steal, and you will taste the flavor" (Berachos 5b).

**The smaller piece is placed between the two matzos,** because it is over that one that we recite the Haggadah, as mentioned above.

## Maggid

**And one lifts the Ke'arah** (Rosh, end of Pesachim, Tur Shulchan Aruch. However, what is stated in Pesachim 115b refers to the removal of the Ke'arah, which we do **after** reciting *Ha Lachma Anya*, and not as written in *Shaar HaKollel*). In the house of the Rebbe, they do not lift the Ke'arah, but only uncover a portion of the Matzos.

**Ha** – with a *tzeirei* (Mishnas Chassidim, Siddur HaAriZal ; see there for the reason). This vocalization is found in Scripture (Bereishis 47:23 ; Yechezkel 16:43 ; Daniel 2:43), but there it means **"behold,"** not **"this."**

**Ha Lachma** – This is the version in the Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon, in the *Yotzer* for Shabbos HaGadol by R' Yosef Tov Elem (cited in Tosafos, *Lama*, Pesachim 115b), Machzor Vitry, Shibolei HaLeket (sec. 218), Rambam (end of Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah), Abudraham, and others. And even though this is not the actual bread itself, but rather a representation of the bread, it is the language of Scripture (Shemos 16:32): "So that they may see the bread that I fed you" (Magen Avraham, sec. 473, sub-sec. 24; see there).

They instituted this passage in Aramaic so that even the unlearned would understand, as it was established at a time when everyone spoke Aramaic. However, **"Next year we will be free men"** is in Lashon HaKodesh so that the Babylonians would not understand and consider them rebels against the kingdom (Kol Bo ; see in Shelah an explanation of all this according to the *sod*). One should not raise a difficulty from the rest of the Haggadah, which is in Lashon HaKodesh, and especially from *Ma Nishtana*, which is the children's question and is also in Lashon HaKodesh—because this part of the Haggadah already existed during the time of the Beis HaMikdash, as brought in the Mishnah (Pesachim 116a). It is worth noting that in the Siddur of Rav Saadia Gaon, the entire paragraph is in Aramaic (though the beginning of the paragraph, "Ha Lachma – of Egypt," is not there), and in the Rambam, only the first **"Next year"** is in Lashon HaKodesh, and the rest is Aramaic.

In Machzor Vitry, it adds at the end of the *Ha Lachma* paragraph, and likewise at the end of *Ma Nishtana*, "and translate." In Sefer HaManhig, he mentions this as a custom that had already been accepted. He writes: "And what they were accustomed to translate the first two passages, it is for the sake of recognition and to publicize the miracle." And in Kol Bo : "There are those who translate *Ha Lachma* and *Ma Nishtana* so that the women and children will understand, and there are those who translate even for the servants, and it is a beautiful custom, etc., for what use is understanding the question if they do not know the answer?"

To resolve our custom, where we do not translate except for *Ma Nishtana*—see below.

One can say simply that we are particular that at the very least, the one reciting the passage should understand what is being said by him. And as for *Ma Nishtana*, the children are the ones asking.

**All who are hungry, let them come and eat; all who are in need, let them come and celebrate Pesach.** That is to say, let him come and perform the Seder of Pesach, which is the Mitzvah-Matzah, the four cups, etc., similar to what our Sages said (Taanis 20b): "When R' Huna would break bread, he would open his door and say, 'Whoever is in need, let him come and eat'" (Abudraham).

**Next year, etc. Next year.** My father-in-law, the Rebbe, said: the first [occurrence] is accented on the penultimate syllable (*mil'eil*), the second is accented on the ultimate syllable (*mil'ra*) (Sichos 1931). See Rashi on Bereishis 29:6 ; Zohar I, 265a ; Rambam, Hilchos Teshuvah, end of ch. 8.

**They remove the Ke'arah** (Pesachim 115b) so that the child will be astonished and ask: "We have not yet eaten, and they are already removing the table?" And through this, he will come to ask the other questions (ibid., Tosafos, *Kedei*).

**And they pour him the second cup, and here the son asks** (Mishnah, Pesachim 116a). Here, at the pouring of the second cup, the son asks *Ma Nishtana*, "Why is it different now that we pour the second cup before eating?" (Rashi and Rashbam, ibid.). And through this, he will also ask the other questions. Therefore, we pour the cup before reciting the Haggadah, even though one does not need to hold it in his hand until reaching *Lefichach* (Shulchan Aruch of the Rebbe, sec. 473, sub-sec. 40).

**And they pour him.** The word **"him"** (*lo*) is a printing error; in the first Kapust Siddur, which the Alter Rebbe proofread, it is not there. And likewise, he omitted it in his Shulchan Aruch (*Shaar HaKollel* ; see above, the paragraph *Kadesh*).

**The son asks.** The fact that it is specifically the child who asks arouses above the concept of "For Israel is a youth, and I love him," a verse that speaks of the time of the Exodus from Egypt (Sichos, Pesach 1944).

**The son asks, "Why is it different"** (*Ma Nishtana*). In the old Siddurim, there was also the version: **"The son asks, 'What'"** (Sichos, Pesach 1943, sec. 5).

And in Zohar Chadash, *Tikkunim* at the end (112d): "On the night of watching... when the night of her [the Shechinah's] redemption comes, *Mah* (What) shines for her, which is the explicit Name at that time. The son asks his father, '*Mah* (Why) is this night different,' etc." See there.

Our custom is that the one asking precedes his recitation of *Ma Nishtana* with: "Tatty, I will ask you four questions".

- And even if he has no father, he says this —and continues:

*Ma Nishtana*, etc., *HaLeilos*. "What is different about the night of Pesach from all the nights of the entire year? The first question is, 'On all nights we do not,' etc., 'two times.' All nights of the entire year, etc., two times—one time *karpas* in salt water, the second time *maror* in *charoses*. The second question is, 'On all nights,' etc."

After the recitation of *Ma Nishtana* as above by the youngest among those reclining, the Rebbe, my father-in-law, was accustomed to repeat and say—in a whisper—*Ma Nishtana*, etc. (with the aforementioned introduction) and its translation.

***Ma Nishtana*, etc.** (Mishnah, Pesachim 116a).

***Anu*** (We). The *Aleph* has a *kamatz*.

***Matbilin* (dipping), *Matzah*, *Maror*, *Mesubin* (reclining)**. This order is in the Mishnah in the Yerushalmi, Rif, and Rosh (though the last two questions are missing there; the Hagahos HaBach on the Rif requires study). It is in the Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon (in manuscript, B.M.), in the Siddur of Rav Saadia Gaon, Rambam, Tur, Abudraham (since they precede the explanation of the *Matbilin* question to the explanation of *She'ar Yerakos*), *Zevach Pesach* by Maharit Abarbanel, *Pri Etz Chaim*, Siddur HaAriZal, *Mishnas Chassidim*, and others. And so it is in the first printed Haggadah (Soncino 1485). This corresponds to their order on this night: dipping (at least the first, which is only to astonish the children), Matzah, Maror. The question of *Mesubin* is not included in the above because it was added a long time after the first questions, i.e., when they ceased eating while reclining all year round. Therefore, it is not mentioned in the Mishnah and Gemara (Gra). (His explanation requires study, for in Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah, ch. 8, halachah 2, he brought the question of *Mesubin* and also the question of "all of it roasted," which means they were asking about *Mesubin* even during the time of the Beis HaMikdash). And therefore it also comes at the end of all the questions according to all opinions. See *Pri Etz Chaim* that the aforementioned order corresponds to the order of the worlds from below to above: *Asiyah, Yetzirah, Beriah, Atzilus*.

One should not raise a difficulty that if so, according to the version of the Rambam, why was the question of *Mesubin* not mentioned in the Shas? It is not a difficulty, for **it must be** that according to the Rambam, the question of *Mesubin* was recited (whether in the Mishnah or in the Gemara) —for he would not innovate on his own a version that was recited—something that is not brought **anywhere!**

Furthermore, one can say regarding the above, and by way of introduction—that there are many variations in the manuscripts of the Shas—not only in the order, but also in the number of questions [there are those where the question of *Maror*, or *Tzeli* (even during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed) was omitted].

The reason can be said to be **that according to the law**, it is not essential to recite specifically all the questions, and not even *lechatchilah* (Pesachim 115b: "**You have exempted** them" from reciting).

Therefore, there were variations in custom from the beginning, or there was no fixed custom at all.

Until after a time (during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed —as is evident from the Rambam), they unified the customs and instituted that all five questions be recited everywhere "so that all of Israel would perform one act."

This is similar to the explanation regarding *Tikkun* R' Avahu concerning the blowing of the Shofar (Rav Hai Gaon in *Beis Yosef*, Orach Chaim, sec. 590).

**Two times.** Even though we dip three times —the dipping of the *Maror* and the *Chazeres* that one wraps together are considered as one (Taz, sec. 475).

**This night, *Maror*.** He does not say "all of it *Maror*" because we eat other vegetables during the first dipping (Tosafos, *HaLeilos*, Pesachim, ibid.).

"On all nights we eat roasted, seared, or cooked meat; on this night, all of it is roasted" (Mishnah, ibid.). This is how he would ask during the time the Beis HaMikdash existed.

He does not ask about the obligation to drink four cups—because it is entirely Rabbinic and has nothing from the Torah, unlike reclining, which is an obligation with Matzah, and dipping—for the second dipping is in *Maror* (Maharal).

**And they return the Ke'arah and uncover a portion of the bread,** because the Haggadah must be over the Matzos, the "bread of poverty" over which we answer many things (Pesachim 115b).

**We were slaves** (*Avadim Hayinu*). For he begins with the disgrace. What is the disgrace? Rav said: "Initially, they were idolaters," and Shmuel said: "We were slaves" (Pesachim 116a). And nowadays, we do both (Rif and Rosh; see there the explanation of R' Chananel, Abudraham). There are those who have the version **Rav** (Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon) or **Rava** (HaRav HaMaggid, Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah, ch. 7, halachah 4 ; see *Dikdukei Sofrim* on Pesachim). He says *Avadim Hayinu*, and if so, our custom is correct, for the *halachah* follows Rav in matters of prohibition and follows Rava (and what is written in the book *Imrei Shefer* by the Netziv—the paragraph *Matchilah Ovdei Avodah Zarah* —is puzzling, for it contradicts what is written in the Rif, R' Chananel, Abudraham, and Rosh mentioned above. At the very least, he should have mentioned them). Therefore, the compiler of the Haggadah placed **"We were slaves"** first, even though the matter told in the paragraph "Initially, they were idolaters" occurred before the event of "We were slaves" (Abudraham).

**We were slaves, etc., our G-d, etc., and with an outstretched arm.** Based on the verse (Devarim 6:21), with the addition of the words **"our G-d"** and **"with an outstretched arm,"** which are not in the verse. See in *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Mishnas Chassidim*, and Siddur HaAriZal the explanation—according to the *sod*. In the Siddur of our Rebbe, it is explained according to *Chassidus*. The connection of **"with an outstretched arm"** to **"our G-d"** is also understood according to our Sages that **"Elokim"** indicates the attribute of *Gevurah* (Bereishis Rabbah, end of ch. 12), and **"arm"** is the left hand (Berachos 6a ; Nazir 3b).

**To Pharaoh**—a harsh king—**in Egypt**—from which a slave could not escape (Yalkut Shimoni, remez 269).

**From there**—in the verse it says **"from Egypt."** The reason for the change requires study.

**Behold, we, etc., we were enslaved to Pharaoh.** As is understood, the intention is not to the Pharaoh in the days of Moshe, but that all the kings of Egypt were called by the general name: Pharaoh (Rashi, Tehillim 34:1). And even though in the days of the Mishnah and the arrangement of the Haggadah, this custom had already ceased (as in the difficulty of the Abudraham, and therefore he omitted the word **"to Pharaoh"** in the version of the Haggadah), one can say—to justify our version—that it ceased because at the Exodus from Egypt, Egypt descended from its greatness. Afterward, it had ups and downs and was also conquered by others. But **if the Holy One, blessed be He, had not taken our ancestors out of Egypt,** it would have remained in its strength (note from Yalkut Shimoni, remez 230: "When Israel left Egypt, the kingdom of Egypt ceased, etc. Pharaoh ruled from one end of the world to the other, etc., for the sake of the honor of Israel") and all the customs and manners of royalty would have been preserved. One must necessarily say this to justify what they were saying—even according to the version of the Abudraham—"we were enslaved **in Egypt**," even at a time when the kingdom of Egypt had ceased.

**And even if we were all wise, etc.** And proof of this: **"It happened that Rabbi Eliezer, etc."**.

**We were all understanding, we were all knowledgeable,** and we do not say **"we were all elders"**, and so it is in *Shibolei HaLeket*, cited in *Tanya Rabbasi*. And in *Bris HaLevi* by Maharsh Alkabetz, even though he reconciles both versions, nevertheless, in his Haggadah, he omitted **"we were all elders"** (*Shaar HaKollel*). And likewise, these words are not in *Sefer HaMitzvos* by the Rambam, positive commandment 157 (unlike the Haggadah in his *Chibur*), in *Zevach Pesach* by Maharit Abarbanel, *Pri Etz Chaim*, *Mishnas Chassidim*, Siddur *Kol Yaakov*, Gra.

**It is a mitzvah upon us to tell of the Exodus from Egypt** (Mechilta, end of *Bo* ; Zohar III, 41a —but it is not certain there that it refers specifically to the night of Pesach—Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah, beginning of ch. 2 ; Sefer Mitzvos Gadol, positive commandment 41 ; Sefer HaChinuch, mitzvah 21 ; our Rebbe at the beginning of Hilchos Pesach, and others).

And even women are obligated in this mitzvah (according to the Torah—according to the opinion of the Chinuch and R' Yehudah in Tosafos, *She'af* Megillah 4a. See Tosafos, *Mi* Sukkah 38a —or Rabbinically. See *Birkei Yosef*, sec. 473, sub-sec. 15, and *Shevach Pesach* at length, and so is the implication of the opinion of our Rebbe in his Shulchan Aruch, sec. 472, sub-sec. 25. And also in the book *Beis David*, which thought they were exempt—and so is the opinion of the *Minchas Chinuch* —he retracted in his book *Lechem HaPanim*. See the book *Maggid Devarav*, sec. 42).

## Rachtzah

**Rachtzah.** **And afterwards.** It requires study as to why sometimes the words **"and afterwards"** are added, and sometimes they are not[1](#_ftn764).

## Motzi Matzah

**Motzi Matzah**

*Motzi* is performed before *Matzah*[1](#_ftn765), because when one has a frequent obligation and an infrequent one, the frequent one takes precedence (see *Berachos* 51b[2](#_ftn766)). Furthermore, it is the standard practice to first bless Hashem for bringing forth bread from the earth, and only afterwards to recite the blessing over the *Matzah*, which is the object of the *mitzvah* (*Siddur Rav Amram Gaon*[3](#_ftn767)).

We do not recite *Shehecheyanu* over the *mitzvah* of *Matzah*, as the *Shehecheyanu* recited in the *Kiddush*—"Who has granted us life... to eat *Matzah* and *Maror*"—is sufficient[4](#_ftn768). Alternatively, it was already recited during *Kiddush* (*Abudraham*[5](#_ftn769); *Birkei Yosef*, sec. 473[6](#_ftn770)). The second reason requires further study, for if that were the case, one would have to have in mind during the *Shehecheyanu* of *Kiddush* that it also covers the *Matzah*[7](#_ftn771), similar to the *Shehecheyanu* of *Megillah* reading, where one must have in mind that it also covers the *Mishloach Manos* and the festive meal, as written by the *Shelah*[8](#_ftn772) and the *Magen Avraham*, end of sec. 692[9](#_ftn773).

**Matzah.** Even in the present time, this is a Torah obligation (*Pesachim* 120a[10](#_ftn774)). This applies to both men and women (ibid. 43b[11](#_ftn775)). The Torah introduced a specific requirement for the nights of *Pesach*—even though one is obligated to eat bread on other *Yomim Tovim* as well—that one does not fulfill the obligation with *Matzah Ashirah* (*Ran*, ch. *HaYashan*[12](#_ftn776)). Some say that the obligation to eat bread on *Yom Tov* is only Rabbinic (*Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, sec. 529, para. 6[13](#_ftn777); see *Rambam*, *Hilchos Yom Tov*, ch. 6, law 16[14](#_ftn778)). This is difficult to reconcile with what our Rebbe wrote in sec. 242, para. 1, in brackets[15](#_ftn779). The opinion of *Rabbeinu Yitzchak*, in the *Rosh*, *Berachos* there, sec. 23[16](#_ftn780), implies that it is a Torah obligation—see there in *Maadanei Yom Tov*[17](#_ftn781)—due to the requirement of *simcha* (rejoicing). However, there is no *simcha* on the first night of *Yom Tov* **from the Torah** (*Shaagas Aryeh*, sec. 68, at length[18](#_ftn782)), and if so, even according to him, it would only be Rabbinic[19](#_ftn783) (except for the first night of *Sukkos*[20](#_ftn784))—except for the first night of *Sukkos*, which we derive from the *Matzah* of *Pesach*[21](#_ftn785). (Regarding what is written in *Responsa Rav Akiva Eiger*, sec. 1, that it is only the night of *Pesach*, and there in the *Hashmatos* [omissions] he writes simply that bread is not relevant to *simcha*—this requires further study.) This is not the place to elaborate.

**Matzah.** A *mekubaldiker vort* (a received teaching):

*Matzah Shemurah* is called by two essential and internal names: *Michla d’Meheimenusa* (food of faith) and *Michla d’Asusa* (food of healing); it provides strength in *Da’as*.

This is the internal, essential, and concise teaching that our master, the *Maggid* of Mezeritch, received from his holy master, the *Baal Shem Tov*, in the name of his master, the *Baal Chai*, and transmitted it to his exalted student who received the internal dimension, our *Rebbe HaZaken*, and it has been passed down through *yichud* and *birur* from Rebbe to Rebbe.

Each and every one of our exalted *Nesi’im*, our holy Rebbes, in his generation, repeated, transmitted, and explained this "concise teaching" (*Sicha* of 11 Nissan 5709, in *Sefer HaMaamarim 5709*[24](#_ftn788)).

**Matzah.** *Admur HaZaken* said: *Michla d’Meheimenusa*—the first night. *Michla d’Asusa*—the second night. The *Mitteler Rebbe* explained the distinction: When the healing leads to faith, it means the person was ill and subsequently was healed, and he thanks Hashem for his recovery; the healing that comes *through* faith—this means that from the outset, he does not come to illness at all. He added: This is the case in both the physical and the spiritual, for among *Bnei Yisrael*, the physical is not separate from the spiritual; from the spiritual, it is drawn into the physical, and the physical is transformed into the spiritual (*Sichos* of *Chag HaPesach* 5702[25](#_ftn789)). (See *Zohar Chadash*, vol. 3, 183b[26](#_ftn790); *Zohar Chadash*, vol. 3, 41a[27](#_ftn791); *Likutei Torah*, *Tzav* 13d[28](#_ftn792)).

**The broken piece between the two whole ones.** For, as on every *Yom Tov*, one is obligated to break bread over *Lechem Mishneh* (*Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim*, sec. 529[29](#_ftn793)), and our Sages taught that on *Pesach*, one **adds** to them another broken *Matzah* because of the concept of "bread of poverty" (*Rosh*[30](#_ftn794); *Mordechai*[31](#_ftn795); *Shulchan Aruch* of *Admur HaZaken* 475, para. 3[32](#_ftn796)).

**He shall hold them in his hand and recite the blessing,** for any item over which one recites a blessing to eat, one must hold it in his right hand while blessing (*Shulchan Aruch*, sec. 206, para. 4[33](#_ftn797)). Here, some say[34](#_ftn798) that the *Hamotzi* blessing is on the whole *Matzah*, and the *Al Achilas Matzah* blessing is on the broken piece (*Rashi*, *Rashbam*[35](#_ftn799), *Rabbeinu Yitzchak*[36](#_ftn800), and *Rosh*[37](#_ftn801) in *Pesachim* 116a; this is the primary opinion[38](#_ftn802)). Others say the opposite[39](#_ftn803) (*Samag*[40](#_ftn804); *Raavan*[41](#_ftn805); *Tosafos* and other *Geonim* as written in *Hagahos Maimoniyos*, end of *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*[42](#_ftn806)). To fulfill both opinions, one holds both in his hand (*Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, sec. 475, para. 6[43](#_ftn807); see *Pri Megadim*, *Mishbetzos Zahav*, sub-para. 2[44](#_ftn808)).

**And he shall not break from them.** So that the blessing is recited over the whole *Matzah*[45](#_ftn809).

**He shall set aside the third Matzah from his hand and recite the blessing** (*Maharil*[46](#_ftn810)). Even though he intends for this blessing to also cover the eating of the third *Matzah*, as he concludes. One could say this is to show that, nevertheless, the primary connection of this blessing is to the first and second *Matzah*, even according to the opinion of *Hillel*, as mentioned above in the paragraph **Korech.** It seems more likely that it is so he does not mistakenly break from the third *Matzah* as well, just as we find that he hides the *Afikoman* for this reason (see above, paragraph **The Larger Part**[47](#_ftn811)).

**And he shall intend to also exempt the eating of the Korech**[48](#_ftn812). Therefore, one is careful in the interim not to engage in conversation that is not related to the meal (*Rabbi Yechiel*, brother of the *Baal HaTurim*[49](#_ftn813); *Ibn Yarchi*[50](#_ftn814)).

**To exempt, etc., the eating of the Afikoman**—(*Shelah*[51](#_ftn815))—this is an extra stringency (*Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, sec. 475, para. 18). Therefore, regarding this, one is not careful about interrupting with conversation—*Minhagei Beis HaRav*.

**"Al" Achilas Matzah, etc., Maror**—and not **"Le'echol"** (to eat) *Matzah*, etc., *Maror* (*Pardes* of *Rashi*[52](#_ftn816); *Ri Migash*[53](#_ftn817) in the name of *Rav Amram Gaon*; *Rambam*[54](#_ftn818), etc.), because he is also exempting others who are listening (*Hagahos Maimoniyos*, *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*, ch. 8, law 12; see *Migdal Oz*, ch. 11 of *Hilchos Berachos*, law 15[55](#_ftn819)).

**And afterwards he shall break a *kezayis* from each one**—since the eating of *Matzah* must be a *kezayis*, and there are those who say it is from the first *Matzah* and those who say it is from the second *Matzah*[56](#_ftn820), as mentioned above[57](#_ftn821).

**And he shall eat them.** And he does not dip them in salt, out of love for the *mitzvah*, so that there will be no mixture of any other taste at all (*Maharil*[58](#_ftn822); *Shulchan Aruch* of our Rebbe, sec. 475, para. 10[59](#_ftn823))—and such is the custom of *Beis HaRav* (this requires study, as the *Shelah*[60](#_ftn824) wrote that according to the writings of the *Arizal*[61](#_ftn825), one should dip in salt. Note the *Likutei Torah*, *Biur* to the *maamar* "Lo Tashbit," para. 6[62](#_ftn826)).

**Together.** Meaning, he should place them into his mouth all at once. The reason is so that there will be no interruption between the blessing of *Achilas Matzah* and the eating of the *kezayis* that pertains to it, whether it is from the first *Matzah* or the second *Matzah*, as mentioned above (*Tur Shulchan Aruch* there[63](#_ftn827)).

## Maror

**Maror.** In the present time, when there is no Korban Pesach, the obligation is only Rabbinic (Pesachim 120a). Women are also obligated in this, just as they are in the other mitzvos of this night (Shulchan Aruch HaRav, Siman 472, se'if 25).

**He dips** — and afterwards — **he recites the blessing.** This is in order to juxtapose the blessing to the performance of the mitzvah, as mentioned above in the paragraph **He dips**.

**He dips into the charoses.** Before dipping, he softens the charoses with the wine that is in the vessel (Shulchan Aruch HaRav, Siman 473, se'if 32-34). One should not submerge the entire kezayis in the charoses, because nowadays the *kapa* (a type of worm) is not commonly found in maror as it was in the days of the Gemara, and the dipping into charoses is done only as a mitzvah, in remembrance of the mortar (Pri Chadash ; Shulchan Aruch HaRav, Siman 475, se'if 11). **It is preferable** not to submerge the whole thing, so as not to nullify the taste of the bitterness ; this is a plausible explanation.

**He shakes off the charoses** (based on what is written in Pesachim 115b).

**He recites the blessing.** It requires study why he did not transcribe what is written in his Shulchan Aruch (475:18) — which is also our custom — that one must have the intention for this blessing to also exempt the maror in the korech sandwich.

**This blessing.** On the face of it, this is redundant phrasing. Perhaps it hints, by the way, that there could have been room for another blessing, namely the blessing of *al mitzvas* over the charoses (see Rambam’s Commentary on the Mishnah, Pesachim 10:3, and Lechem Mishneh on Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 7:11), even though one is not obligated in a *birchas hanehenin* for it. The reason we do not recite a blessing over its mitzvah is because it is secondary to the maror and is exempted by its blessing (Abudraham) — and necessarily, his intent is that its *mitzvah* is secondary to the mitzvah of maror. This can be explained according to what the Maharal writes, and these are his words: "Charoses is a mitzvah, because it is only mentioned regarding maror that 'they embittered their lives with hard labor,' and there is bitterness for a person in hard labor, but it is not absolute bitterness. Therefore, we make a remembrance of the mortar, and there is nothing harder than the work of mortar, as our Sages of blessed memory said in Shemos Rabbah (1:27)."

**Blessed are You, etc.... maror.** The language of the verse is *merorim* (bitter herbs) (and see Pesachim 39a). This is also the text of the blessing found in the Rambam (Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah 8:6, 8:2. And see also Yerushalmi Pesachim 9:3, 10:5. However, it appears in the singular in Rambam there, 8:4, 8:8, 8:12. See also the variations in the text of his Haggadah: "This night... maror... these bitter herbs that we eat." And it requires study).

## Korech

**Korech** (Pesachim 115a). According to Hillel the Elder: At a time when the Korban Pesach exists, one does not fulfill the obligation unless he wraps the Pesach, matzah, and maror together and eats them as one. When the Korban Pesach does not exist, one does not fulfill the obligation of matzah—which is a Torah requirement—unless he eats it **separately.** And one does not fulfill the obligation of maror—which the Sages instituted as a **remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash**—unless he wraps it with matzah and eats them together (just as was done during the time of the Beis Hamikdash). Consequently, according to Hillel, one has a **second** obligation of matzah today, by Rabbinic decree. Since the halacha was not decided according to Hillel nor according to his colleagues, one must fulfill the obligation of both (see Shulchan Aruch of Admur, Chapter 475, sections 16-18, and the Acharonim and the sefer *Shevach Pesach* for an extensive discussion).

**And he shall dip** etc., **and recline,** for we are performing a remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash according to Hillel, and he would dip and recline (Shulchan Aruch of Admur, Chapter 475, sections 19-20).

**He shall dip in charoses**—only the chazeres (lettuce), not the matzah. Those who are meticulous to avoid matzah that has become soaked do not dip it; rather, they place a small amount of dry charoses on the chazeres and shake it off afterward. This is the custom of the Rebbe’s house.

**And he shall say "This."** Seemingly, the word **"This"** is superfluous. Perhaps it emphasizes that one must use specifically this text—namely, by omitting the words **"a remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash according to Hillel"** and adding the word **"Pesach."**

**"So did Hillel..." "was existing..." "Pesach..."** This is the text in the *Siddur Kol Yaakov*. This is also stated in *Chok Yaakov*, *Be'er Heitev*, and *Mishnah Berurah* (based on the addition of the Taz). However, they omitted the first word **"was"** (*hayah*), and it is also absent from the Shulchan Aruch of Admur, Chapter 475, section 18. See further on this below.

One may explain the precision of this text: 
a) We do not say **"a remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash according to Hillel,"** just as we do not say "a remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash" when eating maror. The *Pri Megadim* (Chapter 475, *Mishbetzos Zahav*, subsection 7) explains the purpose of the statement: to inform [the participants] that it is only a remembrance **of the Beis Hamikdash**, but that **in the present time** one does not fulfill the obligation through the *korech* (wrapping). However, since we say this after having already eaten matzah and maror and having fulfilled the obligation, the *Pri Megadim’s* intent must be to warn against the mistake of thinking for the following year that the halacha follows Hillel, and mistakenly believing that Hillel holds that even now one fulfills the obligation through *korech*, while forgetting that the previous year he had already eaten matzah beforehand, and remembering only that he said "a remembrance of the Beis Hamikdash." This is a very forced explanation—especially since in *korech* there is already a degree of redundancy, as one immediately says: "So did Hillel do at the time that the **Beis Hamikdash** was..." Furthermore, one should not interrupt between the blessing and the *korech* with anything unnecessary, as mentioned above (and in *Mishnah Berurah*, Chapter 475, subsection 1, in *Biur Halacha*, he wishes to postpone the entire statement until after eating, for this very reason). This is especially true according to Admur, for whom Hillel does not fulfill the obligation of maror except through *korech*, as mentioned above.

Furthermore, by saying this, one might come to mistakenly think that the *korech* is only a remembrance. In truth, it is only now that one fulfills the obligation of maror according to Hillel, and one must fulfill the obligation according to his view as well, as mentioned above (unlike the *Pri Megadim*, who follows his own view that in the present time, even according to Hillel, one fulfills the obligation of maror when eating it separately. If so, it would indeed be only a remembrance).

b) **"That the Beis Hamikdash was existing."** This is the text in the *Siddur Kol Yaakov*, the *Hagadah* of R. Shabtai Rashkover, and *Simchas HaRegel*. However, for our Rebbe, this requires study, since in the recitation of the *Ketores* passage, his text is: "You are the One... at the time that the Beis Hamikdash **is** existing." And so it is written above: "Pesach that they were... that the Beis Hamikdash **is** existing".

However, in the *Siddur Kol Yaakov* and the *Hagadah* of R. Shabtai Rashkover, in all the aforementioned places, it says: **"was"** (*hayah*) existing. In the *Siddur Yaavetz* and the *Shelah*, the word **"was"** is absent in all three of these places.

c) **"Wrapping the Pesach, matzah, and maror"**—for according to the commentators (except for the Rambam in *Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah*, Chapter 8, Law 5, but see there in *Lechem Mishneh*), this is what Hillel did. If one omits **"Pesach,"** he would be speaking untruths in his narrative. Regarding the difficulty raised in *Chok Yaakov*—that if so, how can one say "So did Hillel do," when there is no Pesach in our hands?—it can be said that the word **"So"** (*ken*) continues to the **next part**, and it is the **beginning** of the matter: "So did Hillel do," as he will narrate. It was not necessary to conclude, "and my wrapping is a remembrance of this" (even though it is not exactly the same), because it is self-evident, and as mentioned above, we minimize speech between the blessing and the *korech*. Alternatively, one could say **"So"**—just as I am wrapping the maror and not eating it now by itself, **so** did Hillel do, to wrap the maror. This is especially true since, according to Admur, the *korech* is primarily for the sake of the obligation of eating maror.

**"Hillel did at the time that the Beis Hamikdash was existing."** All of Hillel’s days were while the Beis was existing —if so, seemingly, the words **"at the time"** etc., are superfluous. In the Shas they are absent. Perhaps it is possible to say that it is to emphasize that specifically because the Beis Hamikdash was existing, he did so; whereas in the present time, one does not fulfill the obligation of matzah through *korech*.

**"With matzos"** (Bamidbar 9:11), which is written regarding *Pesach Sheni*. He did not bring a proof from what is written regarding *Pesach Rishon* (Shemos 12:8), "And they shall eat the meat... and matzos with maror they shall eat it," because, on the contrary, from the extra word **"they shall eat it"** in that verse, we learn that one eats the Pesach even if he does not have matzah and maror (Mechilta, ibid.). Also, the literal reading of this verse implies as the Ramban writes there, that "matzos" continues from above: "And they shall eat the meat and matzos," and then He commanded, "with maror they shall eat it" [referring to] the meat. If so, there is no proof that one must wrap it with matzah as well, were it not for the fact that the verse appears regarding *Pesach Sheni*, and from there we learn for *Pesach Rishon* as well (see *Rashash* there). However, it is implied that the Rashbam reads the verse of *Pesach Rishon* in the Shas, as written in the glosses of R. B. of Ransburg.

## Shulchan Orech

**Shulchan Orech**
Our custom is to eat without *hesibah* (reclining) [1](#_ftn874). We begin—on both nights—by eating the egg (from the *ke'arah*) and dipping it in salt water [2](#_ftn875). This is in remembrance of the mourning for the destruction of the Beis Hamikdash, for when it stood, we would offer and eat the *Korban Pesach* (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, end of 476 [3](#_ftn876)).

We are extremely careful regarding *matzah sheruyah* (matzah that has come into contact with liquid) (see Responsa of the Alter Rebbe, 6 [4](#_ftn877); and see Shaarei Teshuvah, end of 460 [5](#_ftn878)). Therefore, the *matzos* on the table are kept covered, lest water fall upon them. Before pouring water, or liquids containing water, into a cup or bowl, one must check that there are no *matzah* crumbs in the vessel. Regarding the middle and final washings of the hands (*mayim emtza'im* and *mayim acharonim*), one should not pass his hands over his lips [6](#_ftn879). (This applies throughout all seven days of Pesach; however, on the last day of Pesach, **it is a hiddur** to soak [matzah] [7](#_ftn880)).

From the morning of Erev Pesach until after the *korech* of the second Seder, one should not eat any of the ingredients that go into the *charoses* [8](#_ftn881) and *maror* [9](#_ftn882).

After some time, I found [10](#_ftn883): There are those who have the custom not to eat *chazeret* (lettuce) before Pesach, and this was the custom of the Rashba (Rabbeinu Yeruchem, Netiv 5, Part 4).

It should be noted that some say that when the Beis Hamikdash stood, they would eat the entire meal (reciting *Hamotzi* over dough products, etc.) before the Seder, and only afterwards would the Seder and the eating of the *mitzvah* of *matzah*, *maror*, and *Korban Pesach* take place (Mordechai, end of Pesachim [11](#_ftn884); he disagrees on this with the Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah, Chapter 8 [12](#_ftn885)).

**One eats and drinks according to his need.** In *Pri Etz Chaim*: **"And he eats enough for his need."** It is similar in the *Tur* [13](#_ftn886) and *Mishnas Chassidim* [14](#_ftn887). In the *Siddur Yaavetz*: **"They eat and drink all that they need."** It can be said that this teaches us two things: a) That one should eat enough for his need so that he will eat the *Afikoman* while satisfied, as it is a remembrance of the *Korban Pesach*, which was eaten while one was already satisfied. b) That one should not eat more than his need, so that the eating of the *Afikoman* will not be considered *achilah gasah* (eating in a gluttonous manner) [15](#_ftn888).

## Tzafun

**Tzafun, etc. — a kezayis.** In the Shulchan Aruch[1](#_ftn889) (477:3), it is written: "Ideally, it is good to eat two kezaysim: one in memory of the Pesach offering, and one in memory of the matzah that was eaten with the Pesach offering." This is also the custom of the Rebbe’s household.

— This means there are two opinions[2](#_ftn890): whether the kezayis of the Afikoman is in memory of the Pesach offering, or in memory of the matzah that was eaten (with the Pesach offering) when one was already satiated. To fulfill both, it is good to eat two kezaysim[3](#_ftn891). [It may be said that if this is difficult for him, and he eats only one kezayis, he should make a mental stipulation that it is in memory of the opinion that is halachically accepted.] They did not, however, institute a memory for the second opinion regarding the maror eaten when satiated[4](#_ftn892), because at a time when there is no Pesach offering, the obligation of maror is only Rabbinic (see Bach, Siman 477[5](#_ftn893); Derishah and Perishah there[6](#_ftn894)).

— One should not offer the reason for this lack of an enactment, because the entire essence of maror—according to the Torah—is merely an accessory to the Pesach offering (Pesachim 90a[7](#_ftn895)) and not a mitzvah in its own right (Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah, end of Chapter 7[8](#_ftn896))—for there is an obligation for maror even when one is not obligated in the Korban Pesach (see Pesachim 91b[9](#_ftn897); Kiddushin 37b[10](#_ftn898); Tosefta Pesachim, end of Chapter 2[11](#_ftn899); Tzafnas Paneach on the Rambam there[12](#_ftn900)).

**And one should be careful not to drink after the Afikoman** (Rif[13](#_ftn901)—so it is written in Chok Yaakov[14](#_ftn902) and in the cross-references in our Rebbe’s Shulchan Aruch, Siman 478[15](#_ftn903). However, this requires further study, as written in the Pri Megadim, Eshel Avraham, beginning of Siman 478[16](#_ftn904)), so as not to nullify the taste of the Afikoman[17](#_ftn905) (this is implied by the Beis Yosef and Rema, beginning of Siman 481[18](#_ftn906)).

Seemingly, this requires study: (a) It should have warned not to **eat** after the Afikoman, especially since eating is more severe than drinking, as written in the Tur, Shulchan Aruch, and commentaries in Siman 478 and 481. (b) It should have said, "**It is forbidden to drink**," or "**One should not drink** after the Afikoman," as all the Poskim write. What is the meaning of "**And one should be careful not to drink**"? (c) This warning should have been written after the conclusion of the laws of eating the Afikoman, since its time is after that.

One may say: The meaning of "**And one should be careful not to drink**" is that he should endeavor not to become thirsty[19](#_ftn907). With this, the aforementioned points are understood: (a) It was not necessary to warn against eating, because in any case, the Afikoman must be eaten **when satiated**. (b) It does not come to teach us the law of the prohibition of eating and drinking after the Afikoman, because the place for those laws is in the Shulchan Aruch, not in the Siddur. It only teaches us the conduct of the Seder: that he should drink in such a way that he will not become thirsty afterward. (c) It is understood that the time for this caution is at the very last moment, **close to** and **before** eating the Afikoman, and therefore it is written **close to** and **before** the words "**and he should eat**." — In a slightly different way, one could say: If he does not endeavor not to become thirsty, and he craves a drink intensely, he might be permitted to rely on the opinion of some Poskim and drink other beverages (Siman 481:1, in our Rebbe’s Shulchan Aruch[20](#_ftn908)), and therefore we warn about this, which is not the case with eating.

**Not to drink**—from the fact that the statement is general, it implies: (a) That one should be careful regarding any drink, even water (R’ Shmuel of Evreux in the Mordechai, end of Perek Arvei Pesachim[21](#_ftn909)). (b) That one must also be careful on the second night (Shibolei HaLeket[22](#_ftn910)). **And this is our custom.**

**And one should be careful not to drink after the Afikoman.** According to the strict letter of the law, there is no prohibition, etc., and one who eats or drinks (on the second night) is not liable for anything, except that he is found to be **removing himself from the category of the Mehadrin (those who act with extra care)** and transgressing the words of the Sages who said, "A person should never remove himself from the community" (Admur HaZaken in Shulchan Aruch, Siman 481, end of paragraph 1—from Shibolei HaLeket[23](#_ftn911), etc.). A **clear ruling** as to how much every single person is obligated in *hiddur mitzvah* (beautifying the mitzvah), even when it is not according to the strict letter of the law and he is not liable for anything!

**And he should eat while reclining** (Rosh, Perek Arvei Pesachim, Siman 26[24](#_ftn912); and Tur, Shulchan Aruch, Siman 477:1[25](#_ftn913). This is contrary to the implication of the Rambam, Hilchos Chametz U’Matzah, Chapter 7, Halachah 6[26](#_ftn914) and Chapter 8, Halachah 5[27](#_ftn915). See Hagahos Maimoniyos there[28](#_ftn916) and the cross-references to our Rebbe’s Shulchan Aruch, 477:[4][29](#_ftn917)).

**Before midnight** (Tosafos, s.v. *Le’atuyei Megillah*, 21a[30](#_ftn918)), just like the Pesach offering, which was only eaten until midnight.

In his Shulchan Aruch[31](#_ftn919), he wrote that ideally, it is good to finish the Hallel also before midnight (Ran, end of Perek Arvei Pesachim, and end of Chapter 2 of Megillah[32](#_ftn920)). However, they are not particular about this in the Rebbe’s household[33](#_ftn921).

## Barech

**Mayim Acharonim.** The Alter Rebbe was once asked by his son (the Mitteler Rebbe): What will be the purpose of Mayim Acharonim in the time to come, when the verse "I will remove the spirit of impurity from the earth" is fulfilled? He replied: Their purpose then will relate to those who occupied themselves with worldly matters in a state of purity (Sichos Chof-Pey 5702 [1]).

**Barech [2].** All instances of *ma'ayanei* in Bircas Hamazon are pronounced with a *cholam* [3]. One holds the cup in his hand [4], raised, until the end of the blessing *Boneh V'rachamav Yerushalayim Amen* [5]. He then sets it down, and picks it up again for the blessing *Borei Pri HaGafen*. Regarding *Yaaleh V'yavo*: The one reciting the blessing raises his voice slightly when saying *Zochreinu*... *bo* (with a dagesh in the beis) *l'tovah*... *bo* (with a rafei beis) *l'vrachah*... *l'chaim tovim* [6]—and all those seated answer "Amen" after *l'tovah*, *l'vrachah*, and *tovim* [7]. The text is: **Melech** *chanun v'rachum atah* [8]. One does not answer "Amen" after *yechaserenu* [9]. Each person says "my father, my teacher, the master of this house" and "my mother, my teacher, the mistress of this house," even a guest or someone who does not have a father or mother—this is our custom throughout the entire year [10].

**Kos Shel Eliyahu** (Chok Yaakov [11]; Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, end of Siman 480 [12]). This cup is poured after Bircas Hamazon (except at certain times—Sicha of Pesach 5703 [13]). The Rebbe Rayatz was meticulous about pouring this cup himself, and from generation to generation, the Admurim have been meticulous about this.

The reason for this cup is that Eliyahu is the one who testifies that the Jewish people observe the mitzvah of milah (based on what is written in Pirkei D'Rabbi Eliezer, ch. 29 [14]), which is a prerequisite for Pesach, as it is written [15], "No uncircumcised person shall eat of it" (R' M. Chagiz in Sefer Birchas Eliyahu [16]). This can be sweetened based on what is written (Shemos Rabbah 19:5 [17]) that on this night, the Jewish people circumcised themselves [and although one only circumcises during the day—as has been questioned in many books—it can be said simply that the law that a milah performed not at its proper time must be done during the day was only established after the Giving of the Torah (Tosafos, Yevamos 72b [18])].

However, it is difficult, for according to this reason [19], this cup should be poured at the beginning of the Seder, or at the very least before eating the Afikoman, which is a remembrance of the Pesach offering.

**Mozgin Kos, etc. Shfoch, etc.** It is difficult to understand why this part of the Haggadah is not included in the *Kadesh, etc.* structure, as the *Barech* section has already concluded, and *Hallel* is written after this. In the Siddur Kol Yaakov, the sign *Hallel* is written before *Shfoch*.

**Mozgin Kos Daled.** Before reciting *Shfoch Chamascha* (Siddur Rav Amram Gaon [20]; Ran, beginning of Arvei Pesachim [21]; Rabbeinu Bachya, Parshas Va'era [22]; Abudraham [23]). The four cups correspond to the four cups of retribution that the Holy One, Blessed be He, will cause the nations of the world to drink—Yerushalmi, Pesachim, beginning of ch. 10 [24]—and we say: With this cup, we are completing the mitzvah, therefore do what You have promised us and "pour out Your wrath," etc. (ibid).

It is puzzling why they did not explain that this is based on the words of R' Levi in the Yerushalmi there [25], that the four cups correspond to the four kingdoms. If so, the fourth cup is a hint to the final exile and redemption [26], and therefore it is specifically then that we say *Shfoch* (and this resolves the difficulty of the Rashbetz [27] regarding the first reason: why do we say it specifically at the fourth cup and not the others?).

**And we open the door.** The custom of the Rebbe’s house: (When it falls on a weekday, one takes a candelabra with lit candles and) goes to open all the doors between the room where the Seder is held and the public domain (or the courtyard), and we say *Shfoch*—the shluchim say it by the door. It happened that the Rebbe himself, accompanied by one of his household members, went to open the door, and then he too said *Shfoch Chamascha*, etc., by the door [28]—upon the return of the shluchim, we begin *Lo Lanu* [29].

*Shfoch Chamascha* is recited while seated; there is no need to stand [30].

**And we open the door.** In order to remember that it is a "night of watching" (*leil shimurim*), and we fear nothing; and in the merit of this faith, Mashiach will come and the Holy One, Blessed be He, will pour out His wrath upon the nations (Shulchan Aruch of our Rebbe, ibid [31]).

**We open the door** so that Eliyahu may enter, etc., because "he has devoured Yaakov," etc., and Yaakov took from Eliyahu a pledge, the letter *vav* [32], as a sign that he will come and herald the redemption of his children (Siddur Maharid [33]).

**Shfoch, etc. Ki Achal, etc.** (Tehillim 79:6-7). **Shfoch, etc.** (ibid 69:25). **Tirdof, etc.** (Eichah 3:66). This is the version in the Siddur of the Arizal, the Shelah, and the Yaavetz (in the Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon and Zevach Pesach, there are only the two verses: *Shfoch*, etc., and *Ki Achal*, etc. In Machzor Vitry [34], several verses are added to our version).

**Shfoch, etc.**—and even though we are not worthy—**Lo Lanu Hashem Lo Lanu Ki Im L'shimcha**, etc. (Zevach Pesach [35]).

## Hallel Nirtzah

**Hallel Nirtzah.** The meaning of *Nirtzah* is: if one has performed the order in this manner, he is accepted [mirtzah] by Heaven (Shelah). It requires study, however, as the sign *Nirtzah* should logically be at the **end** of the order, not here. Rav Shabtai Rashkover and the Shelah placed it at the end.

**Hallel.** Our Rebbe the Alter Rebbe explains: *Hallel* is a term for praise, and also a term related to *beheilo neiro* (his lamp shining) (Torah Or 30a). Every time the Sages ordained the recitation of Hallel, it is a time of the revelation of the truth of Hashem from above to below—which is that what is below is called *ayin* (nothingness), and what is above is called the category of *yesh* (existence) (Likutei Torah, *D'h B'yom HaShemini Atzeres* – the third).

**Lo Lanu, etc., Ahavti, etc., Halelu, etc., Hodu, etc.** (Tehillim 115-118) – Mishnah, Pesachim (117b): The fourth [cup] completes the Hallel over it.

**Hodu LaHashem, etc.** The leader (or the Chazzan during the rest of the year) reads aloud *Hodu, etc.*, and all those reclining respond after him: *Hodu, etc., Yomar, etc.* The leader then repeats and reads: *Yomar, etc.*, and they respond after him: *Hodu, etc., Yomru, etc.* And so too for *Yomru*.

**Ana Hashem** (four times).

– After the verses *Yomru, etc.* – one says: *Hodu, etc., Ki l'olam chasdo*.

**Yehalelucha, etc., Hodu LaHashem, etc., Nishmas, etc., Uvchen, Yishtabach, etc., Yachid Chai HaOlamim.** Mishnah and Gemara there. The various opinions regarding the interpretation of the Gemara and the text of the Haggadah are collected in the Pri Megadim, Eshel Avraham, end of Siman 480. Our Rebbe’s text is as written by the Rosh and the Tur (based on the commentary of the Taz, end of Siman 486), and so wrote the Levush in Siman 486, the Chok Yaakov and the Barei Heitev in Siman 480, the Siddur Yaavetz, and the Chayei Adam.

**Yehalelucha.** Mentioned in the Gemara there.

**Hashem Elokeinu Kol Maasecha.** Not: *Al kol maasecha* (as per the emendation of the Tzemach Tzedek on his Siddur)—and this is how it appears in the Siddur of Rav Amram Gaon, Rav Saadia Gaon, Rambam, Hilchos Megillah v'Chanukah, chapter 3, halacha 10, the Abudraham, the Siddur Yaavetz, and others. This is based on the verse (Tehillim 145:10): "All Your works shall praise You, Hashem" (Abudraham).

**Ki L'olam Chasdo.** One must separate the words *Ki l'olam chasdo* (Yerushalmi, Berachos, chapter 2, halacha 4) so that it is not heard as *l'olam* (as one word), and also [to pause before] the *samech* of *chasdo* (Rashi in the Rif there).

**Hodu LaHashem, etc.** (Tehillim 136) – This is called Hallel HaGadol.

– (Ordinary Hallel is called Hallel HaMitzri – Berachos 56a) –

– And why? Because it is written in it, "He gives bread to all flesh," which is a great matter (Pesachim 118a ; see Berachos 4b). It contains 26 verses of thanksgiving to the Almighty, corresponding to the numerical value of the Ineffable Name, etc. It is possible that for this reason it is called in the words of our Sages "Hallel HaGadol," because "Hashem is great and exceedingly praised." Our Sages provided another reason, as we read in the chapter *Arvei Pesachim*, etc. (Abudraham) – and see regarding this in the Siddur of the Shelah (Shacharis for Shabbos). He concludes there that it is mentioned in the words of the Mekubalim that this is the Hallel HaGadol of the ministering angels.

From *Hodu* until *Vayotzei Yisrael*, one should concentrate on the *yud* of the name *Havayah*, and from the verse *Vayotzei Yisrael* until *HaMolich*, on the *hei*, etc. (Pri Etz Chaim, Shaar HaShabbos, chapter 19). It seems this *kavanah* is intended for the masses, as our Rebbe included the letters *yud*, *hei*, etc., opposite the aforementioned verses (in the Shabbos prayer), even though he generally did not write *kavanos* in his Siddur, as it is appropriate for every soul.

**Nishmas Kol Chai** (mentioned in Pesachim there). Some say that Shimon ben Shetach composed it and hinted at his name in reverse: *Shochen ad* (the **Sh**), *Mi yidmeh* (the **M**), *Ad henah* (the **A**), *Ve'ilu pinu* (the **V**), *Nishmas* (the **N**) (commentary on the Siddur Kol Bo) – and see Zohar, Vol. II (138a) and the Abudraham.

**Those who are accustomed to say piyutim.** In the Rebbe’s house, it is not the custom to say them.

**Those who are accustomed to say piyutim should not interrupt between them, etc.** Because they are merely a custom (Rema, Bach and Taz, Siman 480; Siddur HaAriZal, Yaavetz), whereas the ordinance of the Sages was to complete the Hallel and the *Berachas HaShir* over the fourth cup (Shulchan Aruch of the Alter Rebbe, Siman 480, paragraph 3).

**Rather, immediately afterward he should recite the blessing.** Seemingly, this is redundant. It can be said that it emphasizes the reason: why one should not interrupt, because one must recite the blessing **immediately**, as that was the ordinance of the Sages to complete, etc., as mentioned above, and the piyutim are not part of the ordinance.

**And afterward he shall say "L'shanah"** – only once (Siddur HaAriZal, Yaavetz. Not as written in the Siddur of the Shelah – three times).

The custom of the Rebbe’s house: After saying *L'shanah haba'ah b'Yerushalayim*, the Admur returns and pours the wine from the cup of Eliyahu back into the bottle, and all those reclining sing during this time the *nigun* for the verse *E-li atah v'odecha* – one of the ten *nigunim* of the Alter Rebbe.

The Rebbe—the Alter Rebbe—did not include the words "Chasal Siddur Pesach" after the Haggadah, because in Chabad, Pesach does not end; rather, it is drawn out continuously.

In truth, the illumination of all the festivals shines every day, but the festival of Pesach is drawn out continuously (Sichos of the festival of Pesach, 5703, from the Rebbe Rayatz).
