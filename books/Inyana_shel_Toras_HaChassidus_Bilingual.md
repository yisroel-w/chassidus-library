=====================

ב"ה.
ענינה של תורת החסידות[ftnref\_1\_\*]
**א.** בנוגע לענינה של תורת החסידות[ftnref\_2\_1] – והחידוש שבה על חלקי התורה שנתגלו לפניה – נאמרו ביאורים רבים. ומהם:
א) בזמנו של הבעש"ט היה העולם במצב של התעלפות, וע"י גילוי הבעש"ט ותורת החסידות, נתעורר העולם[ftnref\_200\_\*1] מהתעלפותו[ftnref\_3\_2].
ב) "חסיד הוא שעושה לפנים משורת הדין"[ftnref\_4\_3] (וכמרז"ל[ftnref\_5\_4] "שורפן חסיד – אע"ג שמזיק לו").
ג) "מעלת החסידות הוא שהכחות הטבעים נעשים כחות אלקים"[ftnref\_4\_3]. וכמאמר רבינו הזקן בעל התניא והשו"ע "כל ענין החסידות הוא לשנות טבע מדותיו"[ftnref\_6\_5]: לא רק לשנות מדותיו הטבעים אלא גם – "טבע מדותיו"[ftnref\_7\_6].
ד) תורת החסידות חידשה אשר כל אחד ואחד, גם מי שאין לו נשמה גבוהה וגם לא זיכך את עצמו, יוכל להשיג אלקות[ftnref\_8\_7]. כי ע"י זה שתורת החסידות ביארה את הענינים של חלק ה"סוד" שבתורה וקירבתם אל השכל בדוגמאות ומשלים מכחות ותכונות הנפש – "מבשרי אחזה אלוקה"[ftnref\_9\_8] – ניתנה האפשרות לכאו"א להשיג גם חלק זה שבתורה; ולא רק בהשכל שבנפשו האלקית, אלא גם בהשכל שבנפשו השכלית עד – בהשכל שבנפשו הבהמית.

## 1

The Essence of the Teachings of Chassidus

B"H.
The Essence of the Teachings of Chassidus

**1.** Regarding the essence of the teachings of Chassidus—and the innovation it brings to the parts of Torah revealed before it—many explanations have been offered. Among them:

a) In the time of the Baal Shem Tov, the world was in a state of fainting, and through the revelation of the Baal Shem Tov and the teachings of Chassidus, the world was awakened from its faint.

b) "A Chassid is one who acts beyond the letter of the law" (as our Sages of blessed memory said, "A Chassid burns it—even though it causes him a loss").

c) "The virtue of Chassidus is that natural powers become Divine powers". As our Rebbe, the author of the Tanya and the Shulchan Aruch, stated, "The entire matter of Chassidus is to change the nature of one's character traits": not merely to change one's natural character traits, but rather—"the nature of one's character traits".

d) The teachings of Chassidus introduced the innovation that every single person—even one who does not possess a lofty neshamah and has not refined himself—can grasp Elokus. Because by explaining the matters of the "secret" part of Torah and bringing them closer to the intellect through examples and analogies from the powers and attributes of the soul—"from my flesh I perceive Elokah"—the possibility was granted to every person to grasp even this part of Torah; and not only through the intellect of his nefesh Elokis, but also through the intellect of his nefesh sichlis, and even—through the intellect of his nefesh habehamis.

**B.** It is understood and simple, as follows: that Toras HaChassidus is a single point that is not composed of various disparate details. Since all the virtues mentioned in the aforementioned explanations (and in other explanations) are distinct virtues, it is therefore necessary that these virtues do not define or exhaust the *essence* of Toras HaChassidus. The essence of Chassidus is an essential point, abstracted from specific details; rather, it is from this essential point that the aforementioned virtues are found and branch out.

The essential point of Chassidus is—as discussed above in the maamar, which is fundamentally a maamar of the Rebbe (Rashab) —the drawing down of a new light from the level of the *pnimiyus* of Keter, and even higher: the drawing down of the level of the *pnimiyus* of Atik itself, the level of Ein Sof that is found in *Radla* (Reisha D'Lo Isyada).

This matter itself (as stated in the maamar) necessitates that all the virtues of Chassidus explained in various places are merely branches of its essential point. For since Chassidus is the drawing down of the level of Ein Sof, it is understood and simple that "Ein Sof" is the essence, and all other matters are merely existences and consequences derived from it.

**C.** What was stated above regarding Toras HaChassidus applies *in general* to all parts of the Torah: The Torah includes all types of perfection and virtues found in the world [which are divided in general—regarding the matters of the "small world," which is man—into the virtues of the *mochin* and the virtues of the *midos*:

Regarding moral conduct and good character traits: the *mishpatim* and paths of *mussar* in the Torah (such as in Maseches Avos and the like) are the ultimate good and truth. All the systems of *mussar* that human beings have invented from their own hearts are mixtures of good and evil, truth and falsehood. And the portions of good that exist in each of those systems have their source in the Torah.

## 3

(As is known from the story of the Rebbe Rayatz, during one of his travels, several people were arguing and expressing different opinions regarding the Torah’s attitude toward various political systems and which one it aligns with, with each of them citing a source in the Torah for their position. When they asked for the Rebbe Rayatz’s opinion on the matter, he replied: "Because the Torah is the ultimate truth and goodness, it contains all the good elements found in every system.")

Regarding wisdom—the Torah is the most sublime wisdom, as it is written, "For it is your wisdom and your understanding in the eyes of the nations."

Not only that, but the entire flow of vitality into the world (even in the higher worlds) depends on (even the slightest detail of) the Torah.

However, this is not the essence of the Torah. The essence of the Torah is that it is "completely unified with the Ohr Ein Sof, which is clothed within it in a state of absolute unity." Therefore, since all the worlds are as nothing in relation to the Ein Sof, so too are they in relation to the Torah. For this reason, "it is not to be praised at all with the praise of the vitality of all the worlds, since they are considered as nothing". Rather, from the perspective of its essential point—that it is unified with the Ohr Ein Sof—it naturally includes all the various virtues and perfections in the world, and through it comes the flow of vitality, and so on.

Indeed, although the essential point of *all* parts of the Torah is that they are unified with the Ohr Ein Sof, nevertheless, this point is expressed primarily in the teachings of Chassidus (as stated in the maamar regarding "Chassidus," that it is (specifically) the drawing down of the Ein Sof that is found in *Reisha D'lo Yada*).

For in all parts of the Torah, the aspect of the "Ein Sof" within them is clothed in some form—a form that defines and "expresses" the essence of that part. This form of that specific part of Torah [Peshat, Remez, Drush, Sod] conceals the simplicity of the Ein Sof clothed within it—a concealment of the garment that, as it were, cannot be exchanged. In contrast, the teachings of Chassidus, since they are not defined by any specific form, and the "forms" through which the subject matter of Chassidus is revealed are merely garments that can be changed and *will be changed* (which is why it includes all four parts of *Pardes* in the Torah and gives them life, as will be explained below), the form does not truly conceal the simplicity of the Ein Sof within it.

10. The concept of "Modeh Ani" according to the *peshat* (literal) dimension of Torah is an expression of gratitude to Hashem for "returning my soul to me." Although we recite the *bracha* of "Elokai Neshamah" to acknowledge the return of the soul, we must also recite "Modeh Ani," because the obligation to express gratitude for the soul's return arises the moment one awakens from sleep. Just as the obligation to recite a *bracha* on physical pleasure arises the moment one experiences the pleasure (and even before one actually enjoys it), so too, the obligation to recite a *bracha* of gratitude for the return of the soul—which is the greatest pleasure of all, encompassing all individual pleasures in the world—arises the moment one awakens from sleep.

Since the *bracha* of "Elokai Neshamah" includes the mention of Hashem’s Name, it cannot be recited (at that moment) before washing one's hands. Therefore, one must express gratitude for the return of the soul immediately upon waking by reciting at least "Modeh Ani." (Nevertheless, one must still recite the "Elokai Neshamah" *bracha* afterward, because—in addition to the fact that "Elokai Neshamah" contains details not found in "Modeh Ani"—"Modeh Ani" lacks the mention of Hashem’s Name, and any *bracha* that does not include the Name and Kingship of Hashem is not considered a *bracha*.)

Based on this, we can understand why, in the time of the Gemara, they did not recite "Modeh Ani" and only recited "Elokai Neshamah," for "Modeh Ani" adds nothing new to the "Elokai Neshamah" *bracha* (and, as mentioned, is actually lesser). The reason we are required to recite "Modeh Ani" nowadays is solely so that the expression of gratitude occurs immediately upon waking; in their time, since they recited "Elokai Neshamah" the moment they awoke, there was no need to recite "Modeh Ani."

The concept of "Modeh Ani" according to the *remez* (allusive) dimension of Torah: The return of the soul each morning *alludes* to the resurrection of the dead. Sleep is one-sixtieth of death, and consequently, the return of the soul is a microcosm of the resurrection of the dead. This is the meaning of "You have returned my soul to me... great is Your faithfulness": from the fact that "You have returned my soul to me," we know that "great is Your faithfulness" regarding the resurrection of the dead.

The explanation according to the Torah of Chassidus regarding *Modeh Ani*, etc., is as follows: The beginning of the daily order is with *Modeh Ani*, which is recited before *netilas yadayim*, even with impure hands, because all the impurities in the world cannot defile the *Modeh Ani* of a Yid. It is possible that there may be a deficiency in this matter or that matter—but his *Modeh Ani* always remains in its completeness.

This concept is specifically due to the *Yechidah*: Regarding the four levels of *Nefesh, Ruach, Neshamah, Chayah*, the concept of a blemish or even impurity, etc., is applicable. And according to the principle that "their beginning is embedded in their end", the more Chassidus is drawn down into lower levels—to the point of transforming the nature of the animal soul, the "outer" aspect within a person, and lower still, the refinement of one's portion in the world, the "outer" aspect outside of the person—the more its essential nature is expressed.

Therefore, the Rebbe Rayatz emphasized in many of his sichos that every concept one learns in Chassidus must be brought into actual practice, for it is precisely through actual avodah that one grasps the essence of Chassidus.

**19.** Just as the level of "Ein Sof" in Chassidus is *generally* present in all parts of the Torah (as mentioned in section 3), so too, the expression of the Ein Sof by drawing it down to the lowest level—to transform even it and make it a vessel for Elokus—is also present in the Torah as a whole (though the primary focus of this is in Chassidus).

This is what our Sages meant when they said, "I created the yetzer hara, and I created for it the Torah as an antidote". For on the face of it, this is puzzling: Of all the worlds, this world is the lowest, "than which there is no lower". And within this world itself—the yetzer hara is something regarding which the Ribono shel Olam regrets having created. And since all the worlds, even the higher worlds, are considered as nothing in comparison to the Torah (and therefore, when David praised the Torah by saying that all the upper and lower worlds depend on the precision of a single mitzvah of Torah, he was punished for it), then surely, a fortiori, it would seem there is no place to say that the creation of the Torah was in order for it to be an antidote for the yetzer hara?

## 20

Based on the above, we can understand: the very essence of Torah, which is unified with the *Atzmus* of Hashem, is expressed precisely through the fact that it is a "spice" for the *yetzer hara*. This is because the power to "spice" (refine) the *yetzer hara* and transform it into *tov* comes specifically from the *Atzmus* of Hashem.

The explanation for this is as follows: All revelations, even the most sublime, are defined by the parameters of *ohr* and revelation. Therefore, the reality of *ra*—which is the opposite of *ohr*—stands in opposition to them. Consequently, they lack the ability to transform it into *tov* (they can only fight it, or at most, cause it to be nullified entirely). Only the *Atzmus* of Hashem, which is simple with the ultimate simplicity and is removed from all limitations—and to which no opposition can possibly exist—has the ability to change it and transform it into *tov*.

This is also the meaning of what is said regarding the aspect of Torah that is unified with the *Atzmus* of Hashem: "She was *mesachekes* (playing/rejoicing) before Him." The *sechok* (rejoicing/taanug) that is Above is brought about through the clarification and rectification of *choshech* —the transformation of darkness into light. And this is "she was *mesachekes* before Him": specifically through the aspect of transforming darkness into light, the Torah "plays"—meaning, its inwardness is expressed as it is unified with the inwardness of *Ohr Ein Sof*—"before Him."

**20.** These two matters in *Toras HaChassidus* (and similarly in the Torah as a whole)—(a) its penetration into all parts of the *nefesh, ruach, neshamah, chayah, yechidah*, to the point that it acts even within the *nefesh habehamis* and the world, and (b) the fact that the expression of its essence is specifically through its action within the *nefesh habehamis* and the world—have their parallels (as do all matters in *Chassidus*) in the *nigleh* (the revealed dimension of Torah) and in *halacha*. Furthermore, this *halacha* in the *nigleh* is augmented in its explanation by the aforementioned matters in *Chassidus*:

"A person’s four cubits acquire for him in any place" : in an alleyway, or on the sides of the public domain.

This means that the teachings of Chassidus were clothed in the garments of understanding and comprehension (nourishment) of the human intellect, and from that time on, it has been spreading in a manner of "increasing and brightening, increasing and brightening."

Seemingly, this is perplexing:
After all, the final generations are the time of the "footsteps of Moshiach, when the Sukkah of Dovid has fallen to the level of the feet and heels," and it is an "orphaned generation," for the darkness within it is doubled and redoubled—so how is it possible that specifically these generations should merit such lofty revelations that previous generations did not merit, not even the generations of the Tannaim and Amoraim, the Geonim and the Rishonim, etc.?

However, we find a similar matter regarding the Torah in general:
Our Sages said, "Everything that a veteran student is destined to innovate in the Torah was already given to Moshe at Sinai." On the face of it, this too is perplexing: Since the innovations of the student were already given to Moshe at Sinai, why were they not revealed in all those generations, until this veteran student came and innovated this matter?

The explanation is:
It is written in the Torah, "He has made everything beautiful in its time." Our Sages said that Adam HaRishon was worthy of having the Torah given through him, etc., but the Holy One, Blessed be He, said, etc., "I will give it to his children"—meaning that every thing that the Holy One, Blessed be He, does at a specific time, that is the "beautiful" time, the time that is appropriate and necessary for this thing. From this it is understood that until the time arrives when this matter in Torah is innovated by the veteran student, the world was not yet in need of this matter, and therefore it was not revealed.

For Torah is from the word *hora'ah* (instruction), whose purpose is to instruct the Children of Israel in the path they should walk and the avodah they should perform to refine and purify the world—the purpose of the descent into this world. And since the world was not yet in need of the clarification provided by this instruction that comes through the matter innovated by the student, the innovation was not *revealed*. And when the time arrives in which the veteran student...

**English:**

## 27

The very fact that a student is able to innovate in Torah is proof that this is the "appropriate" time for that innovation; this is because the state of the world is such that the instruction derived from the student’s innovation is necessary and essential.

The same applies to our current situation: all the revelations of the future, at the coming of Moshiach, are "dependent upon our deeds and our avodah throughout the duration of the golus". This does not refer only to the avodah of this final golus. Rather, since our Sages have said that "a mitzvah is called only by the name of the one who completes it," it is understood that with the completion of the avodah and the birurim of this golus, the purpose and essence of all the exiles will also be completed and concluded (for the redemption from this golus is a redemption after which there is no further golus). It is understood from this that our avodah, which brings about the end of the fourth golus and the subsequent redemption, also effects an elevation in the three previous redemptions.

The conclusion to be drawn from this is that precisely because these final generations are the "ikvesa d'meshicha" — the time of the clarification and conclusion of the fourth and final golus — a greater responsibility is placed upon them, and their avodah must be more elevated, performed with greater strength and intensity than that of the previous generations and exiles. For our avodah is the final preparation for the complete redemption: to transform the double and redoubled darkness into double and redoubled light.

And since "the Holy One, blessed be He, does not act tyrannically with His creatures", and only demands according to the strengths He has granted them, it is precisely in these generations that the hitherto hidden and inner parts of Torah are revealed (which earlier generations did not merit). Through these revelations, and by means of them, special strengths are bestowed from above for this elevated avodah—for the Torah is "oz v'tushiya" ; it is what provides the strength to accomplish great things in one's avodah.

Furthermore: in addition to the fact that this great avodah, in and of itself, requires special strengths...

**English:**

## 28

It is well known that the more essential the avodah is, and the closer it brings us to the desired ultimate goal, the more the kelipah and the yetzer hara intensify their opposition to hinder it. Since the avodah of the *final* generations is directly related to the completion and perfection of all the birurim, the opposition of the yetzer hara against it is with greater force and intensity—seeking to prevent the worker from fulfilling his duty. Consequently, for this reason as well, these generations require special strengths, so that through our avodah we can fulfill and complete the will of Hashem, for "Hashem desired to have a dwelling place in the lower worlds".

**2.** Based on the above, it can be said that just as this applies generally to the difference between the final generations and the generations that preceded them, the same is true within the final generations themselves. In every generation that draws closer to the coming of Moshiach, just as the responsibility grows, so too does the revelation of the lofty matters of Torah increase compared to the generations that preceded it. We see that this is indeed the case regarding the revelation of pnimiyus haTorah—from generation to generation it is *revealed* more, and descends and is enclothed more in the garments of understanding, comprehension, and grasp.

A revelation with such great addition is particularly noticeable and prominent in the maamarim of the Rebbe (Rashab), in which the concepts of Chassidus are presented with broader explanation and with greater intellectual understanding and grasp than they are (in a revealed manner—even for people of our level) in the maamarim of our Rebbes who preceded him.

It is well known the saying of the early Chassidim that the Rebbe (Rashab) is the "Rambam of the Torah of Chassidus," because the concepts of the Torah of Chassidus were presented in his maamarim...

He is the One who gives us the strength to end the Galus and to refine the "small vessels."
